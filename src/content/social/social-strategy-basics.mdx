# Social Strategy Basics

Most small brands fail at social not because their content is bad  -  they just picked the wrong room. A B2B SaaS posting Reels for Gen Z, or a Gen Z streetwear brand grinding on LinkedIn, will burn months for nothing. This lesson is for marketers building their first real social plan, and the core idea is simple: go where your audience already is, then commit.

## What It Actually Is

A social strategy is a written answer to four questions: who are we talking to, where do they hang out, what will we post, and how will we measure it. Everything else  -  content calendars, brand voice, influencer deals  -  is just execution.

Concrete example: Duolingo's social team picked TikTok in 2021 because their target users (Gen Z language learners) lived there. They built one chaotic mascot character and committed. That focus, not the volume of posts, is what took them past 17 million TikTok followers by 2025.

## Why It Matters (with data)

About 5.66 billion people use social media as of October 2025, roughly 69% of the planet, according to [DataReportal's Global Digital Insights](https://datareportal.com/social-media-users). But "social media" is not one channel  -  usage splits sharply by age and intent:

- YouTube is the most-used platform among US adults, with 84% watching videos there, per [Pew Research's 2024 social media fact sheet](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/social-media/).
- Among US teens, YouTube hits 90%, TikTok 63%, Instagram 61%, and Snapchat 55% (Pew, 2024).
- Globally, Facebook still reaches 56.3% of internet users aged 16+, narrowly ahead of YouTube (55.3%) and Instagram (54.6%), per [Sprout Social's 2026 demographics report](https://sproutsocial.com/insights/new-social-media-demographics/).
- Facebook skews older in the US (peak usage among 30–49 year olds) but younger globally (peak 25–34).

Translation: picking a platform without checking who lives there is like opening a vegan restaurant in a steakhouse district. The audience math beats the creative.

## How It Works / The Playbook

A simple five-step starter strategy:

1. **Define one audience.** Write a single sentence: "30–45-year-old finance directors at mid-market US companies." If you cannot name them, you cannot find them.
2. **Match age and intent to platform.** Under-25 lifestyle and entertainment → TikTok, Instagram, YouTube Shorts. 30+ B2B and professional → LinkedIn, YouTube. Broad consumer 30–55 → Facebook and Instagram. Visual products → Instagram and Pinterest. Long-form education → YouTube.
3. **Pick one or two platforms  -  not five.** Most under-resourced teams that spread across 4+ channels post inconsistently on all of them. Choose the top one based on audience overlap, plus an optional second for repurposing.
4. **Set one primary goal per channel.** Awareness, traffic, leads, or sales. One platform, one job. Mixing goals dilutes the content.
5. **Define a posting cadence you can sustain for 12 weeks.** Three good posts a week beat ten panicked ones followed by silence.

<Callout type="example">
Gymshark grew to a $1.4B valuation largely by ignoring Facebook ads early on and going deep on Instagram and YouTube, where their 18–25 fitness audience lived. They partnered with micro-athletes instead of celebrities, then layered TikTok on top once it matured. As of 2024 the brand has over 7 million Instagram followers and 5.8 million on TikTok  -  built from a focused two-platform bet, not a five-platform spray. ## Common Mistakes

- **Choosing platforms based on what you personally use.** Your habits are not your customer's habits.
- **Being on every platform "just in case."** Five inconsistent channels lose to one strong one. Sprout Social and others repeatedly find that focus and posting cadence predict growth better than channel count.
- **Confusing reach with relevance.** Facebook has 3 billion users, but if your buyer is a 22-year-old streetwear fan, that number means nothing to you.
- **Copy-pasting the same post everywhere.** A LinkedIn post and a TikTok require different formats, hooks, and lengths.
- **No measurement plan.** If you cannot say what success looks like in 90 days, you are doing content, not strategy.

## Key Takeaways

- Start with the audience, not the platform. Define one person before you open Canva.
- Use real 2024–2025 platform demographics (Pew, Sprout Social, DataReportal) to match where they actually spend time.
- Two platforms done well will beat five done badly, every quarter, every year.
