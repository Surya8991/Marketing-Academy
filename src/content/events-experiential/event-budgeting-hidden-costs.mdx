You quote your CFO $10,000 for a trade show booth. The final invoice reads $30,000. This isn't a rare horror story, it's close to the median outcome. In 2025, 71% of exhibitors reported their actual show costs exceeded their pre-show budget, with a typical overage of 34% (industry benchmarking data, 2025).

The gap almost never comes from the booth space itself. It comes from a handful of line items that don't show up until the vendor invoice does, and by then it's too late to negotiate. This lesson is about finding them first.

<Callout type="info">
This lesson covers the cost side of event budgeting, the hidden fees that inflate your spend. If you're looking for how to measure what an event returned in pipeline or revenue, that's covered in the separate event-budgeting-roi lesson.

## Drayage: the single biggest hidden cost

If you've never budgeted for a trade show before, drayage is the fee most likely to blindside you. It's the charge for moving your booth materials from the loading dock to your actual booth space, and back out again, and it's billed by weight, not by convenience.

Drayage rates run $100 to $180 per hundredweight at major venues, and can climb to $200-$400 per hundredweight in expensive markets like New York or Chicago. For a standard 20x20 custom exhibit weighing 5,000-8,000 pounds, that's $5,000 to $16,000 in drayage alone, on a single show.

Industry estimates put drayage at 30-40% of total trade show expenses once everything is tallied. It's rarely the line item anyone remembers when they're pricing out "the booth."

<Callout type="warning">
Drayage is billed on the weight and timing of your shipment, not a flat fee. Off-target shipments, ones that miss the "advance warehouse" window and arrive direct to show site, often get charged a materially higher rate. Confirm the shipping deadline with your show's exhibitor kit early.
</Callout>

## AV markups nobody warns you about

Bringing your own AV gear to most convention centers isn't optional, it's against the venue contract, and the in-house AV provider knows it. That's how a $200 projector rental at a big-box store becomes a $1,800 line item at a convention center.

Common AV markup traps include per-outlet electricity fees, mandatory union labor for setup and teardown even for a simple screen, and rush charges if you request equipment changes once you're on-site. None of these are negotiable once the show floor opens, so the only leverage you have is asking for the full AV price list before you sign anything.

## The line items everyone forgets to budget

Beyond drayage and AV, a predictable set of costs shows up on nearly every event invoice and catches first-time exhibitors off guard.

- **Shipping and storage** — outbound shipping, plus storage fees if your booth materials sit at a warehouse between shows
- **Electricity and internet** — often $300-$1,000+ per outlet or connection, priced separately from your booth space
- **Cleaning and carpet** — daily booth cleaning and carpet rental are frequently billed as add-ons, not included
- **Staff travel and lodging** — flights, hotel nights during a peak-demand week, and per diems for everyone staffing the booth
- **Labor for install/teardown** — many venues require union labor, and hours outside the standard window can double the rate

Lost staff productivity is the quietest cost of all: the days your best salespeople spend traveling and staffing a booth are days they're not closing other deals, and almost nobody puts a number on that in the budget.

## A checklist that survives contact with the vendor invoice

Build your budget in two passes. First, list every cost category above with a placeholder estimate. Second, go back through the show's official exhibitor kit and confirm real prices for each one, before you commit to a booth size or design.

- Pull the exhibitor service kit before finalizing booth design, it lists every mandatory fee (drayage, electricity, labor rates) in writing
- Confirm the advance-warehouse shipping deadline to avoid direct-to-show-site drayage surcharges
- Get the full AV price list, not a verbal quote, before choosing your AV package
- Add a 20-30% contingency line on top of your itemized total, matching the real-world overage rate exhibitors report
- Track staff travel and lodging separately from booth costs, so the true per-show cost is visible to whoever approves next year's budget

<Callout type="tip">
Keep a running "actuals vs. budget" sheet after every show. The gap between what you planned and what you paid is the single best predictor for next year's line items, better than any vendor's estimate.
</Callout>

Total trade show spend for a mid-size booth commonly lands between $10,000 and $30,000 once every category is counted (Cvent, 2025). Knowing that range before you start planning is what turns a budget conversation with finance from a surprise into a plan.
