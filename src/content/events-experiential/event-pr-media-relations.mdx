# Event PR: Getting Press to Show Up and Stay in the Story

You booked the venue, built the stage, and sent 200 press invites. Twelve journalists RSVP'd, and three showed up. Getting media to your event is a different skill than running the event itself.

## Quick Summary

- 72% of journalists say press releases are the most useful resource a PR team can give them, but 86% say they immediately reject pitches that don't match their beat or audience.
- An embargo (an agreement letting reporters see your news early, in exchange for not publishing until a set date and time) exists to synchronize coverage, not to create false urgency.
- Embargoes work for real announcements, movie premieres, book launches, or major product reveals, but misusing them on routine updates burns trust with reporters fast.
- A press kit needs to answer a reporter's questions before they have to email you, or you lose them to a competing story with a faster kit.
- Event coverage fades in days unless you deliberately extend the narrative with follow-up angles reporters can use later.

## Getting Journalists to Actually Attend

The hardest part of event PR isn't writing the pitch, it's targeting it. A generic invite list guarantees a low turnout, because most journalists get dozens of these a week and filter hard.

[86% of journalists say they immediately reject pitches that don't match their beat or audience](https://www.contentgrip.com/embargoed-press-release/), which means your invite list needs to be built from people who've actually covered your category recently, not a purchased media database. Pull the last 10 articles a reporter wrote before you pitch them; if none touch your space, skip them.

Give reporters and analysts a reason to physically attend beyond the news itself: exclusive interview slots with your executives, an early briefing the day before, or first access to data or a demo nobody else gets. A press release they can read from their desk competes with your travel-and-attend ask, and the desk usually wins unless you offer something the release can't.

<Callout type="info">
An analyst is different from a journalist: analysts (from firms like Gartner or Forrester) evaluate your product or market position for paying clients, while journalists write for a general audience. Both need separate outreach, since analysts care about competitive positioning and data, journalists care about the story.
</Callout>

Once you've got the right people committed to attend, the press kit determines whether they leave with a usable story or an unusable pile of assets.

## What Actually Belongs in a Press Kit

A press kit's job is to remove every reason a reporter has to email you back with a question. Every missing piece is a delay, and delays kill same-day coverage.

**The news itself, written as a release, not a brochure.** Lead with what's actually new in one sentence, then back it up with the "so what" for their readers, not for your investors.

**High-resolution photos and B-roll video**, pre-cleared for press use with no watermarks or usage restrictions attached. A journalist on deadline will skip a story entirely before they'll chase down usable art.

**Executive bios and headshots**, spokesperson contact info, and 2-3 pre-approved quotes they can use directly without needing a live interview. This matters most for wire and trade coverage, which often runs same-day off the kit alone.

<Callout type="tip">
Build the press kit as a single shareable link (a folder or a page), not an email attachment. Reporters lose attachments; they don't lose a bookmarked URL, and you can update it live if a detail changes after distribution.
</Callout>

A complete kit is what separates same-day coverage from a "we'll get to it next week" that never comes.

## Embargoes: Timing Coverage, Not Manufacturing Hype

An embargo lets you brief reporters on your news early, in exchange for an agreed publish time, so multiple outlets go live at once instead of trickling out over days.

[Embargoes are beneficial for events like conferences, movie premieres, and book launches](https://leveluppr.io/understanding-embargoes-public-relations-strategy/), because they let reporters write detailed, well-researched stories instead of rushing a thin one to be first. The payoff for you is a wave of simultaneous coverage right as the event starts, which reads as bigger news than the same stories trickling in over a week.

Use embargoes selectively. [Embargoes should not be used for routine updates, thin product announcements, or stories that only matter internally](https://leveluppr.io/understanding-embargoes-public-relations-strategy/), because reporters remember who wasted their early access, and that memory costs you cooperation on the next real announcement.

## Making Coverage Outlast the Event

Most event coverage is written the day of and forgotten within 72 hours, unless you give reporters new angles to return to.

Pitch follow-up stories that don't depend on the event still being "news": a data trend you can share two weeks later, a customer case study that ties back to what launched, or an executive available for a deeper interview once the initial rush has passed. This is what separates one news cycle from a narrative that compounds over a quarter.

## Key Takeaways

- Target journalists based on their last 10 articles, not a purchased media list; 86% reject pitches outside their beat immediately.
- Give press a reason to physically attend, like exclusive interviews or early briefings, since a desk-readable release competes with the travel ask.
- Build the press kit as a single shareable link with pre-cleared photos, bios, and quotes, so reporters never have to email you back with a question.
- Use embargoes for real announcements only; misusing them on routine news burns trust reporters remember on your next pitch.
- Plan follow-up story angles before the event ends, so coverage compounds into a narrative instead of fading in 72 hours.
