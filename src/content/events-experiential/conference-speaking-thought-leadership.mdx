# Getting Your Executives on Stage: CFPs and Thought Leadership

Your CEO wants to speak at the industry's biggest conference. The talk you submit will most likely get rejected, unless you understand what conference organizers are actually screening for.

## Quick Summary

- Most CFPs (call-for-papers, the formal process conferences use to collect and review talk submissions) reject the majority of submissions; it's common for only 1 in 30 proposals to get accepted at a competitive event.
- The single most common rejection reason is that the talk reads like a vendor pitch or product demo instead of genuine insight.
- Reviewers look for three things in order: a clear problem, specific takeaways, and a credible reason you're the one to present it.
- Smaller, niche conferences have meaningfully higher acceptance rates and often better-matched audiences than the flagship industry event everyone targets first.
- One accepted talk can be repurposed into 6-10 pieces of content: blog posts, social clips, a podcast episode, and sales enablement material.

## What a CFP Review Committee Actually Does

A call-for-papers is the formal process conferences run to collect talk submissions from potential speakers, usually reviewed by a committee of program organizers, past speakers, or industry volunteers. Understanding how they read submissions changes what you write.

Reviewers see hundreds of proposals for maybe 20-40 speaking slots. They are not reading for polish, they are pattern-matching against two questions: is this going to teach the audience something, and is this secretly an ad.

[The most common reason proposals get rejected is that they read like a vendor pitch or product demo](https://dev.to/github/top-tips-for-getting-a-cfp-accepted-hh0), according to speakers who've sat on review committees. Conference organizers protect their audience fiercely, because an audience that feels sold to stops trusting the entire program, not just your session.

<Callout type="warning">
If you want to give a straightforward sales pitch, most conferences have a paid sponsorship track for exactly that. Submitting a thinly-veiled product demo to the open CFP track is the fastest way to get your executive rejected, and it can hurt your standing for future submissions too.
</Callout>

Knowing the committee's actual filter is half the battle. The other half is writing a proposal that passes it.

## Writing a Pitch That Beats the Sales Pitch

A strong CFP submission follows a predictable shape, and skipping any piece of it weakens the whole thing.

**Lead with the problem, not the solution.** Open by naming a pain point your audience actually has, framed in their language, before you ever mention what you did about it. This signals you understand their world instead of just wanting airtime.

**Be specific about the takeaway, not the topic.** "Marketing analytics trends" is a topic. "Three metrics that predicted a 40% churn spike before our dashboard did" is a takeaway, and it's the version that gets accepted, because [reviewers want the abstract to speak specifically to what participants will learn](https://www.civo.com/blog/everything-you-need-to-know-about-cfps), not a vague theme.

**Explain, briefly, why you're the right presenter.** One or two sentences on your specific experience with this exact problem, not a full bio. The committee wants a credible narrator, not a resume.

Keep the whole abstract to a single tight paragraph, roughly 3-6 sentences. A CFP reviewer scanning 200 submissions rewards the one that respects their time.

## Picking the Right Conference, and Stretching the Talk

Don't aim your executive's first submission at the biggest event in the category. Aim for fit and odds first, prestige second.

**Smaller and niche conferences have real advantages.** They typically have higher acceptance rates, more supportive audiences for a first-time speaker, and organizers who are more willing to coach a rough submission into a strong one. Build a track record there before chasing the flagship stage.

**One accepted talk is a content engine, not a one-time event.** Break the 30-minute talk into a blog post expanding the core argument, 3-5 short video clips of the best moments, a LinkedIn post from your speaker with the key insight, and sales enablement slides using the same framework. That's a quarter of content from one prep cycle.

<Callout type="tip">
Record the talk yourself even if the conference does, and get the slides in a shareable format before you walk on stage. The content plan should be locked before the CFP is even accepted, so you're not scrambling to repurpose it after the fact.
</Callout>

A good conference talk isn't the finish line, it's raw material. Treat the acceptance as the start of a content calendar, not the end of a project.

## Key Takeaways

- CFP reviewers reject sales pitches disguised as talks more than anything else; save the product demo for the paid sponsorship track.
- A strong submission leads with the audience's problem, states a specific takeaway, and briefly establishes why your speaker is credible.
- Acceptance rates can be as low as 1 in 30 at flagship events, so smaller niche conferences are often the smarter first target.
- Keep the abstract to one tight paragraph; reviewers are scanning hundreds of submissions and reward specificity over length.
- Plan the content repurposing (blog, clips, social, sales enablement) before the talk happens, not after, so one session becomes a quarter's worth of material.
