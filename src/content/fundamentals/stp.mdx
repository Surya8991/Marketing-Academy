# Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning

## What It Is

Not everyone is your customer. That sounds obvious, but most early-stage marketers behave as if the whole world is their audience — writing ads for everyone, building landing pages that try to say everything, and wondering why nothing converts. STP is the framework that fixes this. It stands for Segmentation, Targeting, and Positioning, and it's how every serious marketer decides who to talk to and what to say.

Segmentation is the act of dividing a broad market into smaller groups of people who share something in common — a problem, a behavior, a life stage, a budget. Targeting is choosing which of those groups you're actually going to pursue. And positioning is crafting a message that resonates specifically with that group, in a way that sets you apart from competitors. The three steps run in order: you can't target without segmenting, and you can't position without knowing who you're talking to.

The reason STP matters more than ever is that attention is fragmented. Generic messages get ignored. A 2023 McKinsey study found that 71% of consumers expect personalized communication, and 76% get frustrated when they don't receive it. STP is the strategic foundation that makes personalization possible — not at the level of a single email, but at the level of your entire go-to-market strategy.

## Real-World Example

Dollar Shave Club launched in 2012 targeting a very specific segment: men aged 18–34 who were frustrated with overpriced razors at the drugstore. They didn't try to win the premium end (that was Gillette's Fusion) or the disposable end (Bic had that locked). They targeted the annoyed middle — guys who thought they were being ripped off and would switch if someone made it easy.

<Callout type="example" title="Dollar Shave Club's STP in Action">
Dollar Shave Club segmented the razor market by attitude toward price, not just demographics. They targeted men who felt the category was broken. Their positioning — "Our blades are f***ing great" at $1/month — was designed entirely for that segment's cynicism. The launch video cost $4,500 to produce and drove 12,000 orders in 48 hours. By 2016, Dollar Shave Club held 15% of the US cartridge razor market by dollar share and sold to Unilever for $1 billion.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

STP isn't just strategic theory — it's a practical tool you reach for constantly.

- **When launching a product**, STP forces you to define exactly who you're building for before you spend money on ads or content.
- **When a campaign isn't converting**, revisiting your targeting often reveals you've been speaking to the wrong segment or using positioning that doesn't land with your actual audience.
- **When entering a competitive market**, STP helps you find the underserved pocket — the segment competitors are ignoring or serving poorly.
- **When scaling**, STP tells you which segments to expand into next, and in what order, so you don't grow in all directions at once and dilute your brand.

## How It Works

Start with segmentation. There are four main ways to slice a market: demographic (age, income, gender, job title), geographic (country, city, urban vs. rural), psychographic (values, lifestyle, personality), and behavioral (purchase frequency, loyalty, usage patterns). The best segmentations usually combine two or three of these dimensions. A software company might segment by company size (demographic) and by whether the team already uses competing tools (behavioral). Each combination creates a distinct group with different needs and different willingness to pay.

Once you have your segments mapped out, targeting asks you to pick one — or at most two or three — to go after first. The criteria are simple: Is this segment large enough to be worth pursuing? Can you reach them cost-effectively? Do you have a genuine advantage over competitors in serving them? And is the segment growing or shrinking? You're looking for the intersection of "segment we can win" and "segment worth winning."

Positioning is where the strategy becomes creative. Your positioning statement is an internal document that answers: for whom, against what alternatives, and why you're better. From that statement flows your messaging, your creative direction, your pricing logic, and your channel choices. Everything in your marketing should be traceable back to a positioning decision, which itself traces back to a targeting decision, which traces back to a segmentation choice.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Targeting everyone.** "Our product is for anyone who needs X" is not a target — it's an abdication of strategy. When you aim at everyone, your message resonates with no one. The market leader can afford to be broad. You can't. Pick a segment you can actually dominate, even if it feels uncomfortably small at first.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Confusing segmentation with personas.** Segments are groups defined by shared characteristics. Personas are fictional individuals who represent those segments. Both are useful, but doing personas without first doing rigorous segmentation means you're building characters without data. Start with the segment, then build the persona to represent it.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Pro tip.** The best segments are defined by a shared pain, not a shared demographic. "Women aged 25–40" is a demographic slice. "First-time managers who feel underprepared and don't want to show it" is a pain-based segment. The second group has a shared urgency that makes them far easier to reach with a single message — because they all know exactly what problem you're solving.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**You don't win by reaching more people — you win by being the obvious choice for the right people.**
