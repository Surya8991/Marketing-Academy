# What Marketing Actually Is

## What It Is

Marketing is the work of making people aware that something exists, understanding why they should care, and helping them decide it's worth their time or money. That's it. Not ads. Not logos. Not social media posts. Those are tactics — marketing is the thinking that decides whether those tactics are even worth doing.

The most durable definition comes from Philip Kotler, widely considered the father of modern marketing: marketing is "the process by which companies create value for customers and build strong customer relationships in order to capture value from customers in return." Strip away the academic language and you get something simpler — marketing is figuring out what people need, building something that delivers it, and then communicating that clearly enough that the right people choose you over everyone else.

Where most people get confused is in conflating marketing with advertising. Advertising is one output of marketing — a paid message placed in front of an audience. Marketing is the upstream thinking that determines who that audience is, what message will land, what problem your product solves, and whether the price makes sense. A product that solves a real problem for the right people at the right price, communicated honestly, is doing better marketing than a slick ad campaign built on top of a product nobody wants.

## Real-World Example

Airbnb grew without a traditional advertising budget for most of its early years. What they did instead was pure marketing: they identified a real problem (hotels are expensive, impersonal, and often unavailable during peak travel), found a target audience (budget-conscious travelers and people traveling to cities with sold-out hotels), built a product that solved it, and crafted a message around belonging rather than mere lodging. Their tagline "Belong Anywhere" is marketing strategy made visible — it repositions a transaction (renting a room) as an emotional experience.

<Callout type="example" title="Airbnb's Marketing-First Growth">
When Airbnb launched in 2008, they had almost no ad spend. By focusing on the underserved problem of affordable, local accommodation — and investing in professional photography for host listings — they saw bookings increase by 2-3x in markets where they introduced the photography program. By 2011 they had 1 million nights booked. By 2023, the platform had hosted over 1.5 billion guest arrivals across 220+ countries. The product and the message were aligned from day one. That alignment is marketing.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Marketing shapes every interaction between a business and the world — long before an ad is written or a campaign is launched.

- When you're launching a product and need to decide who it's actually for and what to say to them
- When sales are flat and you need to diagnose whether the problem is awareness, messaging, pricing, or the product itself
- When you're entering a new market and need to understand what already exists and how to position against it
- When a competitor copies your features and you need to articulate why customers should still choose you

Understanding what marketing actually is — rather than what it looks like — gives you the ability to make better decisions at every one of these moments.

## How It Works

Marketing works as a loop, not a funnel. It starts with understanding the customer: who they are, what problem they have, what they've already tried, and what a better outcome looks like for them. That understanding shapes product decisions, pricing, distribution, and eventually communication.

Once a message reaches an audience, something happens — people buy, or they don't. They refer others, or they don't. They churn, or they don't. All of that behavior feeds back into the understanding of the customer, which refines the product and the message. The companies that treat marketing as a one-way broadcast — we make, we push, we sell — tend to fall behind the ones that treat it as a continuous learning system.

This is why the best marketers sit close to product, customer success, and data. Marketing isn't a department that runs campaigns in isolation. It's the function that translates between "what we built" and "what the world needs to hear in order to trust it."

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Confusing marketing with promotion.** Many founders and early-stage teams think "we just need more marketing" when they mean "we need more ads." If the product doesn't solve a real problem, or the pricing is wrong, or you're reaching the wrong people — promotion makes the problem worse, not better. More eyeballs on a broken offer is more evidence that the offer is broken.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Starting with the channel, not the customer.** "We should be on TikTok" or "we need a newsletter" are channel-first decisions. Marketing strategy starts with the customer — who they are, where they already spend time, what they respond to — and then selects channels accordingly. Choosing channels before understanding your audience means spending budget to reach the wrong people in the right places.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Pro tip.** The most effective marketers spend more time talking to customers than they do in tools and dashboards. Before writing any copy, running any test, or launching any campaign, do five customer interviews. Ask people who already bought why they bought, what almost stopped them, and how they describe the problem you solve in their own words. Their language will outperform anything you write from scratch.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Marketing is the work of understanding what people need and connecting that need to something real — everything else is execution.**
