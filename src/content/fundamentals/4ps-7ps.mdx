# The 4 Ps (and the extra 3)

## What It Is

Every business decision about how to bring something to market eventually comes down to a handful of questions: What are you selling? What does it cost? Where can people get it? And how will they find out about it? These four questions map directly to the four Ps of marketing — Product, Price, Place, and Promotion. Together they form what marketers call the "marketing mix," a framework that helps you think through all the moving parts of getting something into the hands of the right customers.

The 4 Ps were introduced by E. Jerome McCarthy in 1960 and later popularized by Philip Kotler in his foundational marketing textbooks. The framework gives marketers a structured way to audit their strategy: not just "are we running enough ads?" but "does the price signal match the product's positioning, and is the product available where the target customer actually shops?" Each P influences the others, so a change in one ripples through the rest.

In the 1980s, researchers Bernard Booms and Mary Bitner argued that for service businesses — hotels, banks, software companies, healthcare providers — four Ps weren't enough. They added three more: People (the humans delivering the service), Process (how the service is delivered), and Physical Evidence (the tangible cues that signal quality when the product itself is intangible). The expanded 7 Ps framework is now the standard for any business where service delivery is part of the offer.

## Real-World Example

Apple's product launches are textbook 7 Ps execution — every element of the mix is deliberate and reinforcing.

<Callout type="example" title="Apple iPhone Marketing Mix">
When Apple launched the iPhone in 2007, the product (a touchscreen smartphone with no physical keyboard) was genuinely different. The price ($499 with a 2-year AT&T contract) was premium by market standards, deliberately signaling that this was not a commodity device. Place was constrained — available only through Apple Stores and AT&T, creating scarcity and exclusivity. Promotion was a single Steve Jobs keynote that generated 300,000+ pre-orders before a single ad ran. People (Apple Store "Geniuses") and Physical Evidence (the retail store environment, the white packaging, the unboxing experience) made the intangible promise feel tangible. The result: 1 million iPhones sold in 74 days, and a product category Apple still dominates nearly two decades later.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

The 7 Ps framework is useful whenever you need a structured way to diagnose or plan a marketing strategy. Reach for it:

- When launching a new product or service and you need to make sure no variable is being left to chance
- When diagnosing why sales are underperforming — often the problem isn't the promotion, it's the price or the channel (Place)
- When entering a new market segment where customer expectations around People and Process may differ from your current base
- When briefing an agency or new team member, because it gives everyone a shared vocabulary for what's in scope

## How It Works

Start with Product — not just what the thing is, but what job it does for the customer. A product has features (what it does), benefits (what that means for the user), and positioning (how it compares to alternatives). Price is not just the number on the tag; it's a signal of value. Charge too little and customers assume low quality. Charge too much without the right brand equity and they go elsewhere. Pricing strategy options include cost-plus, value-based, competitive, and penetration pricing.

Place is about distribution: where and how customers access your product. A DTC (direct-to-consumer) brand selling only through its own website has a very different Place strategy than a CPG brand stocked in 30,000 grocery stores. Promotion covers everything that drives awareness, interest, and conversion — advertising, content marketing, PR, social media, email, sales promotions, and partnerships. The key is that promotion should be the amplifier of a strong Product/Price/Place strategy, not the fix for a weak one.

For service businesses, the extra three Ps fill the gaps. People refers to anyone who interacts with a customer — a rude support rep can undo months of brand advertising. Process covers how the service is delivered: a smooth onboarding flow is a Process win; a checkout that requires three phone calls is a Process failure. Physical Evidence is the visible proof of quality when you can't touch the product — think the cleanliness of a hotel lobby, the design of a SaaS dashboard, or the feel of the packaging a subscription box arrives in.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Treating promotion as the only lever.** Most marketing problems get diagnosed as "we need more ads." But if the product is wrong for the market, the price is out of step with the value, or the product is sold in the wrong channel, more promotion just accelerates learning that something is broken. Audit all four (or seven) Ps before increasing ad spend.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Setting price based on costs alone, not customer value.** Cost-plus pricing (add a margin to what it costs to make) feels logical but ignores what the customer is actually willing to pay. A B2B software tool that saves a company $500,000 per year in labor can charge far more than its development cost would suggest — because the price should reflect value delivered, not cost incurred.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Pro tip.** The 7 Ps are most powerful when you use them to find the *constraint* — the one P that is holding back the others. Strong product, fair price, decent promotion, but sales are flat? Map your customer's actual purchase journey and you'll often find the friction is in Place: the product isn't where the customer goes to buy this category of thing. Fix the constraint first; optimizing the other six Ps around a bottleneck rarely helps.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**The marketing mix is a diagnostic tool first and a planning tool second — figure out which P is broken before you spend money on the ones that are working.**
