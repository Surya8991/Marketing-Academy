# Brand vs. Performance Marketing

## What It Is

Every dollar a company spends on marketing is doing one of two jobs. The first job is building a feeling — making sure that when someone eventually needs what you sell, your name is the one that comes to mind. The second job is converting that feeling into a purchase right now, today, this click. The first job is brand marketing. The second is performance marketing. Most companies are bad at one or the other, and the ones that win long-term are unusually good at both.

Brand marketing is slow, diffuse, and hard to measure. You run a TV spot, sponsor a podcast, or publish a book, and you can't draw a straight line from that spend to a sale. What you're really doing is lowering the cost of every future sale by making strangers already trust you before they've heard your pitch. Performance marketing is the opposite — paid search, retargeting ads, email sequences, affiliate programs. Every campaign has a clear input-output relationship: you spend $X and generate $Y in revenue, and the tracking tells you exactly where things fell apart.

The tension between these two modes has defined marketing strategy debates for decades. The famous "60/40 rule," popularized by researchers Les Binet and Peter Field in their analysis of the IPA Effectiveness Databank (covering hundreds of award-winning campaigns), argues that brands should allocate roughly 60% of their budget to long-term brand building and 40% to short-term sales activation. That ratio isn't arbitrary — it reflects what the data showed actually produced sustained profit growth versus short-term revenue spikes that fade.

## Real-World Example

Nike has one of the most studied brand-to-performance ratios in marketing. The company consistently spends the majority of its marketing budget on brand — athlete sponsorships, the "Just Do It" narrative, culturally resonant campaigns like Colin Kaepernick in 2018 — while layering performance tactics on top through its Nike Training Club app, direct-to-consumer digital channels, and retargeting.

<Callout type="example" title="Nike's Brand-First Strategy">
When Nike launched its Kaepernick "Believe in Something" campaign in September 2018, the immediate online reaction was polarized. But within 3 weeks, Nike's stock hit an all-time high, online sales jumped 31% in the days following launch, and the brand's overall valuation increased by approximately $6 billion. The campaign did zero direct response — no discount code, no click-to-buy. It was pure brand. The performance numbers followed because the brand trust was already there.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Understanding where you sit on the brand-performance spectrum is one of the most consequential strategic decisions a marketer makes. It's not just about ad creative — it shapes your entire budget, team structure, and timeline for results.

- When you're a new brand with no awareness, performance-heavy spending burns money because strangers don't convert well — you need brand investment first to warm the market
- When your category is high-consideration (B2B software, financial products, healthcare), brand trust is the actual purchase trigger, not the last-click ad
- When you're scaling a direct-to-consumer product and need to hit quarterly targets, performance channels give you a controllable lever that brand work can't match on short timelines
- When your cost-per-acquisition is rising year over year in paid channels, that's often a brand signal — you've saturated the already-aware audience and need to invest upstream

## How It Works

Brand and performance marketing work through fundamentally different mechanisms, and mixing them up is the source of most strategic mistakes. Brand marketing works by building mental availability — the probability that your brand comes to mind in a buying situation. This is measured over years, not weeks. Performance marketing works by capturing demand that already exists, pushing prospects who are already in-market over the line. It is measured in days and weeks.

The practical implication is that these two modes require different creative, different channels, and different success metrics. Brand work lives in broad-reach environments: broadcast TV, YouTube pre-roll, out-of-home, podcast sponsorships, content publishing. Its KPIs are brand awareness lift, aided and unaided recall, share of voice, and net promoter score. Performance work lives in intent-rich environments: Google Search, shopping feeds, retargeting networks, email automation, conversion-optimized landing pages. Its KPIs are CPA, ROAS, conversion rate, and LTV.

The 60/40 split is a starting point, not a law. B2B SaaS companies with 90-day sales cycles often need to skew further toward brand because the purchase committee needs sustained exposure before a demo request makes sense. Early-stage startups with no runway might run 90% performance while they find product-market fit. The key insight from Binet and Field's research is that companies systematically under-invest in brand because performance marketing is easier to justify in a quarterly review — and that under-investment compounds over time into weaker pricing power, higher CAC, and brand commoditization.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Treating brand and performance as an either/or choice.** Startups often go 100% performance because it's measurable, then wonder why their CAC keeps climbing. Mature brands sometimes go so heavy on brand that they lose quarterly revenue. The insight from decades of effectiveness research is that the two modes are multiplicative, not additive — brand makes performance campaigns work better because your name already means something when the ad appears.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Applying performance metrics to brand campaigns.** If you run a brand awareness video on YouTube and kill it because the conversion rate is 0.2%, you've made a measurement error, not a media buy error. Brand campaigns should be judged on reach, frequency, brand recall, and sentiment change — measured through brand lift studies, not last-click attribution. Forcing brand spend through a performance dashboard creates pressure to make brand work look like performance work, which destroys the creative quality that makes brand campaigns effective.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Run brand and performance simultaneously, even at small budgets.** You don't need a Nike-scale brand budget to apply the principle. Even a 70/30 performance-to-brand split with a small brand budget — a podcast sponsorship, a content series, a community sponsorship — starts building mental availability in your category. The compounding effect of brand investment means that $5K/month in brand now is worth far more than $60K in brand spend two years from now when you're trying to fix a commoditization problem.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Performance marketing fills your pipeline today; brand marketing makes that pipeline cheaper and more durable every year you invest in it.**
