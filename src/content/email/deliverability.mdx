# Deliverability: SPF, DKIM, DMARC

You can A/B test subject lines all day, but if Gmail silently drops your campaign at the SMTP layer, none of that matters. Since February 2024, the three letters SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are the difference between a 40% open rate and a 0% delivery rate. This lesson is for marketers who own (or share blame for) a sending domain.

## What It Actually Is

Three DNS records that prove an email is really from you.

- **SPF (Sender Policy Framework)** is a TXT record listing the IPs and services allowed to send mail from your domain. Example: `v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com include:sendgrid.net ~all`.
- **DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)** attaches a cryptographic signature to every outgoing message. The receiver fetches your public key from DNS and verifies nothing was tampered with in transit.
- **DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance)** is the policy layer. It tells receivers what to do when SPF or DKIM fails (`p=none`, `p=quarantine`, or `p=reject`) and where to send forensic reports.

Concrete example: a phisher sends a fake invoice "from" billing@yourbrand.com. Their server is not in your SPF record, and they cannot sign with your private DKIM key. DMARC at `p=reject` tells Gmail to bounce it before the user ever sees the inbox.

## Why It Matters (with data)

The rules changed in February 2024. Google and Yahoo now require any domain sending 5,000+ messages per day to Gmail or Yahoo addresses to have SPF, DKIM, and a published DMARC record (even at `p=none`). Once classified as a bulk sender, that label is permanent, even if your volume drops. From November 2025, Gmail tightened enforcement so non-compliant mail faces temporary or permanent rejections, per [Security Boulevard's 2025 update](https://securityboulevard.com/2025/11/google-and-yahoo-updated-email-authentication-requirements-for-2025/).

Microsoft joined the party on May 5, 2025, applying the same 5,000-message threshold to outlook.com, hotmail.com, and live.com mailboxes, per [Valimail](https://www.valimail.com/blog/new-email-sender-requirements-for-dmarc-spf-and-dkim-at-google-and-yahoo/). PCI DSS v4.0, effective March 31, 2025, also mandates DMARC at `p=quarantine` or `p=reject` for any business that touches card data, with non-compliance fines of $5,000 to $100,000 per month.

Adoption is still embarrassing. DMARC coverage among top domains grew from 27.2% to 47.7% between 2023 and 2025, a 75% surge, but only 11.2% of monitored domains run a full `p=reject` policy at 100% enforcement, per the [EasyDMARC 2025 Adoption Report](https://easydmarc.com/blog/ebook/easydmarc-dmarc-adoption-report-2025/). That gap is your competitive moat: most senders are still misconfigured.

## How It Works / The Playbook

1. **Inventory every sender.** List every service that sends mail "from" your domain: Google Workspace, Marketo, SendGrid, Zendesk, Stripe receipts, your billing system, your CEO's Calendly. Missing one is the #1 cause of broken SPF.
2. **Publish SPF.** One TXT record per domain. Stay under the 10 DNS lookup limit (use SPF flattening if needed). End with `~all` (softfail) while testing, then `-all` (hardfail) in production.
3. **Enable DKIM on every sender.** Each platform gives you a public key and a selector (e.g. `s1._domainkey.yourbrand.com`). Use a 2048-bit key. Rotate annually.
4. **Start DMARC at `p=none` with reporting.** Example: `v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourbrand.com; pct=100;`. Pipe `rua` reports into a tool like dmarcian, Valimail, or Postmark.
5. **Read the reports for 2 to 4 weeks.** Find every legitimate sender that fails alignment. Fix them.
6. **Ramp enforcement.** Move to `p=quarantine; pct=10`, then 25, 50, 100. Then `p=reject`. Most teams take 60 to 90 days.
7. **Monitor Gmail Postmaster Tools.** Keep spam complaint rate below 0.10% (hard ceiling 0.30%).

## Common Mistakes

- **Multiple SPF records on the same domain.** RFC 7208 says only one is allowed. A second record causes a `permerror` and silent failure.
- **Forgetting subdomains.** Mail from `marketing.yourbrand.com` needs its own SPF/DKIM. DMARC's `sp=` tag controls the subdomain policy separately.
- **Going straight to `p=reject` with no monitoring.** You will block your own invoices, your HR portal, and your founder's newsletter. Always start at `p=none` with `rua` reporting.
- **CNAMEs that decay.** ESPs rotate DKIM keys. If you copy-pasted a TXT instead of using the vendor's CNAME, your signatures eventually break.
- **Ignoring BIMI and brand alignment.** Once at `p=quarantine` or stronger, you unlock BIMI logos in Gmail and Apple Mail, which lift open rates 10% in tested deployments.

## Key Takeaways

- SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are now table stakes, not optional. Gmail, Yahoo, and Microsoft all enforce them at 5,000 messages per day.
- The goal is `p=reject` with 100% coverage. Only 11.2% of domains have made it there. Get there in 60 to 90 days via staged rollout.
- Read your DMARC aggregate reports. They are the only honest source of truth about who is actually sending as you.
