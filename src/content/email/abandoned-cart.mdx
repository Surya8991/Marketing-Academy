# Abandoned Cart and Browse Flows

If you sell anything online and you only have time to build one automation, build this one. Abandoned cart and browse abandonment flows recover revenue you have already earned the attention for, and they consistently outperform every other email a brand sends. This lesson is for ecommerce marketers, founders, and lifecycle specialists who already have basic email set up and want the flow that pays for the rest.

## What It Actually Is

An abandoned cart flow is a series of automated emails (and often SMS) triggered when a shopper adds a product to cart, reaches checkout, or initiates payment, and then leaves without buying. A browse abandonment flow triggers earlier in the funnel: a known subscriber views a product page but never adds to cart.

Concrete example: a logged-in customer at Allbirds views the Tree Runner page, leaves, and 90 minutes later gets an email with the exact shoe, a size guide link, and recent reviews. No discount. That is browse abandonment. If they had added it to cart and bailed at shipping, they would enter the cart flow instead.

## Why It Matters (with data)

The numbers are not subtle. The average ecommerce cart abandonment rate sits around 70 percent, and [Baymard Institute estimates roughly $260 billion in US and EU revenue is recoverable](https://www.emailvendorselection.com/cart-abandonment-rate-statistics/) through better checkout and recovery flows.

Recovery emails themselves are the highest-converting message type in the stack:

- Abandoned cart emails average a **41 percent open rate** and a **10.7 percent conversion rate**, per [2025 industry data compiled by Email Vendor Selection](https://www.emailvendorselection.com/cart-abandonment-rate-statistics/).
- [Klaviyo benchmark data](https://www.klaviyo.com/blog/abandoned-cart-benchmarks) shows automated abandoned cart flows earn a **3.55 percent placed order rate** per recipient, far above any campaign send.
- A Klaviyo analysis found that **three-email sequences generated $24.9 million versus $3.8 million from single-email flows**, a 6.5x difference from adding two more sends.
- Browse abandonment flows convert at **0.96 percent**, which sounds small until you realize that is [9.6x the average marketing email](https://www.klaviyo.com/blog/browse-abandonment-email), and the audience is much larger than your cart abandoners.

Most brands recover only 3 to 5 percent of carts. Top operators hit 10 to 14 percent. The gap is almost entirely flow design.

## How It Works / The Playbook

Build the cart flow first, browse second.

**Cart flow (3 emails plus optional SMS):**

1. **Email 1, sent 1 hour after abandonment.** Subject: a reminder, not a sale. Show the exact product, the cart total, and a one-click return-to-cart link. No discount yet. Goal: handle the genuine "got distracted" cohort, which is the largest segment.
2. **Email 2, sent 22 to 24 hours later.** Add social proof: reviews of the abandoned item, UGC, or a trust badge (returns, shipping speed). Address objections in the copy. Still no discount.
3. **Email 3, sent 48 to 72 hours after abandonment.** Now you can introduce urgency or a small incentive (free shipping, 10 percent off, or a low-stock note). Reserve discounts for this last touch only, or your loyal buyers will train themselves to abandon.
4. **Optional SMS at hour 4 or 5.** SMS abandoned cart messages routinely double the recovery rate of email-only flows for opted-in subscribers.

**Browse flow (2 to 3 emails):**

1. Trigger when a known profile views a product two or more times, or views any product and does not add to cart within a session.
2. First send within 2 to 4 hours, while intent is fresh. Show the product, related items, and reviews. Treat it as helpful, not pushy.
3. Suppress anyone who entered the cart flow so people do not get both.

<Callout type="example">
Dollar Shave Club rebuilt its abandoned cart sequence into a three-email flow with sharper subject lines and added a 12-hour SMS touchpoint. Per Klaviyo case data, similar flow rebuilds have driven 6.5x revenue lifts compared to single-email recovery. The pattern is consistent: a three-message sequence with one well-timed SMS is the floor, not the ceiling.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

- **Discounting in email one.** You train your best customers to abandon on purpose. Hold the discount for message three, or never.
- **Sending the same generic copy regardless of cart value.** A $40 cart and a $400 cart need different objection-handling. Split the flow by cart value over $150 or so.
- **Forgetting to suppress.** Buyers who completed checkout, unsubscribed profiles, and people already in the welcome series should be excluded.
- **Ignoring browse abandonment entirely.** Cart abandoners are a tiny slice of intent. Browse abandoners are 5 to 10x larger and still convert at nearly 10x campaign rates.
- **No SMS channel.** In 2025 the recovery flows that hit 10 percent plus all use SMS as the second or third touch.

## Key Takeaways

- A three-email cart flow plus one SMS is the baseline. Single-email flows leave roughly 85 percent of recoverable revenue on the table.
- Lead with reminder and social proof. Save discounts for the final touch so you do not erode margin or train abandonment.
- Add browse abandonment once cart is live. The audience is bigger and the flow takes a day to build.
