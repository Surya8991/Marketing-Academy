# Email Copywriting

Email copywriting is the craft of writing words that make people open, read, and click your emails. It covers four parts: the subject line (the headline readers see in their inbox), preview text (the short snippet shown next to the subject), the body (the main message), and the call to action or CTA (the one thing you want readers to do). Get all four right and you turn a list of addresses into a channel that generates real revenue.

## Quick Summary

- Subject lines and preview text decide whether your email gets opened. Treat them as a unit, not separate fields.
- Short, specific, and personal subject lines outperform clever or generic ones in almost every A/B test.
- One email should have one job. Every sentence should push the reader toward one call to action.
- Plain-language body copy almost always beats flashy design: readers scan, not read.
- Personalized CTAs (calls to action) convert 202% better than generic ones. Small copy changes move big numbers.

## Why Email Copywriting Matters

Email is the highest-ROI (return on investment) marketing channel. For every $1 spent, email returns an average of $36 (Litmus, 2024). That ROI only materializes when people open your email and act on it.

Here is the hard truth: most subscribers will not open your email. The average open rate across industries was 26.6% in 2024 (Snov.io). Of those who open, only 1.22% click. Copywriting is the lever that moves those numbers.

Unlike a website where someone might browse for minutes, an email reader makes a decision in under two seconds: open or delete. Your subject line is the only thing standing between your message and the trash folder.

<Callout type="info">
Welcome emails are an exception to average benchmarks. They generate 4x as many opens and 10x as many clicks as standard broadcast emails (Snov.io, 2024). This is why your very first email to a new subscriber deserves your best copywriting effort.
</Callout>

## The Four Layers of an Email

Every marketing email has the same four layers. Each layer has one job.

### Layer 1, The Subject Line

The subject line is the most important sentence you will write. It has one job: earn the open. Nothing else matters if the email goes unread.

What works, based on aggregated A/B test data from Mailchimp (2024) and Snov.io (2024):

- **Specificity beats cleverness.** "3 ways to reduce cart abandonment" outperforms "You need to see this" every single time.
- **Personalization lifts open rates significantly.** Using a subscriber's first name or referencing their recent behavior (a product they viewed, a course they enrolled in) signals relevance immediately. Personalized email campaigns receive 29% higher open rates and 41% higher click-through rates compared to generic ones.
- **Optimal length: 61 to 70 characters** gets the best open rates (43.38% average, Snov.io 2024). On mobile specifically, keep it under 32 characters, which is where iPhone cuts off subject lines.
- **Lowercase or sentence case often beats title case.** It looks more like a message from a person and less like a mass promotion.
- **Numbers and brackets work.** "5 subject line mistakes (and how to fix them)" is more clickable than "Subject line best practices."

<Callout type="warning">
Avoid spam trigger words in subject lines. Words like "FREE", "GUARANTEED", "ACT NOW", and excessive punctuation (!!!) train spam filters to route your email to the junk folder. ALL-CAPS subject lines also score low with human readers: they read as shouting, not urgency. A single spam complaint from 0.1% of your list can damage your sender reputation with Gmail and Outlook.
</Callout>

### Layer 2, Preview Text

Preview text (also called preheader text) is the short line that appears after the subject line in most email clients. Most marketers leave it blank. When you do that, the email client auto-fills it with the first line of the email body, which is often "View in browser" or an image alt tag.

Treat preview text as a second subject line. It extends your hook:

- Subject: "Your cart is waiting"
- Preview: "You left 2 items behind, here is a 10% discount to finish checking out."

Those two lines together do the heavy lifting before a single word of body copy is read. Safe length is 85 to 100 characters. Anything longer gets cut off on most clients.

### Layer 3, The Opening Line

Your opening line has one job: confirm to the reader that opening this email was the right decision. Do not waste it on "Hi [First Name], I hope this email finds you well." Start with the most relevant or interesting thing you have to say.

Compare these two openings for a promotional email about an SEO course:

- Weak: "We are excited to share some news about our latest product offering."
- Strong: "Google updated its ranking algorithm 8 times in 2024. Here is what changed and what to do about it."

The second opening delivers value immediately. It gives the reader a reason to keep reading.

### Layer 4, Body Copy

Email body copy should follow the AIDA structure (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action):

- **Attention:** your subject and opening line (already covered above)
- **Interest:** one clear problem or situation the reader recognizes as their own
- **Desire:** proof that your solution works: a stat, a short story, or a testimonial
- **Action:** one button or link asking for one specific thing

Keep paragraphs to two or three sentences. Use bullet points for lists of three or more items. Avoid large blocks of text because most readers scan before they read. Dense paragraphs signal effort and people skip them.

<Callout type="example">
**Real example: HubSpot's re-engagement email campaign (2024)**

HubSpot ran A/B tests on their re-engagement emails (emails sent to users who had stopped opening for 90+ days). Version A used a designed template with their brand header, three bullet points listing product features, and two CTA buttons ("Explore features" and "View pricing"). Version B was a plain-text email from a named sales rep with one sentence: "Haven't heard from you in a while, is there anything we can help you figure out?" with a single reply link. Version B achieved a 37% higher reply rate. The lesson: conversational, human copy outperforms polished promotional design when you are trying to re-engage cold contacts.
</Callout>

## The Call to Action

One email, one call to action. Every extra button or link you add reduces the chance that readers click any of them. This is called "decision paralysis": when given too many options, people choose nothing.

Rules for an effective CTA button:

- **Action verbs only.** "Download the guide" beats "Learn more." "Start your free trial" beats "Get started."
- **First person converts better.** "Start my free trial" outperforms "Start your free trial" in most tests. The reader mentally takes ownership of the action.
- **Button color should contrast with the email background.** If your button blends in, the CTA text is irrelevant because no one will see it.
- **Place the CTA above the fold** (visible without scrolling) for short emails. For longer story-driven emails, the CTA belongs at the end after the narrative builds.

Personalized CTAs convert 202% better than generic ones (HubSpot, 2024). "Get my free SEO audit" beats "Get a free audit" because it feels like it was written for this specific reader.

## Tone and Voice

Email copy should sound like it comes from a person, not a corporation. The fastest way to audit your draft: read it out loud. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it. If it sounds like how you would talk to a smart friend who needs help with a specific problem, you are close.

Formality level should match your audience:
- A SaaS product targeting engineers: direct and technical is fine
- A wellness brand for first-time buyers: warm and simple is better
- A B2B service for executives: confident and concise, no fluff

One useful test is the "bar stool test": would you say this sentence to someone sitting next to you at a bar? If not, simplify it.

## Segmentation: Write for Segments, Not the Whole List

Sending the same email to your entire list is one of the most expensive mistakes in email marketing. A new subscriber and a three-time buyer need completely different messages.

Segmented campaigns drive 760% more revenue than non-segmented ones (Campaign Monitor, 2024). The three simplest segments to start with:

1. **New subscribers** (joined in the last 30 days): focus on welcome, onboarding, and building trust
2. **Active customers** (purchased or engaged recently): focus on loyalty, upsells, and referrals
3. **Lapsed users** (no opens or purchases in 90+ days): focus on re-engagement with a direct, human message

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Mistake 1, Writing for yourself, not your reader.**
"We are thrilled to announce" signals the email is about the company, not the reader. Replace every "we" with "you" wherever possible. Your reader asks one question when they open any email: "What is in it for me?" Answer that in the first two sentences.

**Mistake 2, Multiple calls to action.**
Adding a secondary CTA "just in case" splits reader attention and reduces clicks on both. Pick one action per email and cut the rest. If you genuinely have two things to say, send two emails on separate days.

**Mistake 3, Ignoring mobile.**
64% of recipients check email on mobile devices (Snov.io, 2024). Yet 1 in 5 campaigns are not mobile-friendly. If your subject line gets cut off at 32 characters or your CTA button is too small to tap, your copy does not matter.
</Callout>

## Testing Your Copy

Never assume your first draft is optimal. Small copy changes produce large performance differences. A/B testing (also called split testing) sends two versions of an email to equal-sized slices of your list, then measures which version performs better before sending the winner to everyone else.

What to test, in order of impact:

1. **Subject line** (highest leverage, affects open rate for the entire send)
2. **Opening line** (affects whether readers continue past the first sentence)
3. **CTA button text** (affects click-through rate directly)
4. **Email length** (short vs. long for the same message and audience)

Test one variable at a time. If you change the subject line and the CTA in the same test, you will not know which change drove the result. Most email platforms including Klaviyo, Mailchimp, and ActiveCampaign include built-in A/B testing. Use it on every major send.

44.14% of all email clicks happen within the first hour after sending (Snov.io, 2024). That means your first-hour data is reliable enough to declare a winner for most list sizes above 5,000 subscribers.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**The best-looking email loses to the most relevant one: write for one reader, with one message, asking for one action.**
