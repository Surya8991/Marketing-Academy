# The Peak-End Rule

Your customer does not remember their experience. They remember a heavily edited highlight reel — specifically, the most emotionally intense moment and the final moment. Get those two beats right and a mediocre middle barely registers. Get them wrong and a great middle is wasted.

## The Origin (Real Research)

The peak-end rule comes from Daniel Kahneman, Barbara Fredrickson, Charles Schreiber, and Donald Redelmeier's 1993 paper [*When More Pain Is Preferred to Less: Adding a Better End*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9280.1993.tb00589.x). In the cold-pressor experiment, participants held a hand in painfully cold 14 degree C water for 60 seconds (trial A), then in a second trial held it for 60 seconds at 14 degrees plus an extra 30 seconds while the water was gently warmed to 15 degrees (trial B). Trial B was objectively worse — same peak pain, more total pain. Yet when asked which trial they would repeat, a majority chose trial B.

Kahneman and Redelmeier replicated the finding in a clinical setting in their 1996 [colonoscopy study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0304395996028948) (N = 154 patients). Patients who got a deliberately gentler last minute rated the procedure as less unpleasant — and were measurably more likely to return for follow-up screening — even though they endured a longer procedure overall. The retrospective memory of an experience is roughly the average of its emotional peak and its end, not its duration. Kahneman later called this "duration neglect" in *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011).

## How It Actually Works

There are two selves at work: the *experiencing self* that lives through every moment, and the *remembering self* that decides whether you will come back. Marketing speaks almost exclusively to the remembering self.

A 90-minute flight with one terrible 5-minute delay at deplaning will be remembered as a bad flight. A 14-hour flight with a warm goodbye and a small unexpected gift at the gate will be remembered fondly. Duration barely figures in.

## Why Marketers Care (2024 / 2025 examples)

- **IKEA's bistro and Swedish Food Market.** The cinnamon-bun-and-meatball stop near the exit is not an afterthought — it is the deliberate "end" of a physically exhausting maze. Multiple CX teardowns in 2024 ([CMSWire](https://www.cmswire.com/customer-experience/using-the-peak-end-rule-for-better-customer-journeys/), [Yotpo](https://www.yotpo.com/blog/the-peak-end-rule-in-cx/)) credit the bistro with rescuing the remembered experience from the maze in the middle.
- **Trader Joe's checkout.** The cashier chats, hand-stamps the kid's sticker, throws in a flower. It is the last 90 seconds, and it is engineered. Internal NPS at Trader Joe's consistently outranks larger grocers despite smaller selection and no loyalty program.
- **Duolingo's streak celebration.** The end of every lesson is a confetti-burst peak — XP, gem count, streak fire. The middle (drill-and-kill grammar) is forgettable on purpose. Duolingo reported a 350M user base in 2024 with engagement metrics that embarrass traditional ed-tech.
- **Apple Store checkout.** No cash register, no queue, no "have a nice day." A blue-shirt walks you to the door with your bag, says your name, and hands you the box. That is the engineered end.

<Callout type="example">
Disney's FastPass+ (now Genie+) is a peak-end machine. Magic Kingdom guests wait an average of 45 minutes per ride — the middle is literally a queue. But the fireworks-and-castle-projection finale at park close is the peak, and the cast-member "see ya real soon" wave at the turnstile is the end. Disney's 2024 Parks segment posted $9.3B in operating income on a model that should, by ride-throughput math, generate complaints. It does not, because the remembering self goes home humming.
</Callout>

## How to Apply It Ethically

- **Map your journey and mark the actual peak.** Survey customers on the single most emotional moment. If your "peak" is unintentional — a confusing form, a long checkout — fix it before adding new highs.
- **Engineer the end on purpose.** The last email in your onboarding sequence, the order-confirmation page, the cancellation flow — these are remembered disproportionately. Spend design budget there, not on the splashy homepage hero.
- **Front-load pain, back-load delight.** If you must collect 12 form fields, collect 10 first and end with two easy ones. Kahneman's data says the order matters more than the count.
- **End every support ticket with a human line.** Not "Is there anything else?" — a specific, named, warm sign-off. It is the cheapest CSAT lever in your stack.

## Where It Backfires / Ethical Limits

The dark version is the "dark pattern goodbye": a cancellation flow that hides the unsubscribe button behind a guilt-ridden video of a sad puppy. You are weaponizing the end against the customer's actual interest. The FTC's 2024 ["click-to-cancel" rule](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/10/federal-trade-commission-announces-final-click-cancel-rule-making-it-easier-consumers-end-recurring) was a direct response to this pattern.

Peak-end also fails when the *middle* contains a deal-breaker — a security breach, a billing error, a rude rep. No amount of confetti at the end will paper over a broken trust event. Use peak-end to amplify a good experience, never to disguise a bad one.

## Key Takeaways

- Memory of an experience is roughly the average of its emotional peak and its end. Duration is largely ignored (Kahneman & Redelmeier, 1996).
- Audit your customer journey for both — most brands accidentally engineer the peak and ignore the end entirely.
- The cheapest experience upgrade in marketing is usually the last 60 seconds of any interaction.
