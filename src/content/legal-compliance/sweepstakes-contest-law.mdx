Giveaways look simple from the outside. Post a prize, ask people to enter, pick a winner.

But 'giveaway' is not a legal category. What you actually build is either a sweepstakes, a contest, or, if you get the mechanics wrong, an illegal lottery. Getting this distinction wrong can mean state fines, platform bans, or a shutdown notice from an attorney general's office.

<Callout type="warning">
This lesson explains common compliance patterns, it is not legal advice. Sweepstakes and contest law varies significantly by US state and by country, so have a lawyer review your official rules before launch, especially if prizes exceed a few thousand dollars.
</Callout>

## Sweepstakes vs. Contest vs. Lottery

A **lottery** has three legal ingredients: prize, chance, and consideration (something of value paid to enter). If all three are present and you are not a licensed government lottery, it is illegal.

A **sweepstakes** removes consideration. Winners are chosen by random chance, but entry must be free.

A **contest** removes chance instead. Winners are chosen by skill, like judging the best photo or caption, so requiring a small entry fee is often allowed since chance is not involved.

Most brand 'giveaways' are legally sweepstakes. That single fact drives almost every rule in this lesson.

## Why "No Purchase Necessary" Actually Matters

You have seen the phrase "No Purchase Necessary" at the bottom of a million promotions. It is not boilerplate, it is the thing that keeps your sweepstakes from being an illegal lottery.

If entering requires a purchase, a fee, or "consideration," meaning something of measurable value, the promotion crosses into lottery territory unless you hold a gambling license. Requiring effort, like a lengthy multi-step task, can also count as consideration under some state laws, which is a common trap.

<Callout type="info">
The fix, an Alternate Method of Entry (AMOE): a free way to enter, such as mailing in a postcard or filling out an online form, that gives equal odds of winning to purchase-based entrants. If any part of your funnel nudges people toward buying something to enter, you need a real AMOE sitting right next to it.
</Callout>

Once you have a compliant entry path, the next question is what your rules document has to say.

## What Official Rules Must Cover

Every sweepstakes needs a written "Official Rules" page, linked from every post and ad promoting it. At minimum, it should spell out:

- **Eligibility**: minimum age, residency restrictions, and who is excluded (employees, their families)
- **Entry period**: exact start and end dates and times, including time zone
- **Prize details**: description, approximate retail value, and how it will be delivered
- **Winner selection**: random drawing method and odds, or judging criteria for a skill contest
- **Sponsor identity**: the legal entity running the promotion, not just a brand name

Skip any of these and you have left an opening for a disqualified winner to sue, or for a regulator to call the whole thing deceptive.

## The State Registration Trap

Here is the part most small businesses miss entirely: a handful of US states require you to register your sweepstakes and post a bond before it launches if the prize value crosses a threshold. New York and Florida generally require registration and bonding once the total prize pool exceeds $5,000. Rhode Island's threshold is lower, and it applies to promotions reaching its residents even if your business is based elsewhere.

<Callout type="example">
A skincare brand runs a $10,000 giveaway open to all 50 states and never registers in New York or Florida. Even with perfect official rules otherwise, that alone is a compliance violation that can trigger fines in those states.
</Callout>

If your prize pool is high value and your entry is open nationwide, check registration thresholds for New York, Florida, and Rhode Island before you set a launch date, not after.

## Platform Rules Are a Separate Layer

Meta and Instagram's promotion guidelines sit on top of, not instead of, sweepstakes law. As of 2025, Instagram's updated guidelines prohibit requiring engagement actions, like liking a post, following an account, or tagging friends, as a mandatory condition of entry in some contexts, and every promotion must include a release statement saying the platform is not a sponsor.

"Tag to win" mechanics are a frequent violation source: tagging real people without their consent can also brush up against platform spam and privacy rules, separate from any sweepstakes law issue. Treat your official rules and your platform-specific disclaimers as two checklists, not one.

## International Complications

Running the same giveaway across borders multiplies the risk. Canada's Quebec province has its own registration and disclosure requirements that are stricter than the rest of Canada.

The EU treats prize promotions under broader consumer protection and unfair commercial practices rules, and some member states restrict "games of chance" tightly regardless of prize value. The safest default for a first giveaway: restrict entry to a single country or a short list of states you have actually researched, then expand once you have a repeatable compliance process.

<Callout type="tip">
Keep a simple internal checklist you reuse for every giveaway: entry mechanics, AMOE, official rules link, eligible regions, registration check, and platform disclaimer. A five-minute checklist beats a legal review that only happens after launch.
</Callout>

Running a giveaway well is mostly a documentation problem, not a creative one. Get the free entry path, the written rules, and the region check right, and the fun part, picking a winner, takes care of itself.
