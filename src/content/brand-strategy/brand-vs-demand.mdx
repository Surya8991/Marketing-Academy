## The Split That Defines Your Growth Trajectory

Every marketing budget contains an implicit bet: how much of your spend builds long-term preference versus how much captures short-term revenue?

Most teams default to demand generation because it is measurable, attributable, and satisfying to report. But WARC's 2024 Multiplier Effect study found the industry has inverted to a **69% performance / 31% brand** average split - the mirror image of what the evidence recommends. Companies that rebalanced toward a 60/40 mix (brand to demand) saw an **average ROI uplift of 90%** over a rolling three-year window.

That is not a rounding error. That is the compounding cost of ignoring brand.

---

## The Binet and Field Framework

Les Binet and Peter Field analysed roughly **1,000 IPA Effectiveness Award case studies** over several decades. Their core finding: marketing works through two distinct mechanisms that operate on different timescales.

| Dimension | Brand Building | Demand Generation |
|---|---|---|
| Timescale | 6 months to 3+ years | Days to weeks |
| Mechanism | Emotional salience, mental availability | Rational persuasion, intent capture |
| Measurement | Brand tracking, share of voice | Conversions, ROAS, CPA |
| Effect decay | Slow (years) | Fast (hours to days) |
| Primary metric | Market share growth | Revenue this quarter |

Neither works without the other. Demand generation harvests the demand that brand building plants.

## What Brand Building Actually Does

Brand building is not "awareness for awareness sake." It accomplishes three measurable things:

1. **Reduces price sensitivity** - Buyers with strong brand preference pay more and defect less. Byron Sharp's research at the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute shows that mental availability is the single strongest predictor of market share in mature categories.

2. **Lowers customer acquisition cost over time** - When a prospect already knows and trusts your brand, your demand gen spend converts at a higher rate. You are paying to capture demand you already created, not create and capture simultaneously.

3. **Creates a compounding moat** - Brand equity accumulates like compound interest. A brand that has been consistently present in a category for five years is structurally cheaper to maintain than a new entrant trying to buy the same mental availability with performance spend.

<Callout type="info">
The 60/40 split is a starting point, not a universal law. Binet and Field's own follow-up work shows the optimal ratio shifts by category maturity, purchase cycle, and audience size. B2B companies and high-consideration purchases often do better at 46/54 brand/demand. Fast-moving consumer goods trend closer to 70/30. Run the 60/40 split as your baseline and test from there.
</Callout>

## What Demand Generation Actually Does

Demand generation - paid search, retargeting, email sequences, affiliate - is the harvesting layer. It works by intercepting people who are already in a purchase decision and making your offer the easiest, most credible choice.

Its strengths:
- **Immediate, attributable revenue** - You can see exactly what a campaign returned in 48 hours.
- **Precise targeting** - You reach people with demonstrated intent rather than the full category.
- **Tight feedback loops** - Creative and copy can be iterated weekly.

Its weakness: it only works on people who are already in the market. At any given moment, only 3-5% of your total addressable market is actively buying. Demand generation fights over that 3-5%. Brand building shapes the preferences of the other 95% so that when they do enter the market, they think of you first.

<Callout type="warning">
The short-termism trap: when budgets get cut, brand spend is usually the first to go because its ROI is harder to attribute in a 30-day reporting window. This feels rational but is self-defeating. Companies that cut brand during downturns consistently underperform in the recovery period, sometimes for years afterward. The WARC data shows brands that maintained brand investment through the 2008-2009 recession recovered market share 3x faster than those that went performance-only.
</Callout>

## A Practical Audit: Where Does Your Budget Actually Sit?

Before debating the ideal ratio, map your current reality:

1. **Categorise every line item** - Is this spend trying to reach people who do not know you yet (brand), or convert people who already do (demand)?
2. **Calculate your actual split** - Most teams discover they are at 80/20 or 90/10 in favour of demand once they categorise honestly.
3. **Identify the gap** - If you are at 85/15, moving to 70/30 is a realistic first-year target. Jumping straight to 60/40 in one budget cycle rarely gets approved.
4. **Choose leading indicators for brand** - Share of voice, aided/unaided awareness, brand consideration scores, and net promoter score give you something to track while the long-term effects compound.

## The Measurement Problem (and How to Handle It)

The reason most organisations skew toward demand generation is measurement, not strategy. Performance campaigns produce clean attribution: this ad generated that sale. Brand campaigns produce fuzzy attribution: this spend made future sales cheaper and more likely, but you cannot pin a single conversion to a single brand impression.

The correct response is not to abandon brand measurement. It is to use the right instruments:

- **Media Mix Modelling (MMM)** - Statistical analysis that separates the contribution of brand from demand spend in your revenue curve over time. Several platforms now offer MMM at accessible price points for mid-market companies.
- **Brand tracking studies** - Quarterly surveys measuring awareness, consideration, and preference in your target category.
- **Share of voice vs. share of market correlation** - Brands that hold share of voice above their share of market tend to grow. This is one of the most robust findings in the Binet and Field dataset.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The industry average is 69% performance / 31% brand - the inverse of what drives the highest long-term ROI.
- Rebalancing toward 60/40 brand/demand is associated with a 90% average ROI uplift over three years (WARC Multiplier Effect, 2024).
- Brand building works by creating mental availability in the 95% of your market that is not actively buying right now.
- Demand generation harvests the demand that brand building creates - cutting brand spend makes demand spend more expensive over time.
- Measurement gaps, not strategic disagreement, drive most companies toward short-termism. Fix the measurement first.
