# Brand Strategy 101

Most companies compete on features. The ones that win long-term compete on meaning. Brand strategy is the discipline of building that meaning deliberately - so customers choose you even when a cheaper or technically similar alternative exists.

Apple's 2024 gross margin was 43%. Samsung's was 18%. Both make smartphones. The difference is not manufacturing efficiency - it is brand strategy.

---

## What Is Brand Strategy?

Brand strategy is a long-term plan that defines what your organisation stands for, who it is for, and how it consistently communicates that across every touchpoint.

It is NOT:
- A logo or color palette (those are brand identity)
- A tagline (that is brand voice)
- A campaign (that is brand activation)

Brand strategy is the document that makes every logo, tagline, and campaign decision obvious.

## The Four-Layer Framework

### Layer 1: Purpose

Purpose answers: "Why does this organisation exist beyond making money?"

Patagonia's purpose: Save our home planet. Every product, campaign, and business decision runs through that filter. They sued their own distributors for misusing the brand. They ran ads telling people not to buy their jackets. Counterintuitive - but entirely consistent with purpose.

A strong purpose:
- Is not about the product category
- Cannot be achieved (it is a direction, not a destination)
- Eliminates a whole class of decisions automatically

### Layer 2: Positioning

Positioning answers: "Where do we win, and against whom?"

The classic positioning statement format:

> For [target audience] who [need or problem], [brand name] is the [category] that [unique benefit] because [reason to believe].

Gartner's 2024 CMO Survey found that marketing budgets dropped to 7% of company revenue (down from 9.1% in 2023). Tight budgets make positioning more important, not less - you cannot afford to speak to everyone.

Positioning requires choices. You cannot be the cheapest and the most premium. You cannot be for everyone and resonate deeply with someone.

### Layer 3: Personality

Personality answers: "If this brand were a person, how would they speak and behave?"

Brand personality attributes usually fall into five dimensions (Aaker's model, 1997):
1. Sincerity (down-to-earth, honest, wholesome)
2. Excitement (daring, spirited, imaginative)
3. Competence (reliable, intelligent, successful)
4. Sophistication (upper class, charming)
5. Ruggedness (outdoorsy, tough)

Most B2B brands default to "Competence" and then wonder why they sound identical to every competitor. Pick the combination that reflects your actual culture - customers detect inauthenticity quickly.

### Layer 4: Promise

<Callout type="info">
A brand promise is the single most important thing customers can count on - every time, without exception. It is not an advertising claim. It is an operational commitment.
</Callout>

FedEx's original promise: "When it absolutely, positively has to be there overnight." That promise shaped their entire logistics operation, not just their marketing.

Your promise must be:
- Defensible: you can actually deliver it consistently
- Differentiated: competitors are not already owning it
- Valued: customers care enough to pay for it

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The two most common brand strategy failures: (1) Aspirational positioning that operations cannot deliver - you promise premium, you deliver average, you destroy trust. (2) Repositioning every time a new CMO arrives - brand equity compounds over decades, not quarters.
</Callout>

---

## A Real Example: Apple and the Headphone Jack

<Callout type="example">
In 2016 Apple removed the headphone jack from iPhone 7. The internet erupted. But the decision was entirely consistent with Apple's brand positioning: courage, simplicity, and being first to remove legacy friction. Their brand strategy made a product decision that looked irrational from the outside look inevitable from the inside.
</Callout>

This is the real power of brand strategy - it becomes a decision-making filter, not just a marketing document.

---

## Building Your Brand Strategy Document

A working brand strategy document covers:

1. **Purpose statement** - one sentence, present tense
2. **Target audience** - specific person, not a demographic bucket
3. **Competitive positioning** - where you win, where you do not compete
4. **Personality attributes** - 3 to 5 words, with behavioral examples for each
5. **Brand promise** - one sentence, operationally deliverable
6. **Proof points** - 3 to 5 facts that substantiate the promise
7. **Tone of voice guidelines** - do/do not examples for copy

The document should be short enough to memorise and specific enough to be useful. If every statement could apply to a competitor, rewrite it.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Brand strategy is a decision-making tool, not just a marketing tool
- The four layers - Purpose, Positioning, Personality, Promise - must align or the strategy collapses
- Consistency over time compounds into brand equity; changing direction erases it
- Tight budgets (like the 7% of revenue average in 2024) make sharp positioning more valuable, not less
