# Brand Tracking

## What It Is

Brand tracking is the practice of measuring how a target audience thinks and feels about your brand over time. Instead of asking "did our campaign perform well?", brand tracking asks "are more people aware of us, considering us, and choosing us over rivals?" You run the same survey questions on a recurring schedule - weekly, monthly, or quarterly - and watch the numbers move.

The core metrics sit inside a funnel with five stages: Awareness (people know you exist), Consideration (people would put you on their shortlist), Preference (people would choose you over competitors), Usage (people have actually bought from you), and Loyalty (people keep coming back and recommend you). Each stage is narrower than the one above it. A brand with 80% awareness but only 20% consideration has a messaging problem. A brand with high consideration but low preference has a positioning problem.

Brand tracking is different from campaign measurement. Campaign measurement tells you what happened during a specific ad flight. Brand tracking tells you the cumulative effect of all your marketing on how the public perceives you. That distinction matters when you are justifying brand-building budgets to a CFO who wants to see numbers.

## Real-World Example

YouGov runs continuous brand tracking across retail categories in the United States. In their 2024 US retail rankings, Walmart earned a consideration score of 60.8 - the highest across all three retail categories measured. That single number, drawn from daily tracking surveys, tells Walmart's leadership that more than 6 in 10 shoppers would actively consider buying there. The same data showed Nike as the most improved brand across retail categories, giving Nike's team evidence that their brand investments were shifting the needle. (Source: YouGov US Retail Rankings 2024)

<Callout type="example">
Walmart's 2024 consideration score of 60.8 - tracked daily by YouGov across thousands of respondents - illustrates why brand tracking data lands in boardrooms. It is not a vanity metric. It is a leading indicator of revenue. When consideration rises, purchase volume tends to follow within one to two quarters. When it drops, leadership has time to respond before sales figures show the damage.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Without brand tracking, marketing teams fly blind on brand health. They know click-through rates, conversion rates, and ROAS (Return on Ad Spend), but those are bottom-of-funnel signals. They tell you what happened with the people who were already close to buying. Brand tracking tells you what is happening with everyone else - the people who might buy from you six months from now.

Brand tracking becomes essential in three situations. First, when you are running a brand campaign and need to prove it worked beyond "impressions delivered." Second, when a competitor is gaining ground and you need to know whether their ads are hurting your consideration scores. Third, when you are entering a new market and need baseline data before spending.

## How It Works

The basic process has four steps: define your audience, design your questions, field the survey on a schedule, and report trends - not point-in-time snapshots.

Your survey should cover each funnel stage with a clear question. For awareness: "Which of these brands have you heard of?" For consideration: "Which would you consider buying from?" For preference: "Which would be your first choice?" Run the survey to a fresh sample each wave so you are not surveying the same people repeatedly, which would introduce bias.

Competitive benchmarking is what makes the data actionable. Knowing your consideration score is 35% means little in isolation. Knowing your closest rival sits at 52% tells you exactly how far you have to close the gap.

<Callout type="info">
Most brands run brand tracking quarterly. If you are running a large awareness campaign, add a pulse survey during the flight - a shorter, more frequent version - so you can see the lift in real time and adjust creative or media spend if the needle is not moving.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake is treating brand tracking as a one-time project rather than a continuous program. Teams commission a brand health study, present it once, and file it away. Six months later no one knows if the number went up or down. Brand tracking only delivers value as a time series. You need at least three waves of data before the trends become meaningful. Commit to a schedule before you field your first survey, or do not bother starting.
</Callout>

A close second mistake is measuring the wrong audience. If your brand targets 35-55 year old homeowners but your tracking survey samples 18-65 year olds with no filtering, the numbers are noise. Define your target audience tightly before you design the survey, and apply that definition as a quota or filter in every wave.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Brand tracking turns vague perceptions into time-series data that proves - or disproves - whether your marketing is building a brand people actually choose.**
