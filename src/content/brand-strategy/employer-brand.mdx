# Employer Brand

## What It Is

Your employer brand is the reputation you have as a place to work. It is what candidates say about you when they talk to friends, what employees write on Glassdoor, and what a talented engineer thinks when they see your job posting. It exists whether you manage it or not.

The term covers two things. First, your Employee Value Proposition (EVP) - the specific set of benefits, culture signals, and career promises you offer in exchange for someone's time and talent. Second, how you communicate that EVP across every channel where job seekers look: LinkedIn, your careers page, job boards, and word of mouth.

Employer branding is distinct from consumer branding. A company can sell great products and still be seen as a miserable place to work. The discipline asks: what do we genuinely offer people who work here, and how do we make that visible to the right candidates?

## Real-World Example

Salesforce spent years building its employer brand around the concept of "Ohana" - a Hawaiian word meaning family and community. By 2024, the company consistently ranks among Fortune's 100 Best Companies to Work For, and reports that its strong employer brand reduces its cost per hire by more than 40% compared to industry benchmarks. Candidates apply proactively rather than needing to be chased through paid recruiting.

<Callout type="example">
**Salesforce: From Perks List to Cultural Identity**

Rather than advertising free lunches and gym memberships, Salesforce anchored its employer brand to a single idea: belonging. It launched public equality reports, tied executive pay to diversity metrics, and gave employees 56 hours of paid volunteer time per year. These specific, auditable commitments gave the EVP credibility. By 2023, Salesforce received over 1 million job applications annually - a direct consequence of candidates seeking them out rather than the reverse.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Recruiting is a buyer's market for skilled talent. A strong employer brand lowers every cost associated with hiring:

- **Cost per hire drops.** Companies with strong employer brands cut cost per hire by up to 43% (Withe, 2024). That is real budget freed for other work.
- **Applications increase without extra spend.** 75% of active job seekers say they are more likely to apply when a company actively manages its employer brand.
- **Turnover falls.** Companies with strong employer brands see 28% less employee turnover, meaning you spend less replacing people who leave.
- **Revenue is affected.** 96% of companies say employer brand directly affects revenue - both through talent quality and through consumer perception.

The business problem this solves: when you have no deliberate employer brand, you compete only on salary and job title. You attract whoever responds to a cold posting. When your employer brand is clear and credible, the right people come to you already aligned with your values.

## How It Works

Building an employer brand is a four-stage process: discover, define, activate, and measure.

  C["Activate: Publish across channels\n(Careers page, LinkedIn, job ads, employee advocacy)"]

**Discover** means finding out what your current employer reputation actually is - not what leadership hopes it is. Read Glassdoor reviews. Survey recent hires about why they joined. Ask departing employees why they are leaving. This data becomes your baseline.

**Define** means writing an EVP that is honest. An EVP lists the specific things you offer: career growth paths, flexibility, mission, compensation philosophy, team culture. It should include trade-offs. A startup might offer equity and fast ownership but not job security. Say that. Candidates who want stability will self-select out, saving everyone time.

**Activate** means distributing the EVP across every touchpoint a candidate sees. Your careers page is the most important owned channel - most candidates check it before applying. LinkedIn company posts and employee stories are the highest-reach earned channel. Job descriptions are underused: rewriting them in plain language that reflects real culture converts more qualified applicants.

**Measure** tracks whether the brand is working. Key metrics: number of inbound applications, offer acceptance rate, time to fill, cost per hire, and employee Net Promoter Score (eNPS - a survey that asks employees how likely they are to recommend the company as a place to work).

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**The biggest trap: writing an EVP that describes the company you wish you were, not the company you are.**

Candidates read Glassdoor. They talk to your employees. They notice when your careers page says 'we value work-life balance' and your job ads require 'nights and weekends as needed.' The gap between stated and lived culture destroys trust - and trust is the only currency employer branding runs on. Start with an honest audit. Fix real problems before you advertise solutions you have not built yet.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Start with exit interviews and Glassdoor, not a branding workshop.**

The fastest way to understand your employer brand is to read what people say after they leave. Exit interviews and public reviews surface the real EVP - the one candidates already believe. Use that as your starting point, not a blank slide deck. Confirm what is genuinely true, then amplify it.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Your employer brand is your recruiting strategy - define it deliberately or your worst reviews will define it for you.**
