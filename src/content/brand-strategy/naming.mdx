# Brand Naming

## What It Is

A brand name is the single word or short phrase that customers use to find you, talk about you, and remember you. It is the entry point to everything else: your logo, your story, your reputation. A bad name creates friction at every stage of the customer journey. A great name does quiet work for you every day.

Brand naming is not the same as picking a business name for legal purposes. A legal name can be anything. A brand name has to survive real-world conditions: a customer hearing it once in a noisy room, typing it into a search bar on a phone, or describing it to a friend over coffee.

There are six main naming categories used by professionals. A descriptive name tells you exactly what the company does (General Motors). An invented name is made-up entirely (Kodak, Xerox). An evocative name suggests a feeling or idea without stating it literally (Amazon, Slack). An acronym compresses a longer name (IBM, BMW). A founder name uses a person (Ford, Disney). A metaphor borrows meaning from an unrelated object (Apple, Jaguar). Each category has different strengths and risks.

## Real-World Example

Better Place raised $850 million in venture capital between 2007 and 2012 to build a battery-swapping network for electric vehicles. Tesla was founded in 2003 and went public in 2010. By May 2013, Better Place had filed for bankruptcy. Tesla had not. Both companies were selling the same core idea: clean electric transport. One name won and one name vanished.

<Callout type="example">
Better Place vs. Tesla (2007-2013): Better Place raised $850M and built real infrastructure. Its name described a goal - a vague promise of improvement. Tesla named itself after Nikola Tesla, a specific inventor associated with electricity, invention, and genius. The name carried a story and a set of associations that needed no explanation. Better Place felt like a slogan. Tesla felt like an identity. When Better Place filed for bankruptcy in 2013, its brand had never become a noun. People say 'I drive a Tesla.' Nobody said 'I drive a Better Place.'
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

A weak name costs money across every channel you use. Paid search becomes more expensive because your brand terms compete with generic keywords. Word-of-mouth slows because people cannot easily pronounce or recall your name. Press coverage is thinner because journalists find it hard to write around an awkward brand name in a headline.

A 2024 analysis by Spellbrand found that brands with short, pronounceable names - typically one to three syllables - achieve significantly higher unaided recall in consumer surveys compared to longer or hyphenated alternatives. Recall is the foundation of trust, and trust drives purchase decisions: 59% of consumers say they prefer to buy from brands they already know (Source: DesignRush, 2024).

If you are naming a new product, entering a crowded market, or rebranding after a pivot, brand naming is one of the highest-leverage decisions you will make. The name is the only brand asset that appears on every touchpoint simultaneously: packaging, email subject lines, invoices, social handles, and human conversation.

## How It Works

Professional naming follows a structured process. The goal is to move from a blank page to a shortlist of names that have been tested against real constraints.

Start by locking down your brand positioning: what you do, who you serve, and how you are different. A name that sounds great in isolation can feel wrong if it clashes with your positioning. A luxury product should not have a name that sounds bargain-priced, and a youth-focused brand should not have a name that sounds like a law firm.

Next, choose your naming category deliberately. Invented names like Google or Kodak are ownable and trademarkable but require marketing spend to build meaning from zero. Evocative names like Amazon or Slack arrive with borrowed meaning but require care to avoid being too vague. Descriptive names are easy to understand but are almost impossible to trademark because the words are common property.

Generate volume before you filter. Aim for 50 to 100 raw candidates. Use root words, portmanteaus (blending two words), foreign language translations, and phonetic variations. Then apply four filters in order: Can you say it aloud without hesitation? Can you spell it after hearing it once? Does it survive a Google search without being buried by unrelated results? Is the trademark clear in your primary market?

<Callout type="tip">
Test names out loud before a trademark search. Ask three people outside your company to spell the name after you say it once. If more than one person spells it wrong, the name will cost you in search traffic and word-of-mouth every single day.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake is naming a company after its current product category. If you name yourself 'FastEmailSend,' you own that lane forever - or you spend years and millions trying to escape it. Amazon was not called 'OnlineBooks.' Slack was not called 'WorkChat.' Leave room for the brand to grow. A name that is too literal becomes a trap the moment you expand your product line, enter a new geography, or pivot your business model.
</Callout>

A second mistake is optimizing for internal taste instead of external recall. Founders often choose names that feel meaningful to the founding team - inside references, clever wordplay, or personal connections - but mean nothing to a customer hearing the name for the first time. The customer's first encounter is always cold. The name has to work for a stranger.

A third mistake is skipping the trademark search until late in the process. Domain names and social handles are cheaper problems to solve. A trademark conflict can force a rename after you have already printed packaging, launched ads, and built an audience. Run a basic trademark search before you fall in love with any candidate.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**A brand name is not what you call yourself - it is what sticks in the mind of a stranger who hears it once.**
