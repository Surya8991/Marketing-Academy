# Writing to Think

Amazon's most-copied cultural practice is not the flywheel or Working Backwards. It is the six-page memo: no slides, no bullets, just clean written prose. Bezos's reason was simple: the discipline of writing full sentences forces the discipline of full thinking. Marketers who write clearly strategize clearly. It is not a coincidence.

## Quick Summary

- Writing is not just how you communicate ideas; it is how you discover which of them are actually thought-through.
- Bullets and slides let vague thinking hide. Full sentences do not.
- The habit is deceptively small: write out your reasoning in prose before every meaningful decision or presentation.
- Payoff compounds: the writers on any team become the strategy-shapers within a year, not because they write well but because they think well.

## What It Actually Is

Paul Graham described it best: ["writing is nature's way of showing you how sloppy your thinking is."](http://www.paulgraham.com/words.html) When ideas live in your head, they feel coherent. When you have to arrange them in sequence on the page, the gaps become impossible to hide.

The practice is not "become a better writer." It is using writing as the thinking tool. Whether the final deliverable is a memo, an email, a strategy doc, or nothing at all, the writing exposes what the analysis missed.

Bullet points are the enemy here. Bullets are what let poor thinking look tidy. "Launch Q2 campaign" as a bullet passes; the same claim in a paragraph ("we should launch a campaign in Q2 because...") forces you to actually finish the sentence.

## Why It Matters

Amazon requires six-page written memos for meaningful decisions. Meetings begin with 20 minutes of silent reading. Bezos's rationale: bullet points are ["easy to hide behind"](https://commoncog.com/blogs/working-backwards-jeff-bezos.html), full narratives are not.

The result is not just better meetings; it is better decisions. When you cannot get a decision approved without writing the reasoning end-to-end, the weak decisions never survive the writing.

Every senior marketer eventually notices the correlation: the members of any team who become influential in strategy are almost always the ones who write. The direction of causation is easy to miss. It is not that strategic thinkers happen to be writers. Writing regularly *builds* strategic thinking. The correlation is causal.

The reverse is also true. Teams that live in slide decks and bullet lists produce plans that look good in a presentation and fall apart on contact with reality. The plan looked done because the slides made it look done.

## The Playbook: The Prose Test

Before any decision that would benefit from clear thinking, write out the reasoning as prose. Not bullets, not a matrix. Complete sentences that string into paragraphs.

The test is deceptively simple. Take the plan from bullets to prose. Read it back. Ask: does the reasoning actually hold sentence to sentence, or does it collapse into "and then a miracle happens"?

Most first drafts fail this test. That is the point. The failure surfaces on the page while there is still time to fix it, instead of at the launch retro.

<Callout type="tip">
The best time to write for thinking is before a meeting, not after. Post-meeting summaries capture what got decided. Pre-meeting memos force the decision to be defensible before it hits the room. The pre-meeting memo saves the meeting from becoming the debate.
</Callout>

## What Actually Improves From Writing

**Strategy documents.** A one-page prose strategy document exposes weak positioning that a 30-slide deck can hide for years.

**Campaign briefs.** A prose brief forces you to explain the "why" behind creative direction, not just list requirements.

**Positioning statements.** If you cannot write a paragraph explaining your positioning without using "leading" or "innovative," you do not have a position.

**Retros.** Written retros surface the actual causes of wins and losses. Verbal retros default to "the launch was great" and "the market moved."

**Pitches to leadership.** A written prose pitch that anticipates the questions gets funded. A slide-only pitch invites debate for its own sake.

## A Real Example

Stripe's early team was famous for a writing culture disproportionate to its size. Founders Patrick and John Collison enforced full-prose memos for meaningful decisions and hired for writing ability across every role, including engineers.

The observable outcome: Stripe consistently shipped positioning, docs, and product marketing that was clearer than competitors with 10x the marketing headcount. Not because they were better writers by talent, but because writing was the tool by which every idea was inspected for coherence before it shipped.

The direct competitors that lost to Stripe over the years had bigger teams, bigger budgets, and worse ideas that survived internal review because the review was verbal.

<Callout type="warning">
Writing to think does not mean writing more. It means writing to the point where reasoning has to be exposed. A three-sentence memo that gets to the reasoning is worth more than a 20-page brief that never does. The metric is clarity, not volume.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Writing after the decision instead of before.** Post-hoc documentation captures what was decided. It does not test whether the decision was good. Reverse the order.

**Mistake 2: Confusing writing with corporate prose.** The goal is clear thinking exposed on the page, not weighty-sounding sentences. Short sentences that say something specific beat long ones that sound impressive.

**Mistake 3: Skipping it because "we do not have time."** Ten minutes of writing before a decision saves weeks of implementing a bad decision. The trade always favors the writing.

## Key Takeaways

- Writing is the tool by which vague thinking becomes visible.
- Full sentences expose the gaps that bullets and slides let hide.
- The prose test: write your plan as full sentences and see whether the reasoning survives.
- Do it before decisions, not after; the value is in exposing weakness while there is still time to fix it.
- Teams that write regularly build strategic depth automatically, without any additional training.
