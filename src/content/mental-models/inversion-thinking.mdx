# Inversion: Solving Problems Backwards

Charlie Munger built a career on a single instruction: "Invert, always invert." He credited it to the mathematician Carl Jacobi. The idea: many problems are easier to solve backwards than forwards, and the answers are usually more actionable.

## Quick Summary

- Inversion means solving a problem by first asking what would guarantee failure, then avoiding all of it.
- It is unusually effective in marketing because failure modes are more concrete and universal than success paths.
- The playbook is a single question: "What would guarantee this campaign fails?" followed by "Am I doing any of that?"
- The technique costs 20 minutes and routinely catches decisions the forward analysis missed.

## What It Actually Is

Forward thinking: "How do we build a great landing page?" You imagine a great landing page, list its features, and try to hit them.

Inverted: "What would guarantee this landing page fails?" You imagine a terrible one: slow load, unclear value prop, buried CTA, no social proof, distracting design, mismatched search intent. Then you check whether your current draft has any of those failure signatures.

Both are useful. Only one gives you a specific, actionable checklist you can verify against.

## Why It Matters

Munger, in his famous 2007 USC Law commencement speech, said inversion "solves problems that cannot be solved any other way." The reason is that success has many paths and requires many things to go right, but failure often only requires one thing to go wrong.

That asymmetry is why inversion is a marketing power tool. Successful campaigns look different from each other. Failed campaigns fail in the same handful of ways: unclear positioning, wrong audience, weak offer, poor timing, cluttered creative. Eliminate the handful and you have done more than the average team, without needing genius on the upside.

Even Amazon's writing culture uses a form of inversion. Bezos's "working backwards" process, where teams write the launch press release before building the product, is [inversion applied to product marketing: define what finished success looks like first, then reverse-engineer how to build it](https://commoncog.com/blogs/working-backwards-jeff-bezos.html).

## The Playbook: The Pre-Mortem Drill

The most practical form of inversion is the pre-mortem, popularized by psychologist Gary Klein. Before the campaign launches, gather the team and ask a single question: "It is six months from now and this campaign failed. What happened?"

Everyone spends ten minutes writing failure modes independently, then shares. The exercise surfaces risks that the optimism of forward planning had hidden.

Two rules make the pre-mortem work:

1. **Write before you talk.** Independent writing prevents the loudest voice from anchoring the room. Everyone commits to their own worries in writing first, then discussion begins.
2. **Assume the failure, do not debate it.** The prompt is "we failed, what happened?" not "will we fail?" The assumption unlocks the honest answers you would not get otherwise.

<Callout type="tip">
Pre-mortems work best when someone senior explicitly invites failure talk. If the CMO says "our launch is going to be huge," the pre-mortem is dead on arrival. If the CMO says "I want us to find every way this could break before we ship," the same team suddenly finds them.
</Callout>

## Applying Inversion to Common Marketing Decisions

**Positioning:** Instead of "how do we sound different?" ask "what would guarantee we sound like everyone else?" (jargon, feature lists, superlatives, category cliches). Then remove those.

**Landing pages:** Instead of "how do we improve conversion?" ask "what would kill the conversion rate?" (slow load, wrong headline, unclear next step, mismatched intent from the ad). Check each.

**Content strategy:** Instead of "what should we write?" ask "what would make our content invisible?" (topics 500 competitors also cover, no unique data, no personal angle, no distribution plan). Reject anything with those attributes.

**Career:** Instead of "how do I get promoted?" ask "what would guarantee I do not get promoted?" (invisible work, no measurable wins, no relationship with the person who decides). Fix those.

Each inversion is quick. Each surfaces at least one decision the forward analysis missed.

## A Real Example

In late 2019, a mid-market e-commerce brand ran a pre-mortem before their Black Friday campaign. The forward plan looked strong: aggressive discounts, wide channel mix, holiday-themed creative.

The pre-mortem surfaced three failure modes the plan had ignored:

1. Site performance under peak Black Friday traffic.
2. Inventory: if the top three SKUs sold out on day one, would the campaign message still make sense on day two?
3. Ad account health: the account was heavy on a single Meta ad set, meaning one disapproval could tank half the campaign.

They spent two weeks fixing all three: load-tested the site, staged inventory across regions to prevent day-one stockouts, and diversified the Meta ad set to reduce single-point risk. Campaign ran cleanly and hit 138% of target. The team credited the pre-mortem for catching the inventory issue specifically, which would have been visible only after it hurt.

<Callout type="warning">
Inversion has one failure mode of its own: paralysis. If you list 40 failure modes and try to prevent all of them, you never launch. The rule is to rank by probability times impact and pick the top 3-5. Everything else is acknowledged and accepted, not prevented.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Skipping it because "we already thought about risks."** Forward planning imagines success in detail and mentions risks in passing. Inversion imagines failure in detail. They are different exercises.

**Mistake 2: Doing it alone.** Pre-mortems work because different people imagine different failure modes. A solo pre-mortem finds a fraction of what a team pre-mortem finds.

**Mistake 3: Treating inversion as pessimism.** It is the opposite. The people who name the failure modes clearly are the ones who launch confidently, because they have accounted for them.

## Key Takeaways

- Inversion means solving problems backwards: identify what would cause failure, then avoid it.
- Success has many paths; failure has a small number of common signatures. Eliminate the signatures.
- The pre-mortem is the operational form: assume failure, ask what happened, rank the causes, prevent the top 3-5.
- Apply it to positioning, landing pages, content, and careers, not just campaigns.
- Rank ruthlessly; do not try to prevent every failure mode you find.
