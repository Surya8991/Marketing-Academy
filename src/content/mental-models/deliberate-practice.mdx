# Deliberate Practice for Marketers

Anders Ericsson's research on expert performance found that experience alone does not produce expertise. Deliberate practice does. Ten years of running campaigns is not the same as ten years of getting better at campaigns. Most marketers accidentally do the first and call it the second.

## Quick Summary

- Deliberate practice is structured, feedback-rich effort aimed specifically at improving a defined skill.
- Regular work experience is not deliberate practice, no matter how many years of it you have.
- The four required elements: a specific skill target, high effort at the edge of ability, immediate feedback, and repetition with adjustment.
- Two focused hours per week compounds into career-defining depth over five years. Ten years without it produces surface-level familiarity.

## What It Actually Is

Ericsson studied elite performers across chess, music, athletics, and business. The consistent finding: expertise came from a specific kind of practice, not from years spent doing the job.

Deliberate practice has [four required elements](https://hbr.org/2007/07/the-making-of-an-expert):

1. **A specific skill target.** Not "get better at marketing." "Get better at writing hooks for LinkedIn posts."
2. **Effort at the edge of ability.** You should be failing a nontrivial fraction of the time. If you never fail, you are practicing something you already know.
3. **Immediate feedback.** You need to know whether the last attempt worked before the next attempt.
4. **Repetition with adjustment.** Not the same rep again; the next rep informed by what the last one taught you.

Regular marketing work usually has none of these. You work on many things at once, the stakes prevent you from operating at the edge, feedback arrives weeks late if at all, and campaigns rarely repeat identically enough to build skill.

## Why It Matters

The gap between good marketers and great ones is not talent or intelligence. It is deliberate practice. [Ericsson's research showed](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/26312997-peak) that even in fields believed to be talent-driven (chess, violin), the top performers accumulated more hours of deliberate practice than the second tier, and the gap explained most of the outcome difference.

Marketing has almost no formalized deliberate practice culture. Engineers have code review, doctors have grand rounds, athletes have coaching sessions. Marketers have quarterly retros, which is the wrong scale and the wrong feedback loop.

The practical consequence: most marketing skill compounds by accident when it compounds at all. A career of 15 years often produces the strategic depth that two years of deliberate practice would produce. That gap is where careers get made.

## The Playbook: Two Hours a Week

**Step 1: Pick one specific skill.** Not a job title (be a great CMO) but a discrete skill (write briefs a designer can execute without questions, forecast pipeline from top-of-funnel signals, write cold emails that get replies). One skill at a time.

**Step 2: Create the feedback loop.** Deliberate practice cannot happen without feedback. Options include: a peer who reviews the same category of work, a senior marketer who does 30 minutes of monthly review, a running scorecard on measurable outcomes, an AI assistant used specifically for critique.

**Step 3: Practice at the edge.** For headline writing, this looks like writing 20 headlines for a real (or fake) campaign against a rubric, then having them scored against a benchmark. For briefs, it looks like writing three briefs a week and rewriting them after every review.

**Step 4: Iterate weekly.** Deliberate practice is boring on purpose. The next rep is informed by the last rep. Small adjustments compound into visible gains over months.

<Callout type="tip">
Two focused hours per week is enough. This is not "put in extra time"; it is "put in time of a different kind." Two hours of deliberate practice will teach you more than eight hours of regular execution. Book it on your calendar like a meeting; treat it as non-negotiable.
</Callout>

## A Real Example

A junior product marketer in 2022 committed to deliberate practice on a single skill: writing product-launch briefs. She wrote three briefs a week (some for real launches, some for imaginary ones based on public product releases), submitted each to a senior PMM for 15 minutes of review, and rewrote until the reviewer had zero notes.

Over six months, the review time dropped from 15 minutes to 3. Reviewers stopped correcting; they started giving nuance. Two years later, she was leading launch strategy for the entire product line.

Her peers with the same tenure were still doing the same work at the same skill level they had started with, because they had experience, not deliberate practice.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Confusing volume with practice.** Writing 100 mediocre emails without feedback does not build skill; it builds habit. Ten emails written under critique and rewritten builds skill.

**Mistake 2: Practicing what you are already good at.** Comfortable practice is not deliberate practice. If the reps do not feel like effort, they are the wrong reps.

**Mistake 3: Skipping the feedback step.** Feedback is the most difficult element to arrange, especially in remote work. It is also the non-negotiable one. Without it, you are just doing work.

<Callout type="warning">
The trap is looking at senior marketers and assuming their skill came from career length. Some of it did; most of it came from a smaller number of hours of deliberate practice, often concentrated in a few high-growth years. If you replicate the deliberate practice, you compress the timeline.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Experience alone does not produce expertise. Deliberate practice does.
- Four required elements: specific skill target, effort at the edge, immediate feedback, iteration.
- Two focused hours per week compounds into career-defining depth over five years.
- Rotate skills monthly or go deeper; do not scatter.
- Feedback is the hardest element to arrange and the non-negotiable one. Solve it before you start the practice, not after.
