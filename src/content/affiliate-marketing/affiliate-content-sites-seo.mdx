# Affiliate Content Sites and SEO: How Review Pages Actually Earn

A "best budget blender" article looks like simple content. Underneath, it is a small business with its own SEO strategy, review workflow, and revenue model.

## Quick Summary

- Content sites like Wirecutter earn per-click or per-sale commission on outbound affiliate links, not on ad impressions.
- Google's Site Reputation Abuse policy, enforced from May 2024, punishes sites that host third-party affiliate content just to borrow domain authority.
- Affiliate and product-recommendation sites had the highest impact rate of any category, 71%, in Google's December 2025 core update.
- First-person, hands-on testing signals now outrank generic spec-comparison content.
- AI Overviews are pulling review snippets straight into search results, so click-through rates per impression are shrinking even for sites that still rank.

## How a Review Site Actually Makes Money

The business model is simple in structure, harder in execution. A reader searches "best running shoes for flat feet," lands on a comparison article, clicks an outbound link to a retailer, and the site earns a commission on whatever they buy, usually within a 24-hour to 30-day cookie window.

That means traffic alone does not pay the bills. **Conversion rate on outbound clicks** is the number that actually matters, and it depends on trust, not just rankings.

Wirecutter built its reputation on paying staff writers to physically test products, a cost most affiliate sites skip. That difference now shows up directly in search rankings.

## Why Google Cracked Down in 2024

Google launched its Site Reputation Abuse policy in May 2024, targeting **parasitic SEO**: third-party affiliate content hosted on a trusted domain purely to exploit that domain's authority. Before this policy, a media company could rent out a "/reviews" subfolder to an outside content farm and instantly rank on borrowed trust.

That loophole is now a penalty risk. Domain authority is no longer a guaranteed safe harbor for low-quality third-party review content, even on sites like Forbes or CNN.

<Callout type="warning">
Google's December 2025 core update hit affiliate marketing sites hardest of any category, with a 71% impact rate. If your entire strategy is "rank first, write later," this is the update that ended that approach.
</Callout>

Major publishers felt it too. Forbes, CNN, and The Wall Street Journal all saw meaningful traffic declines in their commerce sections after these updates rolled through.

## What Actually Ranks Now

Google's guidance rewards reviews written in the first person, showing real usage: photos you took, measurements you recorded, failure points you personally found. Generic "top 10" posts assembled from spec sheets and other people's reviews are exactly what the update targets.

A page that ranks and converts today usually has:

- **Original photos or video** of the product in the reviewer's own hands, not stock images.
- **A stated testing method**, how long you used it, what you measured, what conditions.
- **Honest tradeoffs**, at least one real con for every product, even the one you recommend.
- **A clear "why we picked this" section**, not just a spec table.
- **Updated dates**, prices and stock change, and Google notices when a page is stale.

This is more work than the old model of scraping ten competitor pages and rewriting them. It is also why the sites that survive these updates keep compounding traffic while thin competitors disappear.

## The AI Overviews Complication

Even a page that ranks well faces a new problem: Google's AI Overviews now pull review snippets directly into the search results page for many product queries. A user can get a decent answer without ever clicking through.

This creates a real tension. High-quality, well-cited review content is more likely to get pulled into an AI Overview, which is good for visibility but can mean fewer clicks per impression, since the answer already satisfied the searcher.

<Callout type="tip">
Structure your review pages so the AI Overview only gives a partial answer, save the specific "which one should I buy for X situation" recommendation for the full article. Readers with a specific need still have a reason to click through.
</Callout>

The sites adapting well are shifting weight toward comparison depth, personalized recommendations by use case, and community trust signals like reader Q&A, things a snippet can't fully replace.

## Key Takeaways

- Affiliate content sites earn on outbound-click conversions, not traffic volume, so trust and testing quality matter more than word count.
- Google's Site Reputation Abuse policy, active since May 2024, ended the era of renting domain authority for third-party review content.
- Affiliate sites saw the highest impact rate, 71%, in the December 2025 core update, more than any other category.
- First-person testing evidence, original photos, and honest tradeoffs are now ranking signals, not just nice-to-haves.
- AI Overviews are compressing click-through rates even for well-ranked pages, so save specific, personalized recommendations for the full article.
