The fastest way to get ignored by a journalist in Tokyo, Sao Paulo, or Berlin is to send them a press release written for a reporter in New York and simply run it through translation software. Global PR fails for the same reason global ad campaigns fail: it treats "localization" as a language problem when it's actually a relevance problem.

According to Muck Rack's State of Journalism 2025 report, 86% of journalists will ignore a pitch if it's irrelevant to their coverage area. Translation does not fix irrelevance. It just makes the irrelevant pitch readable.

## Translation is the last step, not the first

A successful international PR strategy starts with what matters to that market, not with converting your existing sentences into another language.

Before translating a word, ask what a local journalist in that market actually covers, what data points resonate there, and what news is already competing for their attention that week. Only after answering those questions should you start adapting the release itself.

This reordering, market relevance first, language second, is the single biggest gap between PR teams that get real hits abroad and ones that get ignored.

<Callout type="warning">
Machine-translating idioms, humor, or pop-culture references is one of the most common global PR mistakes. Avoid slang phrases and regional idioms that may not translate well, and double-check that any reference to local laws, norms, or holidays is actually accurate for that market, not assumed from your home market.
</Callout>

## What actually needs to change per market

A localized release is not just a language swap. Several concrete elements need rebuilding for each region.

- **Local data and currency.** Swap US dollar figures and domestic statistics for local currency and regionally sourced data points that a local reader can actually relate to.
- **Local contact details.** Provide a local phone number, email, and mailing address in the release so a journalist can reach a real contact in their own time zone, not one twelve hours away.
- **Local news hooks.** Tie the story to something already happening in that market, a regulation, a local competitor, a cultural moment, instead of the hook that worked at home.
- **Local legal and compliance language.** Claims that are fine in the US, "clinically proven," "guaranteed results," may require different substantiation or disclosure under other countries' advertising and consumer protection rules.
- **Local imagery and names.** Photos, spokesperson quotes, and case studies should reflect the market, not feel imported wholesale from headquarters.

## Building media lists market by market

The single biggest structural mistake in global PR is reusing one master media list with names swapped in from a directory. Build a genuinely separate, targeted list of journalists, outlets, and beats for each country.

Regional press release distribution networks and services exist specifically to help narrow the focus to specific beats, verticals, and industries within a country, rather than blasting a generic international wire. GlobeNewswire, for instance, distributes into 158 countries and 35 local languages, but reach alone does not equal relevance. The list still has to be built around who covers your actual topic in that market.

<Callout type="tip">
Hire or contract a local PR partner in your top two or three priority markets before scaling further. A single well-connected local partner who knows which three journalists actually matter will outperform a generic global distribution blast every time.
</Callout>

## Sequencing a multi-market launch

Do not release the same story in every market simultaneously unless you have a genuine reason to. Stagger the sequence so each market's coverage gets full attention instead of competing internally for your team's bandwidth.

Lead with your strongest, best-resourced market, let that coverage prove the story works, then adapt learnings, which specific angle landed, which data point journalists quoted, into the next market's pitch. This also gives your local spokespeople and translators breathing room instead of a single chaotic global launch day.

Global PR done well does not feel like a translated American press release. It feels like a story written by someone who has actually read that country's news that week, because, if you have built the pitch correctly, someone on your team has.
