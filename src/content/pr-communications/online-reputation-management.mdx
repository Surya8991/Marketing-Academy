Type a brand name into Google. Whatever shows up on that first page, in the next ten seconds, becomes the truth in a stranger's mind.

That is the entire premise of online reputation management, or ORM: the practice of shaping search results, review sites, and social profiles so the internet's first impression of you matches reality. It is not about hiding problems. It is about making sure good, accurate information outranks bad, outdated, or unfair information.

## Why the first page is the whole battlefield

Search behavior data makes the stakes concrete. A 2025 Forrester Research survey found 82% of consumers rarely scroll past page one of Google, and 41% say a negative result reduces their likelihood of contacting a company at all.

Click data backs this up: the top 3 organic results capture 54.4% of all clicks on a search page, with the #1 spot alone averaging 27.6%. A damaging article does not need to disappear from the internet to stop doing damage. It just needs to drop from position 3 to position 13, because almost nobody clicks that far down.

That single fact reframes the whole discipline: you are not fighting to erase the internet, you are fighting for position.

## Suppression, not erasure

"Suppression" is the term ORM practitioners use for pushing unwanted content down the rankings by out-competing it with stronger, more relevant content. It is different from "removal," which means getting content taken down entirely (via legal request, platform policy violation, or a takedown negotiation) and is far harder to pull off.

Suppression works because Google ranks pages, not brands. If you publish more authoritative, more relevant, more frequently updated content about your own name than the negative piece has, your content wins the ranking race over time.

<Callout type="info">
Google's E-A-T standard (Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) rewards content that demonstrates real credibility signals, author bios, citations, depth. Thin, generic "fix your reputation" pages rarely outrank a well-sourced negative news article.
</Callout>

Suppression is slower than removal, but it is available to almost everyone, and it does not require the other party's cooperation. That is why it is the default playbook.

## The content and PR toolkit

Modern ORM blends four disciplines that used to live in separate departments: SEO, content strategy, digital PR, and review management. Each one earns you real estate on page one.

- **Owned content**: a company blog, leadership bios, and case studies, all optimized for the searches that matter most (your name, your founder's name, "[brand] reviews").
- **Earned media**: guest posts, expert interviews, and press releases on reputable industry sites, which rank high because the hosting domain already has authority.
- **Social profiles**: LinkedIn, X, Facebook, and YouTube pages naturally rank well for brand-name searches because search engines trust established, high-authority domains.
- **Review platforms**: active, responded-to profiles on Google Business, Glassdoor, or industry-specific review sites, which Google treats as authoritative "about this brand" sources.

Each of these is a separate ranking slot you can claim before anyone else does. The brands that win page one usually aren't fighting fires, they filled the shelf space years earlier.

## A practical workflow

Most ORM engagements follow the same sequence, whether it is an agency or an in-house comms team running it.

<Mermaid chart={`graph TD;
  A[Audit current SERP\nfor brand name] --> B[Identify damaging or\noutdated results];
  B --> C[Build owned and earned\ncontent to outrank them];
  C --> D[Distribute via PR,\nguest posts, social];
  D --> E[Monitor rankings\nand sentiment monthly];
  E --> B;

The loop back from monitoring to identification matters. New reviews, news mentions, and AI-generated summaries appear constantly, so ORM is never a one-time project.

<Callout type="warning">
Never respond to a negative result with fake reviews, paid link farms, or cloaked pages. Google's spam guidelines penalize manipulative link schemes, and getting caught can tank your entire domain's ranking, turning a page-3 problem into a page-1 disaster.
</Callout>

## The AI search wrinkle

The newest complication: generative tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude are increasingly where people research a company before they ever open Google. These tools summarize across sources rather than ranking links, so a single outdated Wikipedia edit or an old lawsuit article can get baked into an AI-generated answer with no "page two" to bury it on.

Comms teams are responding by making sure the same E-A-T signals, updated bios, clear ownership pages, recent press, exist wherever these models pull from, since suppression logic in classic search does not map cleanly onto how an AI answer gets generated.

<Callout type="tip">
Set a quarterly habit: search your brand name in Google, in an AI chat tool, and on the top two review sites for your industry. Screenshot what you see. That log becomes your evidence for whether ORM efforts are actually moving the needle.
</Callout>

Reputation online is never "finished." It is maintained, the same way a garden is maintained, one season of new content at a time.
