# Media Relations: Building Real Journalist Relationships

Most PR fails for one reason: it treats journalists like a mailing list instead of people with a beat, a deadline, and a memory of who wasted their time.

## Quick Summary

- Media relations is the ongoing relationship you build with reporters before you ever need coverage, not the transaction of sending a pitch.
- [Journalist response rates are falling industry-wide](https://muckrack.com/resources/research/state-of-journalism) even as pitch volume climbs, which makes existing relationships more valuable, not less.
- Offering value that is not about you, expert quotes, exclusive data, story tips, is how you earn a reporter's attention bank.
- Researching a reporter's actual beat before reaching out is the single highest-leverage habit in PR.
- Mass-blasting irrelevant pitches and over-following-up are the two fastest ways to get filtered straight to spam.

## What Media Relations Actually Means

Media relations is the discipline of knowing reporters as people who cover specific topics, have specific editors, and specific deadlines. Pitching is one moment inside that relationship.

Think of it like networking, not advertising. You do not cold-email a stranger asking for a favor and expect a warm reply; you build familiarity first.

The reporters who cover your industry are finite. Once you burn a relationship with a bad pitch, that reporter often remembers your name for years.

<Callout type="info">
The goal is not "getting a hit this quarter." The goal is becoming a source a reporter thinks of unprompted when their editor asks for a story on your topic.
</Callout>

## Research the Beat Before You Reach Out

A reporter's beat is the specific topic or industry they are assigned to cover, and it is narrower than most PR people assume. "Tech reporter" might actually mean "enterprise SaaS funding rounds," not your consumer app launch.

Read their last 10 articles before writing a single word to them. Note the angle they take, the sources they quote, and the outlets that syndicate their work.

[86% of journalists say they ignore pitches outside their beat entirely](https://amworldgroup.com/statistics/media-relations-statistics), which means beat research is not a nice-to-have, it is the first filter your pitch has to pass.

Look for patterns in what they have already covered:

- Do they favor data-driven stories, or personal narratives?
- Do they quote founders directly, or paraphrase from press releases?
- What outlets and platforms do they publish to, beyond the flagship site?

That 15 minutes of research is what separates "another PR person" from "someone who actually reads my work."

## Offer Value Beyond Your Own Story

The fastest way to build a real relationship is to be useful when you have nothing to gain. Reporters remember sources who helped them on a story that had nothing to do with your company.

Three ways to give value first:

- **Expert commentary.** Offer your founder or analyst as a quote source for a story the reporter is already writing, unrelated to your launch.
- **Exclusive data.** Share an early look at proprietary survey results or usage data before you publish it publicly.
- **Story tips.** Flag a trend or a competitor move you noticed, even one that does not flatter you, if it is genuinely useful to their beat.

<Callout type="tip">
Set a personal rule: for every pitch you send about your own news, send at least one message that offers something with zero ask attached. That ratio is what turns a contact into a relationship.
</Callout>

Reporters who trust you as a source will often reach out when they need a quote fast, on their timeline, not yours. That is the payoff of showing up before you need anything.

## The Etiquette That Burns Bridges

Two habits destroy media relationships faster than anything else, and both are avoidable.

**Mass-blasting irrelevant pitches.** Sending the same generic pitch to 200 reporters regardless of beat signals you never researched any of them. It also makes the next, relevant pitch from your name easy to ignore.

**Following up too aggressively.** [The most effective cadence is one or two follow-ups](https://muckrack.com/resources/research/state-of-journalism), spaced three to six days apart, not a daily nudge until they reply. A reporter's silence is often "not now," not "convince me harder."

<Callout type="warning">
If a reporter asks to be removed from your list, do it immediately and permanently. Ignoring that request is the single fastest way to get blocked and talked about in reporter Slack channels and group chats.
</Callout>

Other etiquette that matters just as much: respect embargoes exactly as stated, never pitch a story a competitor outlet already ran, and always credit a reporter's prior coverage when referencing it in a new pitch.

## Key Takeaways

- Media relations is the relationship, pitching is one moment inside it.
- Read a reporter's last 10 articles before contacting them; beat mismatch is the top reason pitches get ignored.
- Give value with no ask attached, expert quotes, early data, honest tips, before you need coverage.
- Never mass-blast, and cap follow-ups at one or two, spaced several days apart.
- Honor opt-out requests instantly. A burned relationship rarely comes back.
