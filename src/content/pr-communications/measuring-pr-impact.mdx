# Measuring PR Impact: Beyond Vanity Metrics

A stack of press clips feels like proof PR is working. It rarely tells you whether any of it moved the business.

## Quick Summary

- Clip counts and "reach" numbers are vanity metrics, they count exposure, not whether anyone read, believed, or acted on the coverage.
- [AMEC's Barcelona Principles, the recognized gold standard for PR measurement, explicitly reject Advertising Value Equivalency (AVE) as valid](https://www.prezly.com/academy/advertising-value-equivalency).
- Share of voice tells you how much of the conversation you own versus competitors, but only means something alongside sentiment and context.
- Referral traffic and conversions from coverage connect PR to revenue, the metric executives actually care about.
- Message pull-through checks whether coverage said what you wanted said, not just that it mentioned your name.

## Why Clip Counts And Reach Lie

"Reach" is usually a publication's total readership or a site's total monthly visitors, not the number of people who saw your specific mention. A story in an outlet with 5 million monthly readers does not mean 5 million people read about you, most never scroll past the homepage.

Advertising Value Equivalency (AVE), converting column inches into "equivalent ad spend", compounds the problem by pricing a mention as if it were a paid ad, ignoring that readers trust editorial coverage differently than ads and that the number is fabricated math on top of an already-inflated reach figure.

<Callout type="warning">
[The Barcelona Principles reject AVE outright](https://www.prezly.com/academy/advertising-value-equivalency): it excludes qualitative context, cannot be benchmarked consistently across outlets, and tells you nothing about whether the coverage changed anyone's mind.
</Callout>

Clip counts fail the same way. Ten mentions in irrelevant blogs are not worth one mention in the trade publication your buyers actually read. Volume without relevance is noise dressed up as a result.

## Share Of Voice, With Context

Share of voice (SOV) measures what percentage of the total conversation in your category belongs to you versus competitors, calculated as your mentions divided by total category mentions. [Modern SOV analysis treats the number as a relevance indicator, not a scoreboard](https://www.talkwalker.com/blog/measure-share-voice), it shows how present you are in conversations that matter, not whether you are winning them.

That distinction matters because SOV can rise for bad reasons, a product recall generates plenty of mentions too. This is why SOV must always travel paired with **sentiment analysis**: are those mentions positive, neutral, or negative, and is the ratio shifting over time?

Track SOV monthly against your two or three closest competitors, not the whole market. A rising share against the two companies your prospects actually compare you to is a real signal; a rising share against an unranked long tail of tiny players is not.

## Referral Traffic, Conversions, And Message Pull-Through

This is where PR earns a seat at the revenue table. Tag every placement's backlink with UTM parameters, then pull referral sessions, conversions, and (where trackable) pipeline influenced straight from analytics and the CRM.

[Modern PR measurement increasingly leans on UTM tracking, CRM integration, and tiered dashboards to connect coverage directly to business outcomes](https://www.adverity.com/blog/measuring-the-impact-of-pr-vanity-vs.-sanity-metrics), the same discipline paid marketing has used for years, now applied to earned media.

The final check is **message pull-through**: did the journalist actually include your key message, your product name, your differentiator, or did they run the story and leave your point on the cutting room floor? Read every piece of coverage and score it against the three messages you briefed. A placement that mentions your company but omits your core point earned you visibility, not persuasion.

<Callout type="tip">
Build a simple scorecard per placement: outlet relevance, sentiment, message pull-through (yes/partial/no), and any tracked referral action. Four data points beat one inflated reach number every time.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Retire AVE and raw reach as headline metrics, the Barcelona Principles reject AVE for good reason.
- Track share of voice against your real competitive set, always paired with sentiment.
- Use UTM tagging and CRM data to connect coverage to referral traffic and conversions.
- Score message pull-through on every placement, a mention is not the same as a persuaded reader.
- Report a scorecard, not a clip count, to show PR's actual contribution to the business.
