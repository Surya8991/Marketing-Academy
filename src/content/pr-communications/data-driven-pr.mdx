Most pitches get ignored because they ask for coverage instead of offering something new. A number nobody has seen before changes that math entirely.

Data-driven PR means building your pitch around original research, an internal dataset, or a survey you commissioned, rather than a company announcement. According to a 2023 Muck Rack survey, 78% of journalists say they are more likely to cover a story if it includes proprietary data. That is not a small edge, it is the difference between a pitch that gets opened and one that gets deleted.

<Callout type="info">
Harvard Business Review research found original research is roughly 2.5x more likely to earn media coverage than a standard company announcement. Numbers travel further than adjectives.
</Callout>

## Why Journalists Want Your Data

Reporters are not chasing your product launch, they are chasing a story their editor will approve. Original data gives them a hook: a trend, a surprise, a number that contradicts what everyone assumes.

Journalists surveyed on pitch preferences rank original research and market data second only to press releases as the content they most want from PR contacts. That means a well-built dataset can outperform almost anything else in your outreach toolkit.

- A surprising stat gives a reporter a headline before they even open your full pitch.
- A dataset lets them build their own angle instead of reprinting yours.
- A named methodology signals the numbers are real, not marketing spin.

Give a journalist a reason to write their own story, and they will write it faster than one you hand them fully formed.

## Finding the Story Inside Your Data

You do not need a research department. You need one finding that is either surprising, timely, or contrarian.

Look at data you already have: product usage logs, support tickets, sales trends, or customer surveys. If nothing jumps out, commission a short survey through a panel tool like Pollfish or SurveyMonkey Audience, 300 to 500 respondents is often enough for a credible top-line stat.

<Callout type="tip">
Ask yourself: "Would this number make someone pause while scrolling?" If the answer is no, keep digging until you find one that does.
</Callout>

Test your top finding against a simple filter before you build anything else around it.

- Does it challenge a common assumption in your industry?
- Does it connect to something already in the news cycle?
- Can you express it in one sentence without jargon?

If your finding passes that filter, you have the seed of a real pitch, not just a chart.

## Packaging the Data for Reporters

The strongest data pitches lead with the finding, not the methodology. Open with the number, then explain how you got it.

Data journalists prioritize stories backed by original research that reveals a new trend, and they often want to verify or extend it themselves. That means your pitch should include more than a headline stat.

- A one-line summary of the single most striking finding.
- A short methodology note: sample size, dates, and data source.
- A clean CSV or spreadsheet, not just a locked PDF chart.
- One visual, a simple chart, that a reporter could use as-is or redraw.

Reporters who can independently check your numbers trust them more, and trust converts into a byline with your brand's name attached.

## Writing the Pitch Itself

Even great data dies in a bloated email. The ideal word count for a successful pitch is around 200 words, and pitches under that length see the highest reply rates.

Lead with the number in your subject line or first sentence, not your company name. Save the "who we are" paragraph for the second half, if you need it at all.

<Callout type="example">
Subject: "1 in 3 marketers admit they've never tested their own landing pages"

Opening line: "A new survey of 412 marketers found that 34% have never run an A/B test on their highest-traffic landing page, despite 89% saying they believe in testing."
</Callout>

That single sentence does three jobs: it states the stat, gives the sample size, and hints at the contradiction that makes it newsworthy. Keep every pitch you send held to that same standard.

## Timing and Distribution

A great dataset with bad timing still gets buried. Pitch during a slow news week when possible, and tie your release to a relevant industry moment if one exists, an earnings season, a regulatory change, or an annual report cycle competitors also cover.

Send embargoed data to two or three priority reporters a day or two before wider distribution, giving them a reason to publish first. Everyone else gets it on the public release date.

<Callout type="warning">
Never send the same embargoed exclusive to multiple competing outlets at once. Breaking an embargo agreement, even once, can quietly end future access to that reporter.
</Callout>

Once your data has run once, it is not finished, it is a template you can repeat quarterly with new numbers.

## Measuring What Worked

Track which finding got picked up, which outlet ran it, and what headline they chose, that pattern tells you what to research next. Digital PR analysis shows data-led campaigns made up nearly 40% of all articles analyzed in one 2025 UK media study, roughly twice the share earned by mapped or generic survey campaigns.

Build a simple log after each campaign: the stat used, the pitch angle, the outlets that responded, and the coverage tone. Over three or four campaigns, that log becomes your playbook for exactly which numbers your industry's reporters actually want.
