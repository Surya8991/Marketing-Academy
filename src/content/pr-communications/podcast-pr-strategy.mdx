Podcasts solved a problem PR had for decades: how do you let an audience hear an executive think out loud for 40 minutes instead of reading a 200-word soundbite?

That's why podcast PR has become its own discipline rather than a side project for the media relations team. It splits into two very different jobs: getting your executives booked as guests on other people's shows, and deciding whether your company should run its own show at all. Confusing the two leads to wasted budget on one or the other.

## Guesting: the actual goal is trust, not reach

Getting an executive booked on a podcast isn't a numbers game the way a press hit sometimes is. A listener who hears your CEO reason through a hard problem for 35 minutes trusts them differently than someone who reads a 4-line quote in a trade article, because long-form audio strips away the polish and lets expertise show.

That's showing up in B2B results: 2025 data shows executives and buyers listening to industry shows, trusting expert guests, and acting on what they hear, with podcast guesting now reaching buyers earlier and more credibly than many paid channels. One documented B2B podcast program reported 94 C-level executives engaged and 43 sales opportunities tied back to podcast activity, which is the kind of pipeline attribution PR rarely gets to claim.

**Booking process that actually works:**

- **Build a target list, not a wishlist.** Filter by actual audience overlap with your buyer, not just download numbers, a niche 3,000-listener show full of your ICP beats a general 50,000-listener show full of the wrong people.
- **Pitch the host's show, not your company.** Reference specific past episodes, name a guest whose segment you liked, and propose a topic angle that fits their format, generic "we'd love to have our CEO on" pitches get ignored.
- **Prep the executive like a media interview, not a webinar.** Give them 2-3 stories with specific numbers they can tell, not talking points, because podcasts reward narrative and punish scripted answers.
- **Track it as a program, not one-off wins.** A single podcast hit is a nice mention, a steady cadence of 2-3 relevant placements a month builds the recognition that actually shifts how buyers see the executive.

<Callout type="tip">
Booking agencies and in-house teams increasingly measure podcast guesting the same way they'd measure a sales channel, tracking which appearances generated inbound, not just which shows have the biggest audience.
</Callout>

## Launching your own show: a much bigger bet

A branded company podcast is a content commitment, not a PR tactic, and treating it as a quick PR win is the most common reason company podcasts die after eight episodes.

Before greenlighting one, be honest about three things: do you have a genuinely interesting host, not just an available executive, do you have a realistic production budget for editing and distribution, and do you have a topic angle broad enough to sustain 40+ episodes without repeating yourself.

**When a branded podcast is worth it:**

- You have a distinct point of view your competitors don't have, and a podcast is the best format to demonstrate it repeatedly.
- You already have a base of engaged customers or prospects who'd actually subscribe, not just an internal audience of employees.
- Someone in the company is a genuinely compelling interviewer or storyteller, and can commit real time to it weekly or biweekly.

**When it's the wrong move:**

- You're hoping it replaces earned media, it doesn't, a company podcast is owned media and reaches almost nobody outside people who already know you exist.
- Leadership wants it to "go viral," most B2B podcasts plateau at a small, loyal audience, and that's fine if the goal is depth with existing prospects, not reach.
- Nobody on the team can commit to a consistent publishing cadence, an irregular podcast damages credibility more than having no podcast at all.

<Callout type="example">
A common hybrid that works well: launch a modest branded podcast built almost entirely around interviewing customers and industry peers, not your own executives. It's cheaper to produce, builds real relationships with guests who then share the episode, and avoids the "just another company podcast talking about itself" problem.
</Callout>

## Where the two disciplines meet

Executives who guest regularly on other podcasts get much better at the format, which makes hosting a branded show, if you decide to build one, dramatically easier. Run guesting first, for six months, before committing budget to your own show, and you'll know by then whether your organization actually has the appetite and talent for long-form audio at all.

Podcast PR isn't a checkbox next to "we should probably do this." It's a real trust-building channel, but only if you're honest about which half of it, guesting or hosting, fits what your company can actually sustain.
