Every CEO wants to be 'everywhere'. That instinct is exactly how a media tour goes wrong.

An executive media tour is a coordinated campaign that places a company leader on multiple podcasts, panels, and interviews over a set period, usually 3 to 6 months, to build a specific reputation with a specific audience. Done well, it turns one person's expertise into dozens of pieces of durable content. Done poorly, it burns a leader's calendar on shows nobody in your buyer base actually listens to.

<Callout type="info">
Thought leadership budgets rose 53% in 2024, and some high-growth SaaS startups now spend up to $140,000 a year on thought leadership programs, according to industry benchmarking cited by Leverage with Media. Podcasts have become the primary vehicle for that spend.
</Callout>

## Why Podcasts Became the Default Channel

Press releases still matter, but podcasts do something a release cannot: they let an audience hear how a leader thinks, not just what a company announced.

Industry recaps going into 2026 describe podcasting shifting from a nice-to-have into a required part of go-to-market strategy, working alongside PR and social rather than replacing them. The format rewards unscripted, specific answers over polished talking points.

That shift changes who should go on a tour. A leader who can only repeat approved messaging will sound stiff next to a host used to real conversation. Confidence in unscripted answers matters more than podcast fame.

## Pick the Audience Before the Show

The single biggest mistake in executive media tours is optimizing for download counts instead of audience fit.

Booking agencies that work with executives increasingly stress matching shows to the specific listeners you need, not the biggest number available. A niche 2,000-listener show packed with your exact buyer persona beats a 200,000-listener general business show that your prospects don't tune into.

Before you book anything, build a short target list using three filters:

- Does the host's typical guest overlap with your leader's actual expertise?
- Do recent episodes get engagement (comments, shares) from people who look like your buyers?
- Would this placement embarrass or bore your leader if the conversation got specific?

Narrow first, then expand once the tour proves itself.

<Callout type="tip">
Track each show against your actual target account list, not general download or ranking sites. A podcast that reaches three of your top-20 target accounts is worth more than one that reaches thousands of people outside your market.
</Callout>

## Build the Leader, Not Just the Booking List

A media tour lives or dies on prep, and prep is more than a one-page bio.

Give your executive a point of view document: 3 to 5 strong opinions they can defend in any conversation, each with a real example or number behind it. Command Your Brand's guidance on executive booking notes that the most credible thought leadership in 2025 comes from authentic experience and proprietary data, not AI-polished talking points a host has heard a hundred times.

Run a 45-minute mock interview before the first real booking. Ask the hardest, most skeptical question you can think of, then watch whether your leader gets specific or retreats into vague corporate language. Fix that gap before a host does it live.

## Sequence the Tour Like a Campaign

Treat the tour as a rolling campaign with phases, not a scattered list of one-off bookings.

Start with smaller, friendlier shows to build reps and refine the story, then move to higher-profile or harder-audience placements once the pitch is tight. Guidance from PR practitioners frames this the same way: begin with industry podcasts, local business outlets, and conference panels, then graduate to national press and keynotes as the profile grows.

## Turn One Appearance Into Ten Assets

The interview is the most expensive part of a media tour, so treat the recording as raw material, not a finished deliverable.

Long-term booking clients who commit to a steady cadence, roughly 4 shows a month, typically see meaningful results within 6 months and stronger compounding results by 12 months, according to booking-agency benchmarks. That patience only pays off if every episode gets repurposed.

- Pull 3 to 5 short clips for LinkedIn and social
- Write a blog post expanding your leader's strongest answer
- Quote the best soundbite in your next press pitch or newsletter
- Add the episode to your leader's speaker one-sheet as a proof point

<Callout type="success">
A single well-placed podcast appearance, repurposed properly, can outlast a press release by months. Clips, quotes, and blog posts keep surfacing in searches and shares long after the episode airs.
</Callout>

## Measure Reputation, Not Just Reach

Download numbers are the easiest metric to report and the least useful one for judging a media tour.

Track instead: inbound inquiries that mention the episode, sales conversations where a prospect references the interview, and whether target accounts engaged with the content. Those signals tell you if the tour is building the specific reputation you set out to build, not just noise.

Review the tour quarterly against your original audience list. If placements are drifting toward "impressive-sounding" shows that don't touch your buyers, pull the plan back to the filters you started with.
