Launch day looks chaotic from the outside, dozens of articles appearing within minutes of each other. It is not luck.

It is the result of a PR team coordinating journalists for days or weeks before anyone hits publish. This lesson breaks down the three tools that make that coordination possible: embargoes, exclusives, and tiered outreach.

## What an embargo actually is

An embargo is an agreement: you give a journalist your news early, they agree not to publish until a specific date and time you set. It is not a contract, no court enforces it.

It is a handshake built on trust, and that trust is the entire mechanism. Break it once as a journalist and PR teams stop sharing early access with you.

Why bother? Because it gives reporters time to actually do their job, research your product, write a thoughtful piece, maybe record a demo, instead of scrambling to publish a thin post in twenty minutes. The payoff for you is coordinated timing: instead of one story trickling out today and another next week, ten outlets publish within the same hour and the story feels inescapable.

<Callout type="info">
Embargoes are not legally binding. They work because you only extend them to journalists who have a track record of honoring them, and because breaking one publicly costs a reporter future access.
</Callout>

## Exclusive vs embargo: they solve different problems

An exclusive means only one journalist or outlet gets the story first, full stop.

They publish, and only after that do you pitch anyone else. An embargo means many journalists get the same information at the same time, all of them agree to hold until a shared release moment. Mixing these up is a common rookie mistake, and it burns relationships fast.

The rule of thumb PR teams follow: embargoes suit time-sensitive news like product launches, since the value is in one unified moment. Exclusives suit feature stories and narrative pieces, where the value is in depth and a single outlet's unique angle.

One more nuance worth remembering: there is no such thing as an "exclusive to three outlets." If you tell three journalists it is exclusive, you have lied to at least two of them, and they will find out.

## Building your tiered media list

Not every journalist gets the same treatment, and pretending otherwise wastes your best relationships on your least important contacts.

A practical three-tier structure looks like this:

- **Tier 1 (3-5 journalists):** Your most-wanted reporters, personal outreach, early access, maybe even a pre-briefing call days ahead.
- **Tier 2 (10-15 journalists):** Sector reporters who cover your space regularly, a tailored pitch plus the standard embargo.
- **Tier 3 (broad list):** Everyone else, they receive the press release the moment the embargo lifts, no special treatment needed.

This is not about playing favorites for its own sake. It reflects where your limited time and best assets, like an exclusive spokesperson interview, will generate the most coverage.

<Callout type="tip">
Give Tier 1 journalists something Tier 3 never gets: a demo, a direct line to your founder, or first look at data. Save the polished press release for the tier that just needs the facts.
</Callout>

## Timing the embargo window

Most PR practitioners share embargoed materials with key media roughly 7 to 10 days before launch.

That window gives serious journalists enough runway to test a product, schedule an interview, or fact-check claims, but it is short enough that the news still feels fresh when it publishes. Too early and the story goes stale in a reporter's queue, or worse, someone forgets the embargo date entirely.

According to Cision's 2025 State of the Media Report, 57% of journalists say they specifically want to receive exclusives from the PR professionals they work with, a reminder that reporters are not just tolerating this system, many actively prefer it. That preference is exactly why a well-run embargo, paired with a smaller slice of true exclusivity for your top contacts, tends to outperform a single mass press release blasted to everyone at once.

## When embargoes break, and how to recover

Sometimes a journalist publishes early, by accident, by carelessness, or because a competitor's leak forced their hand.

When that happens, do not panic-blast your press release to everyone immediately, that just amplifies the leak. Instead, quietly confirm the story is genuinely out, then make a fast call: either hold the rest of the embargo and accept one outlet jumped the gun, or move the whole window up and notify every journalist on the list at once so nobody else feels blindsided.

<Callout type="warning">
Never publicly call out the journalist who broke the embargo. Handle it privately, note it for future outreach decisions, and keep your energy on protecting the rest of the launch.
</Callout>

The bigger lesson: the fewer people who hold an embargo, and the more trustworthy each one is, the lower your risk. A leak-proof launch is really just a well-chosen list.

Coordinated PR is a skill you build one launch at a time, and every embargo you run cleanly makes the next one easier to pull off.
