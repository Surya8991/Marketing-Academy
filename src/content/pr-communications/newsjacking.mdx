## What Newsjacking Actually Is

Newsjacking is the practice of injecting your brand into a breaking news story to earn attention you could not buy. David Meerman Scott coined the term back in 2011, describing it as hijacking a trending story and adding your own angle before the news cycle moves on.

The window is short, often just hours. A journalist or a founder who reacts fast with something genuinely useful gets quoted; everyone else gets ignored.

<Callout type="info">
Newsjacking is not the same as trend-chasing. Trend-chasing rides a meme for laughs; newsjacking earns media coverage or expert positioning by adding real insight to an active story.
</Callout>

## Why It Works When It Works

Journalists on deadline need sources fast, and a brand with a sharp, on-topic quote is easier to include than one that requires research. In spring 2025, when cyberattacks hit UK retailers Marks & Spencer, Harrods, and Co-op, the ecommerce platform Omnisend moved quickly with expert commentary and landed coverage in The Scotsman and Your Money.

Speed beat size here. Omnisend was not the biggest voice in ecommerce, but it was the fastest with something useful to say.

The same logic applies to consumer confusion, not just crises. When TikTok rumors claimed a new UK law banned GPS apps while driving, MoneySuperMarket combined actual Department for Transport guidance with plain-language advice for drivers. That is newsjacking as public service: it answers a question people are already searching for, right when they are searching for it.

## The Three Newsjacking Lanes

Not every trending story calls for the same response. Picking the wrong lane is where most brands go wrong before they even open their news feed.

- **Expert commentary**: a journalist needs a quote on a breaking story, and you have the relevant expertise.
- **Cultural moment**: a shared, low-stakes event, like a game or an award show, where a witty tie-in fits your brand voice.
- **Service response**: news creates confusion or a practical need, and your brand fills the gap with clear information.

Astronomer's 2025 response to its own viral Coldplay-concert moment fell into cultural moment territory. Rather than staying silent, the company posted a short video featuring Gwyneth Paltrow as a 'very temporary spokesperson,' then redirected the attention toward its actual product message on LinkedIn. Owning the joke on your own terms beats letting the internet write your narrative for you.

Knowing your lane tells you how fast to move and how much risk to accept. Next comes the harder question: should you even be in this story at all?

## Where Newsjacking Turns Into a Crisis

The single biggest failure pattern is treating tragedy as an opportunity. In February 2011, Kenneth Cole tweeted a joke tying its new collection to the Arab Spring protests in Cairo, a moment where people were risking their lives for democracy. The backlash was immediate and was picked up by major news outlets, turning a clumsy tweet into a lasting reputational stain.

DiGiorno made a nearly identical mistake by hijacking the #WhyIStayed hashtag, a tag survivors used to discuss domestic violence, to promote pizza. Multiple brands also tried to newsjack Hurricane Sandy while people were losing their homes.

<Callout type="warning">
If the news involves death, disaster, violence, or serious human suffering, the correct newsjacking move is silence. No stat, pun, or product tie-in is worth being the brand remembered for exploiting a tragedy.
</Callout>

A useful gut check before you post: would this line read fine if a victim of the story saw it? If there is any hesitation, that is your answer.

## A Fast Decision Framework

Speed matters, but a 60-second filter still fits inside the newsjacking window. Run every idea through it before you hit publish.

- **Sensitivity check**: does this story involve real harm or loss? If yes, stop.
- **Relevance check**: does your brand have a genuine, non-forced connection to the topic?
- **Value check**: are you adding information, humor, or utility, or just attaching your logo to attention?
- **Approval check**: has one more set of eyes, ideally legal or a senior comms lead, seen it if the topic is remotely sensitive?

Reactive PR agencies like Cartwright and Umpf built entire practices around exactly this filter, moving on stories like Charlton Athletic's Bluewater ban comments within 24 hours in March 2025 while staying inside brand-safe territory. Fast does not have to mean reckless.

<Callout type="tip">
Keep a lightweight 'newsjacking triage' doc: a single Slack channel where any team member can flag a trending story, and a named decision-maker who can approve or kill it within the hour. Most fails happen because no one owned the call.
</Callout>

Newsjacking rewards brands that are prepared, not just fast. Build the muscle before the next big story breaks, and you will be ready to add value instead of scrambling to explain a mistake.
