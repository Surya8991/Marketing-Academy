# Press Release Writing: The Structure That Gets Picked Up

Only 2 to 3% of press releases result in media pickup. Almost all of the failures come from the same handful of structural mistakes, and they are fixable.

## Quick Summary

- Journalists expect a fixed structure: headline, dateline, lead paragraph, body with quotes, boilerplate.
- The lead paragraph must answer the 5 Ws, who, what, when, where, why, in the first two sentences.
- Releases with a quote from an executive or expert get [about 40% higher pickup rates](https://gitnux.org/press-release-industry-statistics/) than releases without one.
- Most releases get ignored for three reasons: no real news, a buried lede, or promotional language a reporter cannot reuse.
- Industry-targeted releases see [67% higher pickup rates](https://gitnux.org/press-release-industry-statistics/) than releases blasted to a generic list.

## The Structure Journalists Expect

A press release is not an ad. It is a document written so a busy reporter can lift whole sentences straight into their story, so the format is standardized for exactly that purpose.

**Headline:** one sentence, active voice, states the news itself, not a teaser. "Company X Launches Product Y" beats "Company X Announces Exciting New Chapter."

**Dateline:** the city and date the release is issued from, e.g. "SAN FRANCISCO, July 22, 2026". This tells the reporter where the news originates and when it is current.

**Lead paragraph:** two to three sentences answering who, what, when, where, and why. A reporter should be able to read only this paragraph and still understand the entire story.

**Body:** two to four short paragraphs expanding the lead with context, data, and at least one quote.

**Boilerplate:** a fixed one-paragraph "About [Company]" description, reused verbatim across every release, and a media contact line at the very end.

## Why Most Press Releases Get Ignored

Journalists respond to just 3% of the pitches they receive, and the reasons are consistent across every newsroom survey.

**No real news.** A minor product update, a routine hire, or an anniversary is not news to anyone outside the company. If the announcement would not interest a stranger, it is not a release, it is an internal memo.

**Buried lede.** The actual news sits in paragraph three, after two paragraphs of company history and mission-statement language. Reporters do not read that far.

**Too promotional.** Words like "revolutionary," "game-changing," and "industry-leading" signal marketing copy, not news, and get a release filtered straight to the trash by editors trained to spot them.

<Callout type="warning">
If you cannot rewrite your headline as a neutral newspaper headline, with no adjectives a stranger would find suspicious, the release is not ready to send.
</Callout>

Fix all three and pickup odds rise sharply. Releases targeted to the right industry list specifically, rather than blasted broadly, see 67% higher pickup rates because the pitch lands with a reporter who already covers that beat.

## Before and After: A Real Example

**Before (buried, promotional):**

"Acme Corp is thrilled to announce an exciting new milestone in its ongoing mission to revolutionize the industry with its game-changing platform update, which the passionate team has been working tirelessly to deliver to customers everywhere."

Notice the problems: no product name until the imagined paragraph two, no date, no numbers, three adjectives doing the work a fact should do.

**After (5 Ws in the lead):**

"Acme Corp today launched Version 4.0 of its inventory platform, adding real-time stock forecasting for retailers managing more than 10,000 SKUs. The update rolls out globally starting July 22, 2026, and is available at no extra cost to existing customers."

The rewrite names the who (Acme Corp), what (Version 4.0, real-time forecasting), when (July 22, 2026), and the scale (10,000 SKUs) in two sentences. A reporter can publish that sentence with almost no editing.

<Callout type="example">
Add one quote from a named executive addressing why this matters, not restating the announcement, and pickup odds rise by roughly 40%. "Retailers were flying blind on demand spikes; this closes that gap" works. "We're excited to bring this to market" does not.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Follow the fixed structure: headline, dateline, lead paragraph, body with quotes, boilerplate. Reporters expect it and skip releases that deviate.
- Answer the 5 Ws in the first two sentences of the lead paragraph. That is the paragraph most editors actually read.
- Cut promotional adjectives. "Revolutionary" and "game-changing" signal marketing, not news, and get filtered before a human reads further.
- Add a substantive quote from a named person. It lifts pickup rates by roughly 40%.
- Target the release to the right industry outlets instead of a broad blast; the targeted version sees 67% higher pickup.
