Most people think PR means talking to journalists. But the audience that shapes a company's reputation fastest is the one already on payroll: its own employees.

Internal communications, often shortened to 'IC', is the practice of keeping employees informed, aligned, and engaged through deliberate messaging from leadership and the organization. It sits inside the PR or corporate communications function at most companies because the skill set is identical: know your audience, craft a clear message, choose the right channel, and measure whether it landed. Get this wrong, and the damage shows up everywhere else.

<Callout type="info">
Axios HQ's 2025 internal communications report found employees earning $50K-$100K lose over 35 working days a year to poor communication, roughly $10,140 in wasted salary per person. For senior staff above $200K, that jumps to 63 days and $54,860 per person.
</Callout>

## Why PR owns this, not just HR

HR handles policies, benefits, and performance reviews. PR handles narrative: what employees believe about the company, its leadership, and its direction.

Those beliefs leak outward. Employees post on LinkedIn, talk to friends, and answer questions from reporters or investors long before an official statement goes out. A misinformed or disengaged workforce is a reputational risk waiting to happen.

That's why internal communications sits next to media relations and crisis communications on most PR org charts, not in a silo. The company you present to the public is only as credible as the story your own people tell.

## The trust gap you're actually managing

Axios HQ's 2025 data shows a stark perception gap between leaders and employees. 27% of leaders believe staff are fully aligned with business goals, but only 9% of employees agree.

The gap shows up everywhere: 80% of leaders think communications are clear and engaging, but only 50% of employees do. 72% of leaders say updates follow a reliable cadence, and only 48% of employees concur.

This is the core job of internal comms: close that gap with consistency, not more messages. A single well-timed, honest update beats five vague ones.

## Choose channels employees actually use

Not every message needs an all-hands meeting. Match the channel to the message's urgency and depth.

- **Email or newsletter**: still the top choice, 67% of employees prefer it for routine updates, per Axios HQ's 2025 survey.
- **Manager-led conversations**: research consistently ranks direct managers as the most trusted source of workplace information, ahead of the CEO or intranet.
- **All-hands or town halls**: reserve these for major changes, strategy shifts, or moments that need a human face and live Q&A.
- **Intranet or employee app**: good for reference material employees search for later, not breaking news.

<Callout type="tip">
When in doubt, ask: 'Does this need a response, a reaction, or just awareness?' That answer tells you the channel.
</Callout>

Picking the right channel is only half the job. The other half is giving managers something worth repeating.

## Equip managers, don't just inform employees

A striking pattern across recent industry research (PR Daily and Axios HQ both flagged this as a 2025 priority) is that manager communication enablement is now considered a top internal comms priority. Employees trust the person they report to more than any centralized memo.

That means your job as a communicator often isn't writing the final message; it's writing the manager's cheat sheet. Give them three talking points, an anticipated tough question, and a plain-language summary before the news goes wide.

Skip this step, and managers get caught flat-footed in front of their own teams, which erodes trust faster than the original news ever could.

## Measure what actually matters

Sending an email isn't the goal; understanding and belief are. Track open rates and click-throughs, sure, but pair them with pulse surveys asking whether employees feel informed and aligned.

Axios HQ's research found that when employees understand company goals, productivity rises by 63%, motivation by 59%, and satisfaction by 54%. Those are the outcomes worth reporting to leadership, not just email opens.

Treat every message as a loop, not a broadcast: send, listen, adjust, repeat.

## Handling the hard topics

Leaders increasingly expect to navigate uncomfortable subjects internally. Axios HQ found 41% of leaders flag AI adoption as a difficult conversation to have with staff, and 30% cite political issues or return-to-office mandates.

The instinct to stay quiet on hard topics almost always backfires; silence gets filled with rumor. A short, honest 'here's what we know, here's what we don't yet' note beats saying nothing.

That's the internal comms mindset in one line: employees can handle uncertainty, they just can't handle being ignored.
