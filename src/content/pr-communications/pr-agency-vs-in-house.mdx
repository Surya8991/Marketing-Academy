Every founder hits this question eventually: do we hire a PR agency, or build a team ourselves? The honest answer is 'it depends on your stage,' but that is not helpful on its own.

This lesson gives you the actual numbers and a decision framework you can use today.

## What each option actually costs

A PR agency typically runs $3,000 to $5,000 per month for small and mid-sized businesses, according to 2025 industry data from PR.co and Rosica Communications. Larger retainers scale up fast: agency hourly rates range from $100 to $500 per person depending on seniority, and annual agency spend for growing companies often lands between $60,000 and $180,000.

Building in-house is a different shape of cost. A single PR specialist earns a median salary around $70,000, but a real team (manager, specialist, coordinator) costs $180,000 to $350,000 a year once you add benefits, tools, and training, per AMW Group's 2026 cost-benefit analysis. That is often 40-60% more than an equivalent agency retainer.

<Callout type="info">
Agency cost buys you a team. In-house cost buys you one person's full attention, until you hire more.
</Callout>

The sticker price is only half the story. Speed matters just as much.

## Speed: agency vs building from scratch

Agencies bring existing journalist relationships on day one. Most clients see their first placements within 30 to 60 days of signing, because the agency is already talking to reporters who cover your space.

An in-house team has to build those relationships from zero. Industry estimates put that runway at 6 to 12 months before a new in-house hire has real media contacts and pitch credibility.

That gap explains why so many early-stage companies start with an agency, even temporarily. You are renting a Rolodex, not just a skillset.

## When an agency makes more sense

Agencies win when your need is broad, urgent, or spiky rather than constant. A few signals point you toward hiring one:

- You are launching something big (funding round, product launch, rebrand) and need coverage fast.
- Your industry needs specialized reporter relationships you do not have (fintech, healthcare, deep tech).
- You want senior strategic thinking without a senior-level full-time salary.
- Your PR needs fluctuate, heavy this quarter, quiet next quarter.

If you nodded at two or more of these, an agency retainer is probably your faster path to results.

<Callout type="tip">
Ask any agency you are evaluating for three recent placements in outlets that actually matter to your audience, not just a client list. Results, not roster size, tell you what they can do for you.
</Callout>

## When in-house makes more sense

In-house teams win on depth, not speed. They live inside your company, so nothing gets lost in translation.

Consider building in-house when:

- Your product is technical enough that outsiders take months to explain it correctly.
- You need daily availability for crisis response, not a 24-hour agency turnaround.
- Your PR volume is high and steady enough that a dedicated salary beats a retainer on cost per placement.
- Brand voice consistency matters more than breadth of media contacts.

A dedicated in-house person also builds something an agency structurally cannot: institutional memory of every reporter conversation your company has ever had.

## The hybrid model most companies land on

Most mature companies do not pick one lane forever. A hybrid model, one in-house PR lead who owns strategy and brand voice, supported by an agency for media relationships and campaign execution, is what PR.co's 2025 research found delivers the best outcomes for growing companies.

The in-house person becomes the translator: they know the product, the founders, and the roadmap. The agency becomes the amplifier: they know the journalists, the timing, and the pitch angles.

Neither path is permanent. Revisit the decision every 12 months as your company changes shape.

## Making the switch without losing momentum

If you are moving from agency to in-house, or the reverse, protect one thing above all: your existing media relationships. Ask your outgoing agency for a formal handoff document listing every journalist contact, past pitch, and outstanding conversation.

If you are moving from in-house to agency, do the same in reverse: hand the agency your press list, your messaging documents, and a short history of what has worked and what has flopped. A clean handoff is the difference between a smooth transition and starting your media relationships from zero.

<Callout type="warning">
Never let a PR gap open during a transition. Line up the new arrangement before ending the old one, even if it means a short month of overlap cost.
</Callout>

You now have real numbers and a clear framework, not just a gut feeling. Use it to make the call that fits where your company actually is today.
