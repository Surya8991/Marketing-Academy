# Building a Press Kit That Journalists Actually Use

A journalist on deadline gives your company about ninety seconds before deciding whether to dig further or move to the next email. Your press kit is what happens in that window.

## Quick Summary

- A press kit (media kit) is a single, organized home for everything a journalist needs to write about you without emailing you first.
- [78% of journalists say they prefer receiving a press kit over other pitch formats](https://www.g2.com/articles/media-kit), so this is not optional polish, it is baseline expectation.
- The core contents: high-res logos and product images, a boilerplate, executive bios and headshots, a fact sheet, and links to past coverage.
- Host it as a permanent, easy-to-find web page. A PDF buried in an email attachment gets lost the moment the thread does.
- Keep it lean. A press kit crowded with everything you have ever produced is harder to use than one with just the essentials.

## What Actually Belongs In It

Start with visuals, because they get used first. Journalists and editors need your **logo in multiple formats** (PNG for the web, EPS or SVG for print) and **high-resolution product or team photos** they can drop straight into a story without asking permission.

Next comes the **boilerplate**, the tight paragraph that answers "who is this company" in about three sentences. Every press release should end with it, and it should live word-for-word on your kit page too, so nobody misquotes your positioning.

Add **executive bios with headshots**. A reporter writing a founder profile needs a name, title, one line of background, and a photo they can use immediately, not a bio buried in your About page's fourth paragraph.

Round it out with a **fact sheet** (founding year, headcount, funding, key numbers) and a **coverage archive**, links to your best past press mentions. That archive does double duty: it shows other journalists you are a legitimate, previously-covered source, which lowers the bar for them to say yes.

<Callout type="tip">
Organize the page into clearly labeled sections, Logos, Leadership, Facts, Coverage, so a journalist scanning on mobile between meetings can find what they need in seconds, not minutes.
</Callout>

## Where To Host It (And Why PDFs Lose)

The instinct is to make a beautiful PDF. Resist it. A PDF has to be re-sent every time it goes stale, it cannot be linked to a specific section, and it renders badly on the phone a journalist is actually using.

A **simple, permanent page on your own website**, something like `yoursite.com/press`, solves all three problems at once. You update it in place, you can link directly to `/press#leadership` in a pitch email, and it is indexed by search so a journalist googling your company name finds it without asking.

This also matches how the format has evolved. [Media kits today are commonly built as responsive web pages, downloadable PDFs, or shared drive folders](https://foundr.com/articles/marketing/what-is-a-media-kit), but the website version is the only one that stays current without manual re-distribution.

Keep the URL short, put a visible link in your site footer and email signature, and never let it require a login. The entire point is removing friction between a journalist's question and your answer.

<Callout type="info">
A press kit is not a one-time project. Assign someone to refresh headshots, funding numbers, and the coverage archive on a quarterly cadence, stale facts erode the credibility the kit exists to build.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Build the kit around five things: logos and images, boilerplate, executive bios and headshots, a fact sheet, and past coverage links.
- Host it as a permanent website page, not a PDF, so it stays current and is easy to find and link to.
- Organize by clearly labeled section so it works for a journalist skimming on a phone.
- Refresh it quarterly, an out-of-date press kit undermines the trust it is supposed to build.
- Keep it lean, only the essentials a journalist actually needs to write the story.
