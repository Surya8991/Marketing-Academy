# CRM Basics

A CRM (Customer Relationship Management) system is software that stores every interaction you have ever had with a customer or lead in one place. Think of it as a shared memory for your whole business - every email, phone call, purchase, and support ticket, linked to the person who made it. Without a CRM, that information lives scattered across inboxes, spreadsheets, and people's heads.

## Quick Summary

- A CRM stores customer data, tracks every interaction, and connects your sales, marketing, and support teams.
- Businesses that use a CRM see an average ROI of $8.71 for every $1 spent.
- CRM adoption improves customer retention by up to 27% and cuts acquisition costs for 91% of businesses.
- Free tiers from HubSpot and Zoho give you a full-featured CRM with zero upfront cost.
- The biggest mistake is buying a CRM before defining what data you actually need to track.

## Why a CRM Exists (The Problem It Solves)

Imagine your company has 500 customers. Each one has emailed you, bought things at different times, asked different questions, and has different preferences. Without a system:

- A salesperson leaves and takes all their customer notes with them.
- Marketing sends a discount email to someone who complained last week.
- Support has no idea a customer is about to renew - and could have been upsold.

A CRM fixes all three problems. It centralizes data, automates follow-ups, and makes every team member look like they remember everything about every customer.

## The Three Core Jobs a CRM Does

Most beginners think CRM = address book. That is about 10% of what it does. Here are the three real jobs:

### 1. Contact Management

This is the address book part. But instead of just a name and email, a CRM stores:

- Every email thread, call recording, and chat log linked to that person.
- Their company size, industry, and role.
- What they bought, when they bought it, and how much they paid.
- Notes from every meeting.

### 2. Pipeline Management

A pipeline (also called a sales funnel) is the series of steps a lead goes through before becoming a customer - for example: Lead > Demo Scheduled > Proposal Sent > Deal Won. A CRM shows every deal visually, so you instantly know which deals need attention.

### 3. Automation

CRMs send emails automatically based on triggers. For example: if someone downloads your pricing PDF, the CRM can automatically send them a follow-up email 2 days later. No human needed.

## The Main Types of CRM

Not all CRMs are built the same. There are three main types:

| Type | Best For | Example Tools |
|------|----------|---------------|
| **Operational CRM** | Automating sales, marketing, and support workflows | HubSpot, Salesforce |
| **Analytical CRM** | Analyzing customer data to find patterns | Zoho Analytics, Salesforce Einstein |
| **Collaborative CRM** | Sharing customer info across departments | Microsoft Dynamics 365 |

For most beginners, an operational CRM is what they need. Start there.

## Popular CRM Options and Who They Are For

### Free/Cheap Options (Best for Beginners)

- **HubSpot CRM** (free forever plan): Best starting point. Unlimited contacts, unlimited users, built-in email. Scales up as you grow.
- **Zoho CRM** (free up to 3 users): Strong automation on the free tier. Better for small teams.
- **Notion + a CRM template** (free): Not a real CRM, but works for freelancers managing under 50 contacts.

### Mid-Range Options

- **Pipedrive** (from $14/user/month): Visual pipeline, very easy to learn. Built for salespeople.
- **ActiveCampaign** (from $15/month): Best when email marketing automation is your main use case.

### Enterprise Options

- **Salesforce** (from $25/user/month): Most powerful, most complex. Used by large companies with dedicated admin staff.
- **Microsoft Dynamics 365**: Best if your team is already inside the Microsoft ecosystem.

<Callout type="warning">
**Common Beginner Mistakes**

- **Buying before defining needs.** Salesforce can do everything, but most small businesses need 5% of its features. Start free.
- **Importing dirty data.** Importing 5,000 contacts with missing emails, duplicate names, and wrong fields makes your CRM useless immediately. Clean the spreadsheet first.
- **Skipping the pipeline setup.** A CRM without a defined pipeline is just an expensive address book. Map out your sales stages before you import anything.
- **Treating CRM as a one-time setup.** A CRM only stays useful if your team logs every call, email, and meeting in it. Discipline matters more than features.
- **Adding too many fields.** Every extra field is a field your team might not fill in. Start with 5 fields maximum.
</Callout>

## How to Set Up Your First CRM in One Day

This is a practical checklist for someone starting from zero:

**Morning (2 hours): Choose and Configure**
1. Sign up for HubSpot Free (no credit card required).
2. Set up your pipeline stages (example: New Lead, Contacted, Proposal Sent, Won, Lost).
3. Add 5 custom properties (fields) you actually care about - for example: Budget, Decision Timeline, How They Found You.

**Afternoon (2 hours): Import and Connect**
4. Export your existing contacts from Gmail or a spreadsheet as a CSV file (Comma-Separated Values - a format every CRM accepts).
5. Map the CSV columns to CRM fields (Name maps to Name, Email maps to Email, etc.).
6. Connect your email inbox so every sent/received email is logged automatically.

**End of Day (1 hour): Automate One Thing**
7. Create one automation: when a new contact is added, send them a welcome email automatically.

That one automation alone typically saves 30 minutes per day for a small sales team.
