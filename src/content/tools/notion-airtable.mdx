# Notion vs Airtable for Marketers

Every marketing team eventually hits the same wall. Tasks live in email threads. The content calendar is a spreadsheet no one updates. Campaign briefs are buried in Slack. Someone starts a Google Doc, six people edit it, and now there are four versions. Notion and Airtable both promise to fix this problem, but they solve it in completely different ways. As of 2026, Notion has over 100 million users worldwide and an estimated $600 million in annual revenue. Airtable is used by over 450,000 organizations globally, including Nike, Netflix, and Spotify. Knowing which tool to pick, or how to use both, is now a core marketing skill.

## Quick Summary

- Notion is a flexible writing and knowledge workspace; Airtable is a structured database with spreadsheet power
- Use Notion for docs, wikis, meeting notes, content drafts, and team knowledge bases
- Use Airtable for campaign tracking, editorial calendars, asset libraries, and anything requiring filters, formulas, or automation
- Around 75% of Fortune 500 companies use Notion; Airtable is trusted by Nike, Netflix, and Spotify for structured operations
- Most mature marketing teams use both: Notion for "thinking and writing," Airtable for "tracking and doing"

## What Is Notion?

Notion is an all-in-one workspace that lets you write, organize, and collaborate in one place. Think of it as Google Docs meets a wiki meets a simple project manager.

Every page in Notion is a flexible canvas. You can type like a document, add a table, embed a video, create a checklist, or link to another page. The building block is literally called a "block", each paragraph, heading, image, or table is a block you can drag, reorder, or nest.

Key Notion vocabulary:

- **Page**: the base unit in Notion. A page can be a doc, a wiki article, a meeting note, or a database.
- **Database**: a Notion page that holds structured data in rows and columns. Can be displayed as a table, board (Kanban), calendar, gallery, or list.
- **Property**: a column in a Notion database (text, date, select, checkbox, relation, formula).
- **Template**: a pre-built page structure you can copy and reuse for recurring needs like campaign briefs.
- **Workspace**: your team's shared environment. Everything lives here.

<Callout type="info">
Notion's free plan supports unlimited blocks for individuals. The Plus plan ($10 per user per month billed annually, as of 2026) adds unlimited file uploads, version history, and better permission controls, the threshold where most marketing teams upgrade. Over 12% of Notion's user base works in marketing, making it the third-largest industry segment on the platform.
</Callout>

## What Is Airtable?

Airtable is a relational database that looks and feels like a spreadsheet. If you know how to use Excel or Google Sheets, you already understand 70% of Airtable.

The key difference from a spreadsheet: Airtable lets you link rows across different tables. A campaign record in one table can be linked to all its related assets in another table, and to the team members assigned in a third. This is called a relational database, relationships between records.

Key Airtable vocabulary:

- **Base**: one Airtable database. A marketing team might have one base per brand, quarter, or function.
- **Table**: a sheet inside a base. Similar to a tab in Excel. Example: one table for Campaigns, one for Assets, one for Influencers.
- **Record**: one row in a table. One campaign brief is one record.
- **Field**: one column in a table. Fields have types: text, number, date, attachment, linked record, formula, rollup.
- **View**: how you display the same data: grid (spreadsheet), gallery, Kanban, calendar, Gantt, or form.
- **Automation**: a rule that triggers an action. "When a record's status changes to 'Approved,' send an email to the designer."

<Callout type="info">
Airtable's free plan supports unlimited bases with up to 1,000 records per base and 2GB of attachments. The Team plan ($20 per user per month billed annually, as of 2026) removes record limits and unlocks automations, which is where Airtable's power for marketing teams truly starts.
</Callout>

## The Core Difference (In Plain English)

Here is the simplest way to think about it:

- **Notion** is great when the value lives in the writing, the context, and the thinking. Docs, wikis, briefs, retrospectives, knowledge bases.
- **Airtable** is great when the value lives in the data, the relationships, and the tracking. Calendars, pipelines, asset libraries, campaign trackers.

Ask yourself this question: "Does this need to be searched and filtered by 10 people, or does it need to be read and understood by 10 people?"

If it needs to be filtered and queried, use Airtable. If it needs to be read and referenced, use Notion.

## Where Notion Wins for Marketing

### 1. Content Briefs and Campaign Docs

Notion is the best place to write a content brief. You can embed images, link to research, add checklists, leave comments inline, and have your editor respond in the same document. No version control chaos. No emailing drafts back and forth.

### 2. Team Wiki and Brand Guidelines

Where do new hires learn how your brand speaks, what your ICP (ideal customer profile, the description of your best customer) looks like, or how you write subject lines? In most teams: nowhere. A Notion wiki fixes this. Every page can link to related pages, building a knowledge graph your whole team can navigate.

### 3. Meeting Notes and Decisions

Notion's page-based structure is perfect for recurring meeting notes. Create one page per meeting, use a template, and over time you build a searchable archive of every decision your team has ever made.

### 4. Personal Task Management

Notion's individual databases, boards, and checklists work well for personal task tracking. For team-wide project management with dependencies, Asana or Linear are better, but for "my tasks this week," Notion is more than enough.

---

## Where Airtable Wins for Marketing

### 1. Editorial Calendar

An editorial calendar in Airtable is genuinely better than any spreadsheet or doc-based version. Each piece of content is one record. Attach the draft, link to the assigned writer, set a publish date, mark a status, and filter by channel. Switch to calendar view to see what publishes when. Switch to Kanban to see what is in progress.

### 2. Campaign Tracker

Track every live campaign in a single base: name, channel, budget, spend-to-date, start/end date, status, and linked creative assets. Airtable's formula and rollup fields mean you can see total budget across all campaigns automatically, without a pivot table.

### 3. Influencer or Partner Database

Building a list of influencers, media contacts, or co-marketing partners? Airtable handles this far better than a spreadsheet. You can link each contact to the campaigns they participated in, track their follower count, last outreach date, and deal value, all in one place.

### 4. Content Asset Library

Store every creative asset (ad images, video scripts, landing page copy) as records in Airtable. Add the actual file as an attachment field. Now every asset is searchable, tagged by campaign and channel, and linked to the brief that spawned it. No more digging through Google Drive folders.

## How to Use Notion and Airtable Together

The best marketing teams do not choose one. They use both, with a clear rule for what lives where.

  B -->|"Brief approved,\nstatus updated in Airtable"| C["Writer\nstarts draft"]
  C -->|"Draft lives\nin Notion"| D["Editor reviews\nin Notion"]

The workflow above takes about one afternoon to set up. Once it is running, you have a publishing pipeline where nothing falls through a crack.

**The rule of thumb:** If you are creating something (writing, designing, thinking), it lives in Notion. If you are tracking something (status, date, budget, person), it lives in Airtable.

## Getting Started: A Practical Checklist

If you are new to both tools, here is where to begin.

**Week 1, Start with Notion (it is easier):**
- Sign up for free at notion.so
- Create a "Team Wiki" page and add your brand voice guidelines
- Create a "Meeting Notes" database with a repeating template
- Create one "Content Brief" template your whole team uses

**Week 2, Add Airtable for tracking:**
- Sign up for free at airtable.com
- Import your content calendar from Google Sheets (Airtable has a one-click import)
- Switch to Calendar view to see your publishing schedule visually
- Set up one automation: when status changes to "Ready to Publish," send an email or Slack message

**Month 2, Connect them:**
- Paste Notion brief URLs into Airtable records as a URL field
- Use Zapier or Airtable automations to notify your team when statuses change
- Add a "Review Checklist" inside each Notion brief so nothing ships unreviewed

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake: trying to do everything in one tool. Teams that force all their content writing into Airtable end up with flat, hard-to-navigate text. Teams that try to track campaign status in Notion end up with a messy database that nobody maintains. Use each tool for what it is actually designed for.
</Callout>
