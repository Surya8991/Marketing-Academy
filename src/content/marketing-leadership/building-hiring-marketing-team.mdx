# Building and Hiring a Marketing Team

Most founders hire marketing backwards: a specialist before a generalist, a VP before there's anything to lead. Sequencing the hire matters more than the headcount.

## Quick Summary

- The first marketing hire at an early-stage company should be a generalist who can own strategy and execution together, not a narrow specialist, [most seed-stage teams need a T-shaped marketer comfortable with ambiguity](https://www.vendep.com/post/nailing-your-first-marketing-hire-in-b2b-saas) over someone who only does one channel well.
- [Typical 2026 base compensation for that first marketing hire runs $180k-$275k plus meaningful equity](https://foundersnetwork.com/first-marketing-hire-startup/), and most companies make the hire post-product-market fit, not before.
- Job descriptions that attract the right candidates describe outcomes and problems to solve, not a checklist of tools and years of experience.
- [Structured interviews are the strongest standalone predictor of job performance, with roughly twice the predictive validity of unstructured interviews](https://www.pin.com/blog/structured-interviews-guide/), and they cut hiring bias by more than 60%.
- Marketing-specific evaluation, portfolio reviews and live exercises, catches what a resume and a conversation cannot: whether someone can actually do the work, not just talk about it.

## Sequencing Hires as You Scale

The mistake is hiring in the order roles sound important, not the order the business needs them. Sequence should follow what's actually blocking growth.

**Pre-product-market fit (0-1 marketer).** Don't hire yet if you can avoid it. A fractional CMO or the founder doing marketing directly keeps the function cheap and experimental while positioning is still shifting monthly.

**Early stage, first hire.** [The strongest pattern is a growth or demand-generation generalist who can build a measurable pipeline and prove what's actually driving growth](https://productiveshop.com/first-marketing-hire/), before you commit to specialist headcount in any one channel. This person should be comfortable writing copy, running ads, and reading the analytics, not just one of the three.

**10-30 people, second and third hires.** Hire toward whatever channel proved itself in the generalist's first six months. If content drove pipeline, hire a content specialist next; if paid search did, hire that instead. Let evidence pick the next role, not a template org chart.

**30+ people, specialist and leadership layer.** This is where dedicated product marketing, marketing ops, and a people-manager layer start to earn their cost, because coordination overhead now exceeds what one generalist can absorb alone.

## Writing Job Descriptions That Attract the Right People

A job description full of buzzwords and a ten-bullet tool checklist filters for people who are good at reading job descriptions, not people who are good at marketing.

Lead with the problem, not the title. "We need someone to figure out why trial-to-paid conversion is stuck at 8%" attracts a different, better candidate than "Growth Marketing Manager, 5+ years experience."

Cut the requirements list to what's actually non-negotiable. Every extra "nice to have" bullet quietly discourages qualified candidates, especially ones from underrepresented backgrounds, who self-select out when they don't match every line.

<Callout type="tip">
Add one line near the top describing what a successful first 90 days looks like in this role. Candidates who read that line and get excited are self-selecting for fit; candidates who don't respond to it were never going to be a good match anyway.
</Callout>

## Interview Techniques That Actually Predict Performance

Generic behavioral interviews ("tell me about a time you showed leadership") are weak signal for marketing roles because they test storytelling, not marketing skill.

**Structured interviews beat unstructured ones by a wide margin.** [Validity research puts structured interviews at roughly .51 versus .38 for unstructured, and bias effects shrink by more than 60% when every candidate gets the same questions in the same order](https://www.pin.com/blog/structured-interviews-guide/). Write your question set before the first interview and use it for every candidate.

**Portfolio review beats resume review.** Ask candidates to walk through one real campaign, page, or piece of content they own, and probe the decisions behind it: why this headline, why this channel, what didn't work and how they knew. A strong portfolio walkthrough reveals judgment; a strong resume just reveals what they were near.

**Live exercises beat hypotheticals.** Give a take-home or in-interview exercise close to the actual job: write three subject lines for a real product, critique an underperforming landing page, or sketch a launch plan from a one-paragraph brief. Watching someone reason through real constraints predicts on-the-job output far better than asking them to describe how they'd theoretically approach it.

<Callout type="example">
A practical loop for a content marketing hire: (1) structured behavioral questions on collaboration and feedback, identical for every candidate, (2) a 20-minute portfolio walkthrough of one piece they're proud of, (3) a 45-minute live exercise rewriting a real underperforming page from your own site. Three different lenses on the same candidate, each catching something the others miss.
</Callout>

Keep the exercise short and paid if it takes more than an hour. Unpaid, lengthy take-homes filter out senior candidates who have other options, exactly the people you want most.

## Key Takeaways

- Sequence hires by evidence: generalist first, then specialize toward whatever channel proved itself.
- The first marketing hire commands real compensation, $180k-$275k base in 2026, so budget for it seriously rather than treating it as an entry-level role.
- Write job descriptions around the problem to solve and a 90-day success picture, not a buzzword checklist.
- Structured interviews are roughly twice as predictive as unstructured ones and meaningfully reduce bias, use the same question set for every candidate.
- Portfolio reviews and live exercises reveal judgment and real output; generic behavioral questions mostly reveal storytelling ability.
