# Succession Planning for Marketing Leaders

The marketing leader who is impossible to replace is, counterintuitively, the marketing leader least likely to get promoted. If nobody on your team can step into your seat, you are the one stuck holding it.

## Quick Summary

- A record 1,235 CEOs left their roles in the first half of 2025 alone, and a third of their replacements were interim leaders, up sharply from just 9% in 2024, a sign that leadership transitions are happening faster and less predictably than before.
- Only 26% of organizations treat succession planning as a genuine top priority, meaning most marketing leaders have no formal plan for who follows them.
- Clear, visible career paths are one of the strongest levers for retaining high-potential people and keeping them motivated to grow into bigger roles.
- Companies building leadership continuity now, developing people and documenting processes ahead of need, are the ones best positioned to avoid stalling when a leader eventually moves on.
- Your own promotability is directly tied to how "un-stuck" your departure would leave the team, not how essential you personally feel day to day.

## Why "Irreplaceable" Is a Trap, Not a Compliment

It feels good to be the person everyone needs. It also means the moment a bigger opportunity opens up, above you or elsewhere, your own leadership quietly hesitates to move you, because moving you creates a visible gap.

Think about the last major promotion decision you watched happen above you. In most cases, the deciding factor was not just "is this person great," it was "can the org survive them leaving this seat." Leaders who cannot answer that question favorably get passed over even when they are the strongest candidate on paper.

<Callout type="info">
1,235 CEOs departed in just the first half of 2025, with a third replaced by interim leaders rather than a ready internal successor, up from only 9% the year before. That volatility is trickling down: boards and CEOs increasingly expect every senior leader, marketing included, to have a credible next-in-line.
</Callout>

Building succession is not about making yourself dispensable for its own sake. It is about proving you can build systems and people, which is precisely the skill the next level up actually requires.

## What Real Succession Planning Looks Like in Marketing

Succession planning is not a single document you fill out once a year for HR. It is an ongoing practice with three concrete parts.

**Documented decision-making, not tribal knowledge.** Write down how you actually make budget calls, how you evaluate agency partners, how you decide what gets killed versus funded. If that logic only lives in your head, nobody can run it without you, including for a two-week vacation, let alone a permanent transition.

**A visible number two, or two candidates in tension.** Identify who on your team could plausibly step up, and give them real exposure: let them run the board update once, own a budget line, represent marketing in a cross-functional meeting you'd normally attend. Clear career paths that show a visible trajectory are one of the strongest tools for keeping high-potential people motivated and in place.

<Callout type="tip">
Rotate who presents to leadership, not just for succession's sake, but because it is also one of the fastest ways to develop a direct report's executive presence. Both goals are served by the same habit.
</Callout>

**Regular, honest talent conversations with your own boss.** Do not wait for an annual review cycle to name who you see as ready for more. Proactively flagging "here's who could grow into pieces of my role" signals confidence, not insecurity, to leaders who are watching for exactly that signal.

## How This Actually Helps Your Own Career

The connection between succession planning and your own next move is direct, not abstract. Leaders who can point to a documented, functioning team are the ones trusted with bigger scope, because they have already proven the skill that scope requires.

- When a promotion or lateral move opens up, you become a safe "yes" instead of a "we can't lose them from that seat" no.
- A team that runs well without your daily involvement is itself evidence you are ready for a role with a wider span of control.
- Documented processes protect you personally too, if you are out sick, on leave, or the team hits a rough patch, the wheels do not fall off just because you stepped away.
- It changes how your own manager evaluates you, from "how good is their individual output" to "how good is the system they built," which is the actual criteria for the next level.

<Callout type="example">
A VP of Marketing spent a year deliberately handing her Director of Growth increasing ownership of the quarterly board narrative. When she was tapped for a CMO role at a sister company eighteen months later, the board approved her Director's promotion into her old seat within the same meeting, because the transition risk had already been solved in advance.
</Callout>

Building people who can replace you is not a threat to your own trajectory, it is the clearest evidence you are ready for the next one.

## Key Takeaways

- Being personally irreplaceable makes you less promotable, not more, because it creates a gap your own leadership is reluctant to open.
- Document your actual decision-making logic, budget calls, agency evaluation, prioritization, so it does not live only in your head.
- Give a visible successor candidate real exposure: board updates, budget ownership, cross-functional representation, not just a title on paper.
- Proactively name who could grow into your role in conversations with your own manager, rather than waiting to be asked.
- A team that functions well without your daily involvement is itself the strongest evidence you are ready for a bigger scope.
