# Leading Your Team Through Marketing Layoffs and Restructuring

Nobody trains you for this part of the job. One quarter you are hiring, the next you are deciding which three people on your team of eight keep their roles.

## Quick Summary

- Marketing has been hit hard: over [28,400 marketing roles were eliminated in Q1 2026 alone in the US](https://www.amraandelma.com/marketing-layoffs-due-to-ai-statistics/), and 74% of service firms using AI have cut marketing staff by an average of 22%.
- Cuts are not random, routine and repeatable work goes first: campaign scheduling, A/B ad testing, templated email production, and basic reporting saw the steepest declines.
- 88.3% of marketers report fearing AI-driven job loss, which means your remaining team is already anxious before you say a word.
- Agencies are not safe either, 69% of firms terminated agency contracts within 18 months of adopting AI tools, representing $14.7 billion in reduced outsourced spend.
- Only 19% of workers targeted for retraining after a cut successfully transitioned into a new role, restructuring without a real reskilling plan mostly just delays the next round.

## Deciding What Gets Cut First

Start with output, not headcount. List every channel and role by the revenue or pipeline it demonstrably touches, then rank from clearest line to fuzziest.

Routine, repeatable execution is the first thing to go in almost every real-world cut. If a task follows the same steps every time (ad testing cadence, templated email builds, weekly report pulls), it is the easiest to automate or absorb, and the easiest for a CFO to question.

Protect the roles that require judgment under ambiguity: positioning, channel strategy, and anyone who owns a relationship with sales or product leadership. Those are hard to backfill and hard to explain away in a board meeting.

<Callout type="warning">
Do not cut by seniority or tenure alone. Cutting your most expensive people to hit a number fast often removes the judgment you need most to run a leaner team well.
</Callout>

Write the criteria down before you name a single person. A documented rationale protects you legally, and it forces you to separate "who I like" from "what the function actually needs."

## Telling the Team the Truth

The people who stay are watching how you treat the people who leave. Handle the exits with dignity, generous transition support, a clear timeline, no surprise pulled badges, and the survivors trust you more, not less.

Say the hard sentence out loud in the first team meeting: "We are smaller, the work does not shrink to match, here is what we are stopping." Naming what gets deprioritized is the single most trust-building thing you can do, silence just lets people invent worse explanations.

Do not promise "this is the last round" unless you know that for a fact. A broken promise here is far more damaging than an honest "I don't know, but I will tell you the moment I do."

<Callout type="tip">
Hold 1:1s with every remaining team member within the first week, not a group Slack message. People need to hear directly that they are still needed and why.
</Callout>

Momentum matters more than polish in this first conversation. A rough, honest update beats a slick deck that dodges the real questions people are already asking each other in DMs.

## Rebuilding Morale and Output After the Cut

Reset expectations on scope before you reset anything else. A team that lost 30% of its people cannot deliver 100% of the old roadmap, say that explicitly and cut the roadmap with them, not to them.

Give the survivors real input on what gets dropped. People tolerate a smaller team far better when they helped decide what "smaller" means in practice, rather than having triage decided entirely above them.

Watch for quiet quitting disguised as busyness, remaining staff often overwork short-term out of guilt or fear, which burns them out within two quarters. Protect at least one low-stakes win in the first 60 days, a shipped campaign, a cleaned-up process, something visible that says the team still works.

Rebuild trust in small, consistent moves: transparent weekly updates, credit given publicly, and a stated plan for when (or if) hiring resumes. Teams that survive a cut well tend to come out tighter and faster, not because the layoff was good, but because the leader was honest through it.

## Key Takeaways

- Rank roles and channels by demonstrable revenue impact before naming names, routine and templated work is cut first in nearly every real restructuring.
- Document your cutting criteria in writing before you decide who goes, it protects you and forces objectivity.
- Tell remaining staff directly and specifically what is being deprioritized, silence breeds worse rumors than the truth.
- Reset the roadmap's scope openly with the team that remains, do not expect 100% output from 70% of the headcount.
- Protect one visible early win post-restructuring, it rebuilds team confidence faster than any all-hands speech.
