Your first marketing hire and your fifteenth marketing hire should look nothing alike. That sounds obvious, but plenty of leaders keep hiring the same profile long after it stops working.

The mistake goes both ways. Hire specialists too early and you'll have an SEO expert with nothing to optimize yet. Hire generalists too late and nobody actually owns paid search while five people dabble in it.

## What generalist and specialist actually mean

A **generalist** is a marketer who can competently run several channels, content, email, basic paid ads, light analytics, without deep mastery in any single one. A **specialist** goes deep in one channel or discipline, SEO, paid social, lifecycle email, and knows it at a level a generalist never will.

Neither is "better." They solve different problems at different company stages, and the skill isn't picking a side, it's knowing when to switch.

<Callout type="info">
Forbes' 2025 council roundup on hiring notes that generalists are typically more cost-effective and versatile for smaller teams, while the tradeoff is depth: no one on a generalist-only team can go toe-to-toe with a channel expert on nuance ([Forbes, 2025](https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbescommunicationscouncil/2025/01/15/20-pros-and-cons-of-hiring-marketing-generalists-for-your-team/)).
</Callout>

## Stage 1: one to three people, hire generalists

At this stage you don't know yet which channel will work. A generalist can test five channels in a quarter and tell you which two are worth doubling down on.

For most early-stage companies, a generalist is the better first hire: they cover every channel and help the company learn what actually works before it invests in deep, single-channel expertise ([MarketerHire, 2025](https://marketerhire.com/blog/startup-marketing-team-structure)). Paying for narrow expertise before you have product-market signal is expensive guesswork.

Your first hire's job isn't to be excellent at one thing. It's to be good enough at everything to find out which thing matters.

## Stage 2: four to nine people, add your first specialists

Once a channel proves itself, generalists become the bottleneck on that channel specifically. This is the point to hire your first specialist, usually in whichever channel is already driving pipeline.

A useful rule: hire the specialist for the channel with the *best unit economics*, not the loudest internal opinion. If paid search is quietly outperforming content 3-to-1 on cost per lead, that's where your next specialist hire goes, regardless of what's trendy.

Keep one strong generalist in a marketing-manager-type role during this phase. They become the connective tissue between specialists and prevent the team from fragmenting into disconnected channel silos.

## Stage 3: ten to twenty people, specialization is no longer optional

At mid-market scale and above, specialization becomes essential; generalists at this stage start acting as coordination bottlenecks rather than force multipliers ([Chesamel, 2025](https://chesamel.com/specialists-or-generalists-effective-marketing-teams-in-2025/)). A generalist trying to own five channels at this size means five channels performing at 60% of their potential.

By 20 people, your team should look like a hybrid: specialists owning individual channels deeply, with a smaller layer of generalist-minded managers and a CMO/VP providing strategic oversight across all of it. Many of the most effective teams at this size deliberately combine both profiles, using generalists for strategic connective tissue and specialists for execution depth ([Chesamel, 2025](https://chesamel.com/specialists-or-generalists-effective-marketing-teams-in-2025/)).

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake at this stage is leaving a generalist "owning" a channel that's now big enough to need a specialist, simply because they were there first. Loyalty to your early hires shouldn't override what the channel now requires.
</Callout>

## The interview signal that tells you which profile you're looking at

Ask any candidate: "Walk me through a channel you ran end-to-end, and one you'd never touch again." Generalists answer both parts easily and enthusiastically. Specialists get noticeably more energized on the first question and vaguer on the second, they've simply spent less time outside their lane.

Neither answer is wrong. It just tells you which stage of company they're built for, and whether that matches where you are right now.

## Don't force a generalist to fake specialist depth

A generalist who's been asked to "own SEO" for a year without real specialist mentorship usually produces mediocre SEO work and gets frustrated doing it. If you can't yet afford a specialist, bring in a fractional expert or agency to guide the generalist rather than expecting osmosis.

The goal at every stage is matching hiring depth to what the channel actually needs right now, not what impressed you in a past job or what a competitor's team looks like. Get that match right, and your team scales without any single channel quietly rotting while everyone's attention is elsewhere.
