# Paid Ads 101

Paid advertising is the practice of paying a platform like Google, Meta, TikTok, or LinkedIn to show your message to a specific group of people. Unlike a billboard you rent for a fixed monthly fee, digital ads work through an auction happening in milliseconds every time a page loads or a search is run. You set a budget, define who you want to reach, write your ad, and the platform decides whether to show it and what you pay.

The global search ad market hit **$351.55 billion in 2025** (Digital Silk). In the US alone, businesses spent over **$140 billion** on search ads that same year. Paid advertising is not a niche tactic, it is the single largest category of marketing spend on earth.

## Quick Summary

- Paid ads work through real-time auctions: the winner is not always the highest bidder, but the most relevant one
- The core metrics are impressions, clicks, conversions, CTR (click-through rate), and CPC (cost per click)
- Average Google Ads conversion rate across all industries is **7.52%** in 2025, but varies wildly by sector
- Dedicated landing pages convert **65% better** than sending traffic to a homepage
- Paid ads give fast, measurable results, but traffic stops the moment you stop paying

<Callout type="info">
**Who this lesson is for:** You have heard the words "Google Ads" or "Facebook Ads" but do not know how the underlying system actually works. By the end, you will understand how every dollar moves through a paid ad campaign, from auction to click to conversion.
</Callout>

## The Core Vocabulary

Before you touch a campaign, you need five terms. They appear in every paid advertising conversation.

| Term | Plain English definition |
|---|---|
| **Impression** | Your ad was displayed once. The person may not have noticed it at all. |
| **Click** | Someone actually tapped or clicked your ad. |
| **Conversion** | The person did something you wanted: bought, signed up, called, downloaded. |
| **CTR (Click-Through Rate)** | Clicks divided by impressions. If 100 people saw your ad and 6 clicked: 6% CTR. |
| **CPC (Cost Per Click)** | How much you paid, on average, for each click. |
| **ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)** | Revenue earned divided by ad spend. $8 revenue from $1 spend = 8x ROAS. |

These six numbers are the vocabulary of almost every paid advertising conversation. Learn them once, use them everywhere.

## How the Auction Works

Every time someone searches on Google or scrolls through Instagram, the platform runs a private auction in under 200 milliseconds. You never see it happen. Here is what actually occurs:

1. A user triggers an event (a search query, a page load, an app open)
2. The platform checks which advertisers are eligible to show an ad to that person
3. Each eligible advertiser has a **bid** (max they will pay) and a **Quality Score** (how relevant their ad is)
4. The platform ranks advertisers by: `Bid x Quality Score`
5. The highest-ranked ad wins the slot
6. The winner pays just enough to beat the next person, not their full bid

**The key insight:** a $2 bid with high relevance beats a $5 bid with low relevance. This is why understanding your audience matters as much as your budget.

## The Two Flavors of Paid Ads

All paid advertising splits into two fundamentally different approaches. You need to know which one you are using and why.

### 1. Intent-Based Ads (Search)

Google Search Ads, Microsoft (Bing) Ads, and Amazon Ads show up when someone is **actively searching** for something. The person has already expressed intent. You are meeting a demand that already exists.

- Best for: products or services people already know they want
- Signal: keywords typed into a search bar
- Average Google Search CTR in 2025: **6.66%** (WordStream)

### 2. Audience-Based Ads (Social/Display)

Meta (Facebook/Instagram) Ads, TikTok Ads, LinkedIn Ads, and display banner ads show up in feeds and on websites. The person is **not actively looking** for you. You are interrupting their day with something relevant enough to make them stop.

- Best for: building awareness, reaching audiences who do not know they need you yet
- Signal: demographics, interests, behaviors, lookalike audiences
- Average Facebook conversion rate: **8.78%** for e-commerce (WordStream, 2024-25)

<Callout type="info">
Most brands use both. Search captures existing demand cheaply. Social creates new demand by reaching people who did not know they had the problem you solve. A complete paid ads strategy uses both in sequence, social builds awareness, search captures the intent it creates.
</Callout>

## Real Company Examples

### Glossier, Social Ads for Brand Building

Glossier, the direct-to-consumer beauty brand, built its early growth engine on Instagram ads targeting women aged 18-34 interested in skincare. By 2019 they reported that 80% of their revenue came from peer recommendations and paid social, with Instagram ads consistently delivering a **3-5x ROAS** on new customer acquisition campaigns. They used user-generated content (real customer photos) as ad creative, which cut their creative production cost and dramatically increased CTR compared to polished studio images.

### Booking.com, Search Ads at Scale

Booking.com spent an estimated **$5.9 billion on performance marketing in 2023** (their annual report), with the majority going to Google Search Ads. Their strategy is built on bidding on millions of long-tail keywords (specific city + hotel type combinations like "3-star hotel downtown Prague") where competition is lower and intent is extremely specific. The result: lower CPC than generic terms, higher conversion rates because the match is tight. This is the search ads playbook at its most refined, match specificity = efficiency.

<Callout type="example">
**Worked Example: A local yoga studio running Google Ads**

- Budget: $500/month
- Target keyword: "yoga classes near me" + "yoga studio [city name]"
- Average CPC in fitness/recreation: ~$2.05 (WordStream 2025)
- Budget buys approximately 244 clicks
- If the landing page converts at 7% (industry average): ~17 new leads per month
- If 30% become paying members at $80/month: ~5 new members = $400 recurring revenue from $500 ad spend

That is a 0.8x first-month ROAS, which looks bad until you factor in lifetime value. A yoga member who stays 12 months is worth $960. The real ROAS on that $500 is closer to 9.6x over a year. **This is why lifetime value (LTV) matters more than first-purchase ROAS.**
</Callout>

## The Full Campaign Structure

Paid ad platforms organize campaigns in layers. Understanding the structure stops you from making expensive mistakes.

<Mermaid chart={`
flowchart TD
  A[Campaign\nGoal + Budget + Platform] --> B[Ad Group / Ad Set\nAudience or Keyword Theme]
  B --> C1[Ad 1\nHeadline + Creative + URL]
  B --> C2[Ad 2\nVariant for A/B testing]
  C1 --> D[Landing Page\nSingle action focused]
  C2 --> D
`} />

**Campaign level:** you set the goal (sales, leads, traffic), the daily or monthly budget, and the platform/network. Changes here affect everything below.

**Ad Group / Ad Set level:** you define who sees the ads. On search, this means keyword groups. On social, this means audience targeting. Keep each group tightly themed, one keyword theme or one audience segment per group.

**Ad level:** the actual creative. Headline, image or video, description, destination URL. You should always run at least 2 ads per group so you can compare performance.

**Landing page:** not technically inside the platform, but the most important variable. The ad gets the click. The landing page gets the conversion.

## Key Metrics and What They Tell You

| Metric | Good signal | Bad signal |
|---|---|---|
| **CTR is low** | Your targeting is correct, ad copy needs work | Wrong audience, they are not interested |
| **CTR is high, conversions are low** | Ad is promising something the page does not deliver | Fix the landing page or the offer |
| **CPC is rising** | More competitors entering your auction | Consider long-tail keywords or narrower audiences |
| **Conversion rate is below 2%** | Landing page problem, or wrong traffic | Check page load speed, headline match, single CTA |
| **ROAS below 1x** | Losing money on ads | Pause, diagnose, is it the ad, the page, or the offer? |

Average benchmarks from WordStream's 2025 data:
- Average Google Ads CTR: **6.66%**
- Average Google Ads conversion rate: **7.52%**
- Average Google Ads CPC: **$5.26**
- Average cost per lead: **$70.11**

Your numbers will vary significantly by industry. Legal services average **$131.63 per lead**. Arts and entertainment average **$1.60 per click**. Always compare your performance to your own industry benchmark, not the overall average.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Mistake 1: Sending ad traffic to your homepage.**

A homepage tries to do ten things at once. Someone who clicked "project management software for remote teams" wants a page about exactly that, not a homepage with eight nav links and a generic tagline. Always send paid traffic to a dedicated landing page that matches the ad's exact promise. Research shows dedicated landing pages convert **65% better** than general website pages (Digital Silk, 2025).
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Mistake 2: Starting with too broad an audience or too many keywords.**

New advertisers try to reach everyone. Experts do the opposite: they start with the tightest possible audience, one geography, one keyword theme, one job title, get the unit economics working, then expand. A campaign converting at 5% on a $300 budget teaches you far more than one getting 50,000 impressions and zero conversions on $3,000. Start narrow. Scale what works.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Mistake 3: Touching campaigns too often (or not enough).**

Platforms like Google run a "learning phase" when you launch or significantly change a campaign, typically 7-14 days. During this time, the algorithm is calibrating who to show your ad to. If you change bids or targeting every 2 days, you keep resetting the learning phase and the algorithm never converges. Check in every 3-5 days. Make one deliberate change at a time. Then wait for data before changing again.
</Callout>

## The Budget Question

How much should you spend? The honest answer: enough to get statistically meaningful data.

For Google Search Ads: you need roughly **100-200 clicks** to know if a keyword or ad group is working. If your CPC is $3, that means $300-$600 to test one theme properly.

For Meta Ads: you need enough impressions for the algorithm's learning phase. Meta recommends at least **50 conversion events per week** per ad set. If your conversion rate is 3%, you need roughly 1,700 clicks per week to hit that, which at a $1 CPC means $1,700/week.

Most businesses starting out should budget **$500-$1,500/month** for their first 60-90 days. This gives enough data to learn what works before scaling.

<Callout type="tip">
**Before you spend on ads, check your landing page speed.** A page that takes more than 3 seconds to load loses roughly half its visitors before the ad even gets a chance to convert. Use Google PageSpeed Insights (free) to check. A slow landing page is the most expensive paid ads mistake no one talks about, you pay for the click, then lose the visitor before they read a word.
</Callout>
