# Ad Copy Frameworks

Ad copy frameworks are structured formulas that help you write persuasive text for paid ads. Instead of staring at a blank screen, you follow a pattern that has been proven to move people from scrolling past your ad to actually clicking it. Think of them like recipe templates: the ingredients and steps are laid out, and you fill in the specifics for your product.

## Quick Summary

- Ad copy frameworks (like PAS, AIDA, and the 4 Us) are fill-in-the-blank structures for writing persuasive ads faster.
- PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution) works best for short, intent-driven formats like Google Search ads.
- AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action) suits longer formats like Meta feed ads or YouTube pre-roll scripts.
- The 4 Us is a quality filter for any headline: Urgent, Unique, Useful, Ultra-specific.
- Benefit-focused copy converts 20-40% better than feature-focused copy - frameworks force you to write benefits, not features.

## Why Frameworks Exist

Writing great ad copy without a framework is like cooking without a recipe. You might get lucky once. But when you need to produce 20 headline variants for a campaign, or hand work off to a teammate, or explain why a particular line is not working, you need a system.

<Callout type="info">
**The "8 out of 10" rule:** Research by Copyblogger found that 8 out of 10 people read a headline, but only 2 out of 10 read the body copy. Your headline is doing 80% of the work. Frameworks are headline-first thinking tools - they force you to lead with what the reader cares about most.
</Callout>

Frameworks also remove guesswork from A/B testing (comparing two versions of an ad to see which one performs better). Instead of randomly trying different words, you test structured hypotheses: "Does PAS outperform AIDA for this audience?" That gives you data you can actually learn from and apply to the next campaign.

## The Three Core Frameworks

### 1. PAS - Problem, Agitate, Solution

PAS is the most direct framework. It has three moves:

1. **Problem** - Name the exact pain your customer already feels. Be specific. Use the words they would use, not marketing jargon.
2. **Agitate** - Make that pain feel bigger or more urgent. Show the cost of inaction. Add the emotional dimension.
3. **Solution** - Present your product or service as the natural, obvious fix.

PAS works especially well in Google Search ads because the person searching has already named their problem in the search query. You meet them exactly where they are.

**PAS in a Google Search ad:**
- Headline 1 (Problem): "Wasting hours on manual reports?"
- Headline 2 (Agitate): "Every hour in spreadsheets costs you strategy time."
- Description (Solution): "Automate your reporting in 5 minutes. Free trial, no card needed."

### 2. AIDA - Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

AIDA is a funnel compressed into a single ad. It was developed by advertising pioneer Elias St. Elmo Lewis in 1898 and has been used in advertising ever since. It moves the reader through four states:

1. **Attention** - Interrupt the scroll. A bold claim, a surprising stat, a direct question, or a vivid image does this.
2. **Interest** - Once they pause, give them a reason to keep reading. Add context that makes them curious.
3. **Desire** - Make the outcome feel real and personal. The reader should start imagining their life with the problem solved.
4. **Action** - Remove every obstacle between them and the next step. One CTA (call to action - the button or link you want them to click), zero ambiguity.

AIDA suits longer ad formats: Meta primary text, YouTube pre-roll scripts, LinkedIn Sponsored Content. You need some room to move someone through all four stages.

### 3. The 4 Us - Urgent, Unique, Useful, Ultra-specific

The 4 Us is not a writing formula. It is a quality filter. Run any headline through this checklist before you spend money on it:

- **Urgent** - Does it create a reason to act now rather than later?
- **Unique** - Does it say something your competitors are not saying?
- **Useful** - Does it promise a clear, real benefit to the reader?
- **Ultra-specific** - Does it use a number, a name, or a concrete detail instead of vague language?

A headline that hits 3 or 4 of these is worth testing. One that hits 1 or 2 needs a rewrite.

**Before the filter:** "Improve your marketing results today."
**After the filter:** "Double your click-through rate in 14 days - or your money back."

The rewrite is Urgent (14-day deadline), Unique (money-back promise), Useful (CTR improvement), and Ultra-specific (exact timeframe).

## How to Choose the Right Framework

The biggest mistake marketers make is using the same framework for every placement. The right framework depends on two things: the length of your ad format, and how much your audience already knows about their problem.

Cold audiences (people seeing your ad for the first time) respond best to PAS and AIDA because these frameworks do the work of establishing why they should care. Warm audiences (people who have visited your site or engaged before) respond better to FAB (Features-Advantages-Benefits) because they already understand the problem - they just need to see why your solution is the best one.

## Real Company Examples

<Callout type="example" title="Dollar Shave Club (2012): AIDA in a single sentence">
"Shave Time. Shave Money." is textbook AIDA compressed into four words. The double pun grabbed Attention, the implied comparison with expensive razor brands built Interest, the frugality angle created Desire, and the $1/month CTA drove Action with zero friction. The launch campaign drove 12,000 orders in the first 48 hours. The brand grew to $65 million in annual revenue within three years and was acquired by Unilever for $1 billion in 2016. The copy framework did not change between the viral video and their paid display and social ads - it scaled because the structure was sound.
</Callout>

Slack's homepage copy also follows this pattern. Their headline "Where work happens" scores high on the 4 Us: it is Useful (promises a clear destination for work), Unique (no competitor claimed this positioning), and Ultra-specific enough to mean something. Their subheadline immediately Agitates (so many tools, so little coordination) before the CTA drives Action. Slack's approach helped them reach 80% of Fortune 100 companies as paying customers, a fact they use as social proof directly in their ad copy.

<Callout type="warning">
**The feature trap:** "AI-powered dashboard with real-time syncing" is a feature. "Know exactly where every marketing dollar went - before your Monday standup" is an outcome. Frameworks only work if you fill them with benefits the reader cares about, not specs the product team is proud of. Benefit-focused copy converts 20-40% better than feature-focused copy, according to data from multiple CRO (conversion rate optimization) studies.
</Callout>

## A/B Testing Frameworks Against Each Other

Do not guess which framework wins. Test them. Here is a structured approach:

1. Write one ad using PAS, one using AIDA, and one using a 4 Us-optimized headline.
2. Keep everything else identical: image, landing page, audience, bid.
3. Run for at least 1,000 impressions per variant before drawing conclusions.
4. The winner tells you something about your audience's awareness level - use that insight to brief your next round of creative.

One small change in CTA copy alone can increase conversions by up to 30%. When Unbounce tested "Start your free 30-day trial" against "Start my free 30-day trial" (switching "your" to "my"), the second version drove a 90% increase in click-through rate. That is a framework principle in action: speak in the reader's voice, not yours.

<Callout type="tip">
**Pro workflow:** The highest-performing copywriters use all three frameworks together. Draft the full ad with AIDA. Write the headline with PAS. Then run the headline through the 4 Us filter before publishing. If it scores below 3 out of 4, rewrite just the headline. This three-layer approach consistently outperforms single-framework copy in head-to-head creative tests.
</Callout>

## Quick Reference: Which Framework for Which Format

| Format | Character Limit | Best Framework | Why |
|--------|----------------|----------------|-----|
| Google Search Headline | 30 chars | PAS (compress to 1-2 lines) | Intent-driven - match the problem they searched |
| Meta Primary Text | 125 chars before cutoff | AIDA | Room to build emotional arc |
| YouTube Pre-roll (5 sec) | ~12 words before skip | Attention from AIDA only | You have 5 seconds before the skip button |
| LinkedIn Sponsored Content | 150 chars | PAS or FAB | Professional tone, problem-solution works well |
| Any Headline | Any | 4 Us filter | Run every headline through this before publishing |

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Pick the framework that matches your format, fill it with a specific customer pain and a believable outcome, then run every headline through the 4 Us before you spend a single dollar.**
