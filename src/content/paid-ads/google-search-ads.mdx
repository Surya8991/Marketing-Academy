# Google Search Ads

Google Search Ads are the paid text results that appear at the top (and sometimes bottom) of a Google search page. They carry a small "Sponsored" label. When someone types a query, advertisers who have bid on related keywords can show up at that exact moment.

What makes this channel special is one word: **intent**. The person is not passively scrolling. They typed something into a search box. A search for "best accounting software for freelancers" means they want accounting software for freelancers, right now.

That is an extraordinarily valuable moment to appear in front of them.

## Quick Summary

- Google shows your text ad when someone searches a keyword you have bid on.
- You pay per click (CPC), not per impression, so budget goes toward real interest.
- Ad Rank, not just bid price, decides your position: relevance and quality matter as much as money.
- The average Google Search Ads conversion rate across all industries in 2025 is 7.52% (WordStream).
- Tight account structure (campaign > ad group > matching landing page) is the single biggest factor in keeping costs low.

## How the Auction Works

Every Google search triggers a real-time auction in milliseconds. You do not buy a fixed ad slot. Google ranks every eligible advertiser using a formula called **Ad Rank**.

Ad Rank = Bid x Quality Score x expected impact of ad extensions

**Quality Score** (scored 1-10) is Google's estimate of how relevant your ad and landing page are to the person searching. A higher Quality Score means you pay less per click and rank higher.

This is the most counterintuitive thing beginners discover: a well-written, highly relevant ad from a lower bidder beats a lazy ad from someone spending twice as much.

<Callout type="info">
Ad Rank is recalculated fresh for every single search. There is no fixed position you can "own." Your position changes based on who else is bidding at that moment, what device the user is on, and what time of day it is.
</Callout>

## Account Structure: The Foundation

Your Google Ads account is organized in three layers. Getting this right is more important than any individual optimization.

**Campaign**, Sets the budget, location targeting, and network. One campaign per product line or goal.

**Ad Group**, A cluster of closely related keywords inside a campaign. Each ad group has its own ads. Keep ad groups tight: 5-20 keywords maximum per group.

**Ads**, The actual text shown to users. Each ad group should have 3-5 ads (Google recommends Responsive Search Ads, or RSAs, where you supply up to 15 headlines and 4 descriptions and Google tests combinations).

The rule: every keyword in an ad group should closely relate to the ad copy, and the ad copy should match the landing page. This chain is what drives up Quality Score and drives down cost.

## Keyword Match Types

Match types control which searches can trigger your ad. This is the most commonly misunderstood setting in Google Ads.

**Exact match** `[keyword]`, Your ad only shows when someone searches that precise phrase (or very close variants). Most controlled, usually most efficient.

**Phrase match** `"keyword"`, Your ad shows when the search contains your keyword phrase in order, with other words around it. Good middle ground.

**Broad match** `keyword`, Google can show your ad for any search it considers related. Can reach more people but often wastes budget on irrelevant searches. Use with caution and a strong negative keyword list.

**Negative keywords**, Words that tell Google never to show your ad. If you sell new running shoes, add "repair," "used," and "vintage" as negatives so you do not pay for those clicks.

<Callout type="warning">
The most common beginner mistake: launching a campaign on broad match with zero negative keywords. Google will happily spend your budget on searches that have nothing to do with your business. Always start with exact and phrase match, then check the Search Terms report weekly to find irrelevant queries and add them as negatives.
</Callout>

## Ad Copy: What Makes People Click

A Google text ad has three parts:

- **Headlines**: Up to 15, each max 30 characters. Three are shown at a time. Google tests which combinations perform best.
- **Descriptions**: Up to 4, each max 90 characters. Two are shown at a time.
- **Display URL**: Your domain plus two optional path fields (e.g., yourdomain.com/running/shoes).

What makes a headline perform:

1. Include the keyword the person searched. This creates "relevance echo" and text goes bold.
2. Include a specific benefit or number: "Ships in 24 Hours," "Save 40%," "Rated 4.9 Stars."
3. Include a call to action: "Get a Free Quote," "Start Your Trial," "Book Online Today."

**Ad extensions** (now called "assets") are extra lines of information you can attach to ads. Options include site links, callouts, phone numbers, prices, and reviews. They increase the physical size of your ad and improve click-through rate at zero extra cost per click.

## Real-World Results: What Good Looks Like

**Stride Creative Group (2024-2025):** A digital agency took over an underperforming Google Search Ads account. By tightening keyword match types, rewriting ad copy, aligning landing pages to search intent, and optimizing bids, they achieved:

- 290% increase in conversions
- 386% improvement in conversion rate
- 49% reduction in total ad spend

All within four months. The biggest lever was landing page alignment, not bidding.

**Industry benchmarks (WordStream, 2025):** Across all industries, the average Google Search Ads click-through rate is 6.66%, the average conversion rate is 7.52%, and the average cost per lead is $70.11.

These vary enormously by industry. Automotive repair converts at 14.67% with a $28.50 cost per lead. Legal services converts at lower rates with a $131.63 cost per lead. Knowing your industry benchmark is essential before judging your own numbers.

<Callout type="example">
Worked example: You sell project management software. You create a campaign with a $50/day budget. You create three ad groups: "project management software" (for broad comparison searches), "Asana alternative" (for competitor searches), and "team task tracking tool" (for feature-based searches). Each ad group has its own ads that match those exact phrases, and each links to a dedicated landing page. After two weeks, the Search Terms report shows your "project management software" broad match ad is triggering for "project management certification", irrelevant. You add "certification" as a negative keyword. Your cost per trial signup drops from $85 to $54 within a month.
</Callout>

## Bidding Strategies

You can tell Google how to bid on your behalf. The main strategies:

**Manual CPC**, You set every keyword bid yourself. Full control, but time-intensive. Good for small budgets or when you are first learning.

**Target CPA (cost per acquisition)**, You tell Google what you want to pay per conversion. Google's algorithm adjusts bids to hit that target. Requires at least 30-50 conversions per month to work well.

**Target ROAS (return on ad spend)**, You tell Google the revenue you want for every dollar spent. Requires conversion value tracking (typically for e-commerce).

**Maximize Conversions**, Google spends your full daily budget to get as many conversions as possible. Simple to set up, but watch your CPA carefully.

<Callout type="tip">
Do not switch to automated bidding strategies too soon. Google's algorithm needs conversion data to optimize. If you have fewer than 30 conversions in the past 30 days, manual CPC or Maximize Clicks (to build traffic first) is more predictable. Switch to Target CPA once you have enough data.
</Callout>

## Conversion Tracking: Non-Negotiable

Conversion tracking tells Google what happens after someone clicks your ad. Without it, you are flying blind. You cannot know which keywords drive sales, which ads waste money, or whether automated bidding is working.

What to track:

- **Purchase / form submission**: The most important event. Set this up before spending a dollar.
- **Phone calls**: If your business gets calls, use Google's call tracking numbers.
- **Micro-conversions**: Email signups, video plays, page scroll depth. Useful data, but do not optimize for these unless purchases are scarce.

Set up conversion tracking via Google Ads > Tools > Conversions, or use Google Tag Manager (a tag management system that makes it easier to add tracking code to your site without developer help).

## Common Mistakes Summary

- Sending all traffic to your homepage instead of a specific landing page
- Using broad match with no negative keywords
- Not setting up conversion tracking before launching
- Bidding on competitor brand names without understanding the cost (usually high CPC, low conversion)
- Running too few keywords in too few ad groups, making it impossible to test what works
- Ignoring the Search Terms report

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Google Search Ads are the only ad channel where you pay to interrupt someone who has already decided they want what you sell.**
