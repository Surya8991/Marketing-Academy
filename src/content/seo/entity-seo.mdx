# Entity SEO & Knowledge Graph

## What It Is

When you search for "Tim Cook," Google doesn't just look for pages containing those two words. It pulls up a panel on the right side of the screen showing his photo, job title, birthday, and the companies he's run. That panel is powered by Google's Knowledge Graph — a giant database of real-world *things* (people, companies, products, places, songs) and the relationships between them. Entity SEO is the practice of getting Google to recognize your brand, your founders, and your products as distinct, verified *things* inside that database — not just as strings of text on a web page.

The shift from keywords to entities started in 2012 when Google launched the Knowledge Graph with 500 million entities. By 2020 that number had crossed 500 billion facts about 5 billion entities, and in early 2025 Google began rolling out Knowledge Panels to a much wider range of mid-market companies and their executives — not just the Fortune 500. The practical effect: Google now ranks based on *which entity a page is about* and *how authoritative that entity is*, then uses keywords to match the query.

For an advanced SEO, this changes the unit of work. You're no longer optimizing pages around target queries; you're building an "entity home" (usually your About page), establishing verifiable identity through structured data and sameAs links, and earning third-party corroboration so Google's algorithms can confidently merge the signals into a stable entity record.

## Real-World Example

<Callout type="example" title="HubSpot — turning a category into an entity moat">
HubSpot didn't just rank for "inbound marketing" — they *became* the entity. By 2024 the brand had its own Knowledge Panel, a Wikidata entry (Q5928322), a Wikipedia page, and over 12,000 sameAs-style citations across authoritative domains. Their About page carries a complete Organization JSON-LD block linking founders Brian Halligan and Dharmesh Shah as named Person entities, each with their own knowledge panels. Result: HubSpot ranks #1 for the head term "inbound marketing" (1.2M+ monthly US searches per Ahrefs, 2024), captures the AI Overview citation for the term, and according to SparkToro's 2024 brand-strength index sits in the top 0.1% of B2B SaaS for unlinked brand mentions — a primary entity-corroboration signal.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

- **AI Overviews and ChatGPT Search cite entities, not URLs.** A 2025 BrightEdge study found that 74% of AI Overview citations went to brands with an established Knowledge Graph presence, even when smaller publishers had better on-page SEO.
- **Knowledge Panels compress the SERP.** When Google shows a panel for your brand, branded search CTR to your homepage typically rises 30–50%, and competitors lose the ability to bid against your brand identity.
- **Entity authority compounds across queries.** Once Google trusts that "HubSpot = CRM platform," every page on hubspot.com inherits topical relevance — you stop fighting for individual keywords and start ranking for whole topic neighborhoods.
- **It's the only durable defense against LLM hallucination.** If your brand isn't an entity, AI assistants will guess — often wrongly — about what you do, who founded you, and what you sell.

## How It Works

Google builds an entity record by triangulating signals from three sources: structured data you publish (JSON-LD), authoritative third-party databases (Wikipedia, Wikidata, Crunchbase, LinkedIn, government registries), and unstructured corroboration (press mentions, citations, co-occurrence in trusted content). When enough signals agree, Google assigns a stable internal MID (machine ID) — at which point your brand "exists" in the graph.

The entity home is the anchor. Pick one canonical URL (almost always `/about`), publish a complete Organization or Person schema with `@id`, `sameAs` pointing to your Wikidata, Wikipedia, LinkedIn, Crunchbase, and official social profiles, and reference that same `@id` from every other page using `mainEntityOfPage`. This tells Google: "all roads on our site lead back to this one entity." Then you work outward — claim or create a Wikidata Q-number, get cited in topical authority sources, and align your content clusters so each pillar reinforces a single semantic identity.

The 2024–2025 update worth knowing: Google's `sameAs` weighting now rewards *verified* identifiers (Wikidata Q-IDs, ISNI, LEI codes, government business numbers) far more heavily than social profile URLs. Adding your LEI or D-U-N-S number to Organization schema is now one of the highest-leverage moves for B2B brands trying to enter the graph.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">**Mistake 1: Treating every page as its own entity.** Sprinkling Organization schema on the homepage, blog posts, and product pages creates duplicate entity signals that Google ignores. Pick one canonical entity home, give it a stable `@id` (e.g. `https://yourbrand.com/#organization`), and *reference* that `@id` from other pages — don't redefine it.</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">**Mistake 2: Skipping Wikidata because "we're not famous enough."** Wikidata accepts entries for any company with verifiable third-party sources — you don't need a Wikipedia page first. A clean Wikidata Q-number with cited references is often the single fastest path into the Knowledge Graph for sub-$50M brands, yet 90%+ of mid-market SaaS companies still don't have one.</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">**Pro tip: Build entities for your *executives*, not just your brand.** Founder and CEO Person entities feed back into the parent Organization entity through `founder` and `employee` properties. A CEO with a Knowledge Panel boosts the company's entity confidence score measurably — which is why every YC-stage founder now publishes a personal Person schema on their bio page with `sameAs` linking to their LinkedIn, GitHub, Crunchbase, and any conference talks.</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Stop optimizing pages for keywords; start building a verifiable, well-connected entity that Google's Knowledge Graph can trust and cite.**
