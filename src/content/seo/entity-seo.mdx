# Entity SEO & Knowledge Graph

When you search for "Tim Cook," Google doesn't look for pages that contain those two words. It pulls up a panel on the right side of the screen showing his photo, job title, birthday, and companies he's run. That panel is powered by Google's Knowledge Graph: a giant database of real-world *things* (people, brands, products, places) and the relationships between them.

Entity SEO is the practice of getting Google to recognize your brand, your founders, and your products as distinct, verified *things* inside that database. Not just as strings of text on a web page.

## Quick Summary

- Google's Knowledge Graph launched in 2012 with 500 million entities and has since grown to over 5 billion entities and 500 billion facts.
- Entity SEO shifts your goal from "rank this page for this keyword" to "become the recognized, trusted object that Google associates with this topic."
- A 2025 BrightEdge study found that 74% of AI Overview citations went to brands with an established Knowledge Graph presence, even when smaller publishers had better on-page SEO.
- Your "entity home" (almost always your `/about` page) is the single URL where you declare your identity using structured data (JSON-LD) and links to third-party records like Wikidata.
- Building personal entities for founders and executives reinforces the parent company entity, which is why CEOs with their own Knowledge Panels measurably lift their company's entity authority.

## Why Google Moved Away From Pure Keywords

Traditional SEO (before 2012) matched words. You typed "best running shoes," and Google looked for pages with those exact words. The problem: words are ambiguous. "Apple" could mean the fruit, the tech company, or a New York City nickname.

Google solved this with the Knowledge Graph in 2012. Instead of matching strings of characters, Google now recognizes *entities*: real-world things that exist independently of how you spell them. "Apple Inc." and "AAPL" and "the company Steve Jobs founded" all resolve to the same entity.

The practical effect: Google now ranks based on *which entity a page is about* and *how authoritative that entity is*. Keywords are used to match the query to the right entity, not the other way around.

<Callout type="info">
**What is an entity?** In Google's system, an entity is any real-world thing that can be uniquely identified: a company, a person, a product, a place, an event, or a concept. Each entity gets an internal machine ID (called an MID) inside Google's systems. Once your brand has an MID, it "exists" in Google's world as a known, trusted object.
</Callout>

## Why This Matters Right Now

The stakes for entity SEO have jumped in 2024-2025 for three reasons.

**AI Overviews and AI chat cite entities, not URLs.** When Google's AI Overview answers a question, it doesn't scan pages, it retrieves trusted entities. A 2025 BrightEdge study found that 74% of AI Overview citations went to brands with Knowledge Graph presence. If your brand isn't in the graph, AI search effectively doesn't know you exist.

**Knowledge Panels compress the entire SERP.** When Google shows a Knowledge Panel for your brand name, branded search click-through rates typically rise 30-50%. Competitors lose the ability to insert themselves into searches for your brand.

**Google ran a "Clarity Cleanup" in June 2025.** Google removed approximately 3 billion low-quality entity entries from the Knowledge Graph in a single purge. This shows that entity authority now requires genuine third-party corroboration, not just adding a schema tag to your homepage.

**It defends you against AI hallucination.** If your brand is not a recognized entity, AI assistants will guess about who you are, what you do, and who founded you, and they often guess wrong. Being in the Knowledge Graph is the closest thing to fact-checking your own brand across the internet.

## Real-World Example: HubSpot's Entity Moat

<Callout type="example">
HubSpot didn't just rank for "inbound marketing" - they *became* the entity that Google associates with the concept. By 2024, HubSpot had a Wikidata entry (Q5928322), a Wikipedia page, a full Knowledge Panel, and over 12,000 co-citation mentions across authoritative domains. Their `/about` page carries a complete Organization JSON-LD block linking founders Brian Halligan and Dharmesh Shah as named Person entities, each with their own Knowledge Panels.

Result: HubSpot ranks number 1 for the head term "inbound marketing" (1.2M+ monthly US searches per Ahrefs, 2024), captures the AI Overview citation for that term, and sits in the top 0.1% of B2B SaaS companies for unlinked brand mentions according to SparkToro's 2024 brand-strength index. Unlinked mentions are a primary signal Google uses to verify entity authority.
</Callout>

## The Omnisend vs. Klaviyo Entity Test

Backlinko ran a 2025 co-citation study to test how entity authority affects AI search presence. They ran 15 variations of email marketing queries through Google AI Mode.

- Omnisend appeared in 12 of 15 total queries (80% presence).
- Klaviyo dominated 5 of 5 ecommerce-specific queries.
- In deliverability-focused searches, Omnisend maintained presence while Klaviyo completely disappeared.

The takeaway: Klaviyo had stronger Organization schema with multiple declared software products, while Omnisend had stronger unstructured co-citation across general email marketing discussions. Each brand "owned" the entity space that matched its third-party corroboration. Entity authority is specific, not general.

## How Google Builds an Entity Record

Google triangulates signals from three sources:

1. **Structured data you publish.** JSON-LD schema on your own website declares who you are.
2. **Authoritative third-party databases.** Wikipedia, Wikidata, Crunchbase, LinkedIn, government registries, and industry databases corroborate your identity.
3. **Unstructured corroboration.** Press mentions, podcast appearances, co-citations, and forum discussions where your brand appears alongside other known entities in your space.

When enough signals agree, Google merges them into a stable entity record with a unique MID. You now "exist" in the graph.

## Step-by-Step: How to Build Your Entity

### Step 1: Create your entity home

Pick one canonical URL, almost always `/about`, as the single place where your brand identity lives. This page should:

- Carry a complete `Organization` JSON-LD block with `@id`, `name`, `url`, `logo`, `foundingDate`, `founder`, and `sameAs`.
- The `@id` should be a stable, permanent URL fragment like `https://yourbrand.com/#organization`.
- The `sameAs` array should link to your Wikidata Q-number, Wikipedia page, LinkedIn company page, Crunchbase profile, and official social profiles.

All other pages on your site should reference this `@id` using `mainEntityOfPage`, not redefine a new Organization entity. One entity home, many references.

### Step 2: Claim a Wikidata Q-number

Wikidata is Google's most heavily weighted third-party entity source. A 2024 update made verified identifiers (Wikidata Q-IDs, ISNI numbers, LEI codes, government business registration numbers) worth far more than social profile URLs inside `sameAs`.

Wikidata accepts any company with verifiable third-party sources. You do not need a Wikipedia page first. A clean Wikidata record with cited references is often the fastest path into the Knowledge Graph for companies under $50M in revenue, yet over 90% of mid-market companies still do not have one.

### Step 3: Build Person entities for founders and executives

Founder and executive Person entities feed directly back into the parent Organization through `founder` and `employee` schema properties. A CEO with their own Knowledge Panel measurably increases the company's entity confidence score. This is why YC-backed founders increasingly publish a Person schema on their bio page with `sameAs` linking to LinkedIn, GitHub, Crunchbase, and conference talk URLs.

### Step 4: Earn third-party co-citations

Co-citations are mentions of your brand alongside other known entities in your space, even without a link. An appearance on a respected industry podcast mentioning you alongside your main competitors is worth more to entity authority than 20 low-quality backlinks. Target:

- Industry publication roundups ("10 best tools for X").
- Podcast mentions on shows that cover your topic.
- Reddit and forum threads comparing you to competitors.
- Wikipedia references (even as a citation, not the subject).

### Step 5: Align content clusters to a single semantic identity

Every content cluster on your site should reinforce exactly one entity relationship. If you are "the company that builds email marketing software for ecommerce," every cluster should orbit that identity. Competing signals (a blog post about "SEO tips" on an email marketing platform) dilute the entity signal. Pick your semantic territory and own it completely.

<Callout type="warning">
**Common mistake: redefining Organization schema on every page.** Many sites place a full Organization JSON-LD block on the homepage, blog posts, and product pages. This creates duplicate entity signals that confuse Google's merging algorithm. Define the Organization entity with its `@id` exactly once, on your entity home. On every other page, reference it with `"mainEntityOfPage": {"@id": "https://yourbrand.com/#organization"}` rather than redefining the whole object.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Common mistake: thinking "we're not famous enough for Wikidata."** Wikidata is not Wikipedia. It accepts entries for any company with at least two verifiable third-party sources (a news article, a Crunchbase listing, a government registration record). Most mid-market companies qualify right now and simply have not filed. A Wikidata Q-number is one of the highest-leverage single actions available to a growing brand.
</Callout>

## Measuring Entity Authority

Entity SEO is harder to measure than keyword rankings, but these signals tell you whether it's working:

- **Knowledge Panel appearance:** Search your brand name. A panel means you have an entity record.
- **AI Overview inclusion:** Search your core topic queries. Are you cited in the AI-generated answer at the top?
- **Unlinked brand mentions:** Tools like Ahrefs Content Explorer and Brand24 track how often your brand name appears on the web without a link pointing to you. Growing unlinked mentions signal growing entity authority.
- **Wikidata completeness score:** Log into Wikidata and check your Q-number's property completeness. More cited properties mean a stronger entity record.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Stop optimizing pages for keywords; build a verifiable, well-connected entity that Google's Knowledge Graph can trust, cite, and recommend in an AI-first world.**
