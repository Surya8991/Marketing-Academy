# E-E-A-T

## What It Is

When Google decides which pages deserve to rank, it does not simply count keywords or backlinks. It asks a deeper question: should a real person trust this content? The answer comes from a framework called E-E-A-T — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. These four signals together tell Google how credible a page (and the site behind it) actually is.

Google's Search Quality Rater Guidelines, a document used by thousands of human contractors who evaluate search results, lays out E-E-A-T in detail. Quality raters do not directly change rankings, but their feedback trains the algorithms that do. So when the guidelines say a medical article written by an anonymous author with no credentials is "low quality," that judgment eventually shapes what ranks for health queries.

The framework started as E-A-T (no first E) and gained the "Experience" dimension in December 2022. That addition was intentional: Google now wants to see that content creators have firsthand, lived experience with the topic — not just academic knowledge. A review of a hiking boot written by someone who actually wore it for six months carries more weight than a summary compiled from other reviews. This distinction matters enormously for content strategy, because it means credentials alone are no longer enough. You need to demonstrate that you have actually done the thing you are writing about.

## Real-World Example

Healthline, the medical information website, rebuilt its editorial process around E-E-A-T after Google's Medic Update in August 2018 hammered health and finance sites that lacked clear author credentials.

<Callout type="example" title="Real Example: Healthline's E-E-A-T Overhaul">
After the 2018 Medic Update wiped out an estimated 50% of organic traffic for many health sites, Healthline invested heavily in E-E-A-T signals: every article now carries a named medical reviewer (MD, RN, or relevant specialist), a clear publication and review date, and an author bio linking to external credentials. By 2021, Healthline had grown to over 200 million monthly visitors — surpassing WebMD in some traffic estimates — and consistently holds top-3 rankings for high-competition health queries like "symptoms of diabetes" and "how to lower blood pressure." The site's author pages link out to medical licenses and peer-reviewed publications, giving Google and users alike a verifiable trust chain.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

E-E-A-T is not a ranking factor you can toggle on or off. It is a property Google infers about your entire site and each individual piece of content. Marketers reach for it in specific situations:

- When you operate in a YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) category — health, finance, legal, safety — where Google applies stricter quality thresholds because bad advice causes real harm.
- When a competitor with weaker content keeps outranking you, and the gap is not explained by backlinks or technical SEO alone — E-E-A-T signals may be the invisible differentiator.
- When you are launching a new site or brand in a competitive niche and need to build credibility from scratch rather than just publishing content.
- When your content team includes AI-assisted writing, and you need a framework for ensuring human expertise and experience still stamp every piece.

## How It Works

Each of the four letters does a different job. **Experience** is the most recently added and the most personal: it asks whether the author has direct, first-person involvement with the subject. A financial advisor writing about retirement accounts scores high on experience. An anonymous content farm summarizing Reddit threads does not.

**Expertise** is the formal or demonstrated knowledge dimension. For a doctor writing about drug interactions, this means medical credentials. For a software engineer writing a tutorial, it means verifiable professional background or a body of published technical work. Google's raters are trained to distinguish genuine expertise from the appearance of it — so a page with a fake "MD" suffix in the author name but no linkable credentials will not fool the system.

**Authoritativeness** is measured at the site level as much as the author level. It asks: does the broader web treat this source as a reference? This is where backlinks, brand mentions, Wikipedia citations, and press coverage come in. A single expert writing for an unknown domain carries less authority than the same expert writing for a well-linked institution. Building authoritativeness is slow work — it accrues from consistent publishing, earning editorial links, and being cited by peers.

**Trustworthiness** is the umbrella dimension. Google explicitly calls it the most important of the four. It covers accurate information, clear sourcing, honest business practices (transparent pricing, real contact information, accessible privacy policies), and the absence of deceptive design. A page can have all three of the other letters and still fail on Trust if it buries corrections, hides authorship, or uses dark patterns.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Treating E-E-A-T as a one-time audit.** Many teams run an E-E-A-T checklist once, add author bios, and move on. But authoritativeness builds over time and trust can erode fast — a single factual error left uncorrected, an outdated statistic, or a broken contact page quietly damages your quality signals. E-E-A-T requires ongoing content maintenance, not a one-off fix.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Confusing credentials with experience.** After Google added the first "E" in 2022, many sites responded by recruiting credentialed authors but still publishing generic content that could have been written by anyone. A cardiologist's byline on a bland article about "heart health tips" does not automatically demonstrate experience. The content itself needs to reflect first-hand perspective — specific patient scenarios, personal observations, or direct product use — not just a title in the author box.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Build an author entity, not just an author page.** The highest-leverage E-E-A-T move most sites skip is building a consistent digital footprint for each author: a Google Scholar profile, a LinkedIn page with verifiable work history, guest articles on recognized industry publications, and a Wikipedia mention if warranted. When Google's Knowledge Graph can connect your on-site author bio to a coherent off-site entity, the trust signal multiplies. This is especially powerful for YMYL topics where a single credible author with a strong entity can lift an entire content cluster.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**E-E-A-T is Google's way of asking "would a real expert stake their reputation on this?" — and every content decision you make should answer yes.**
