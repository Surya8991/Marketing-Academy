# Local SEO

Local SEO is the practice of making your business show up when people nearby search for what you sell. When someone types "coffee shop open now" or "plumber near me" into Google, they are not browsing for ideas. They are ready to spend money right now, and they want an answer within walking or driving distance.

Local SEO is how you make sure your business is that answer.

## Quick Summary

- Google runs a separate ranking system for location-based searches, built on three signals: relevance, distance, and prominence.
- The "local pack" (the map + 3 listings at the top of search results) gets 42% of all clicks for local queries (Backlinko, 2024).
- Your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the single most important free asset in local SEO - it directly feeds the local pack and Google Maps.
- Reviews, consistent NAP (Name, Address, Phone number) across the web, and local backlinks determine how high you rank.
- 76% of people who do a local mobile search visit a physical business within 24 hours (Google data) - local SEO traffic is extremely high-intent.

---

## Why Local SEO Is Different from Regular SEO

Regular SEO (search engine optimization) is about ranking for keywords globally or nationally. Local SEO targets a specific geographic area: your city, neighborhood, or service radius.

Here is the key difference: for local queries, Google shows a "local pack" - a map with three business listings - above the regular blue-link search results. To appear there, you do not just need a good website. You need a well-optimized Google Business Profile (a free listing Google provides every business), strong reviews, and consistent business information across the internet.

<Callout type="info">
**The local pack vs. regular search results.** When you search "dentist near me," you see a map and three listings before any website links. That map section is the local pack. It gets roughly 42% of all clicks for local queries (Backlinko, 2024), while the organic website results below it share the rest. Winning the local pack is often more valuable than ranking #1 in regular search results.
</Callout>

## The Three Factors Google Uses to Rank Local Results

Google has officially stated that local rankings depend on three things:

1. **Relevance** - Does your business match what the person searched for? This is determined by your business category, the keywords in your profile and website, and the services you list.
2. **Distance** - How close is your business to the person searching (or to the location they mentioned)? You cannot change your address, but you can signal your service area clearly.
3. **Prominence** - How well-known and trusted is your business? This includes Google reviews, mentions on other websites (called citations), backlinks from local sites, and how complete and active your Google Business Profile is.

## The Four Pillars of Local SEO

### Pillar 1: Google Business Profile (GBP)

Your Google Business Profile - formerly called Google My Business - is a free listing that appears on Google Search and Google Maps. It is the single most important asset in local SEO.

A complete GBP delivers real results. Businesses with complete profiles receive 70% more location visits and 50% more purchase consideration than businesses with incomplete ones (Google). Profiles with photos get 42% more direction requests and 35% more website clicks.

**What to do:**

- Claim and verify your GBP at business.google.com (free).
- Choose the most specific primary category that describes your business. "Italian Restaurant" beats "Restaurant."
- Add your full address, phone number, website, and hours - and keep them accurate.
- Upload at least 10 photos (exterior, interior, team, products).
- Enable messaging so customers can contact you directly.
- Post weekly updates (offers, events, news) - Google rewards active profiles with higher visibility.
- Answer every question in the Q&A section before customers ask them.

### Pillar 2: NAP Consistency

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone number. Your NAP must be character-for-character identical everywhere it appears on the internet: your website, Yelp, TripAdvisor, Facebook, YellowPages, and every other directory.

Why does this matter? Google cross-references dozens of sources to verify your business information. If your GBP says "Suite 200" but your Yelp page says "#200," Google sees potential inconsistency and loses confidence in your listing. That uncertainty hurts your ranking.

**The rule:** pick one exact format for your name, address, and phone number - then use it everywhere, always.

### Pillar 3: Reviews

Reviews are a direct ranking factor in local SEO. Google's algorithm considers review volume, star rating, recency, and whether the business responds to reviews.

The numbers are stark: 97% of consumers read reviews for local businesses (BrightLocal, 2026). A 4.3-star average rating is the minimum threshold before most consumers will consider using a local business (BrightLocal). Review signals account for roughly 15-17% of local pack ranking weight (Moz).

The most common mistake: businesses get 50 reviews at launch and stop asking. Google weights recent reviews heavily. A competitor with 20 reviews from last month can outrank you and your 200 reviews from three years ago.

**Build a review system:** after every completed job or purchase, send a short text or email asking for a Google review. Make it a process, not a one-time effort.

### Pillar 4: On-Page Local Signals and Citations

Your website needs to tell Google your location clearly.

**On-page requirements:**

- Create a dedicated location page (or one page per city if you serve multiple areas).
- Include your city and neighborhood naturally in the title tag, H1, and body copy.
- Embed a Google Maps iframe on your contact or location page.
- Add LocalBusiness schema (structured data markup) to your website - this tells Google your exact name, address, phone, hours, and business type in machine-readable format.

**Citations** are mentions of your NAP on other websites. The major directories include:

- Yelp
- TripAdvisor
- YellowPages
- Facebook
- Apple Maps
- Bing Places
- Industry-specific directories

You do not need hundreds of them. You need the major ones to be accurate and consistent.

## Voice Search and "Near Me" Queries

Voice searches (asking Siri, Google Assistant, or Alexa a question aloud) now account for 58% of consumers finding local businesses at least once per week (data from OnTheMap, 2024). Voice queries are phrased differently from typed searches. Instead of "pizza downtown," someone speaking says "what is the best pizza place near me that is open right now?"

To rank for voice search, make sure your hours are accurate on your GBP, use natural conversational language in your location page copy, and add FAQ content to your website that answers questions people actually speak aloud.

**"Near me" searches** have grown 900% over two years (Google data). Yet only 35% of small and medium businesses have a Google Business Profile at all (BrightLocal, 2025). This is the opportunity: most of your local competitors have not done the basics.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Keyword stuffing your business name in GBP.** Some businesses list themselves as "Joe's Plumbing - Best Plumber Dallas TX" to try to rank for keywords. Google explicitly prohibits this and can suspend your listing entirely. Your GBP name must match exactly the name on your storefront or business registration.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Building citations and then forgetting them.** If you move, change your phone number, or rebrand, every citation across every directory must be updated. One wrong address on a major directory can confuse Google and suppress your ranking for months. Use a tool like BrightLocal or Moz Local to audit and manage your citations in one place.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Respond to every review, including negative ones.** Google's algorithm tracks review response rate. More importantly, 88% of consumers say a business response to a negative review influences their decision to use that business. A calm, professional response to a 1-star review can win back trust publicly. Keep responses short: acknowledge the experience, apologize if appropriate, and offer to resolve it offline.
</Callout>

---

## Local SEO vs. Paid Local Ads

Local SEO and Google Local Services Ads (paid) are different channels. Local SEO (organic) is free to maintain once established. It builds compounding authority over time and earns clicks without ongoing cost per click.

Paid local ads appear above the local pack and can drive immediate traffic, but stop the moment you stop paying. The best local marketing strategy uses both: paid ads for immediate lead flow while organic local SEO compounds over 3-6 months and eventually reduces your paid spend.
