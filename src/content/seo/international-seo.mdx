# International SEO

When a company wants to show up in search results across multiple countries or languages, it needs a strategy built for that goal. International SEO is the practice of structuring your website so search engines understand which version of your content to show to users in different countries or different languages.

Without this, Google might serve your English content to French speakers, or your US pricing page to Australian customers. None of those people will find what they need — and you lose rankings, clicks, and sales.

## Quick Summary

- Tell search engines which page is meant for which country and language using `hreflang` tags
- Choose the right URL structure (ccTLD, subfolder, or subdomain) based on your budget and goals
- Localization means more than translation: keyword research must be done natively in each market
- 75% of users prefer buying in their native language, so this directly affects revenue
- Hreflang must be implemented on every page consistently — partial rollouts create confusion

## The Three URL Structure Options

Before you touch a single hreflang tag, you need to decide how your URLs will be structured. This is the biggest architectural decision in international SEO, and it is hard to reverse later.

<Mermaid chart={`
flowchart TD
  A[International SEO: Pick URL Structure] --> B[ccTLD<br/>nike.fr / ikea.de]
  A --> C[Subfolder<br/>example.com/fr/]
  A --> D[Subdomain<br/>fr.example.com]
  B --> E[Strongest geo signal<br/>Most expensive]
  C --> F[Inherits domain authority<br/>Best for most companies]
  D --> G[Google treats as<br/>separate site - avoid]
`} />

**ccTLD (Country-Code Top-Level Domain):** A separate domain per country, like `amazon.fr` or `booking.de`. This sends the strongest geographic signal to Google. The downside: each domain starts with zero authority and you need a separate SEO strategy for each one. Best for large enterprises with dedicated local teams and big budgets.

**Subfolder (Recommended for most):** One domain, organized by locale folders: `example.com/fr/`, `example.com/de/`, `example.com/es/`. This is the sweet spot for most growing companies. Your international pages inherit the authority of your main domain, and everything is managed in one place. Airbnb, HubSpot, and most mid-size global brands use this approach.

**Subdomain:** Separate subdomains like `fr.example.com`. Google increasingly treats these as separate sites, which splits your link authority without giving you the clear geo-targeting benefits of a ccTLD. This option has mostly fallen out of favor.

## How hreflang Tags Work

`hreflang` is an HTML attribute that tells Google two things: (1) what language a page is written in, and (2) which country it targets. You place these tags in the `<head>` section of every page.

Here is what a basic hreflang setup looks like for a site targeting France, Germany, and a global English default:

```html
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="fr-FR" href="https://example.com/fr/product/" />
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="de-DE" href="https://example.com/de/product/" />
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="en" href="https://example.com/product/" />
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="x-default" href="https://example.com/product/" />
```

The `x-default` tag tells Google which page to show when no other locale matches - usually your English or global homepage.

<Callout type="warning">
**The most common hreflang mistake:** Adding tags only to your homepage or a few top pages, but leaving thousands of product and blog pages untagged. Google only acts on hreflang when it is consistent across your entire site. A partial rollout can actually make things worse - it sends conflicting signals about which pages target which audience. According to SEO auditors, ignoring this can cost you 20-30% of potential international traffic.
</Callout>

Key rules for hreflang:
- Every tagged page must include tags pointing to ALL alternate versions (including itself)
- Every alternate URL must return a 200 HTTP status code
- Language codes use ISO 639-1 format (fr, de, es) and country codes use ISO 3166-1 alpha-2 (FR, DE, ES)
- Tags can go in the HTML `<head>`, the XML sitemap, or HTTP headers - but pick one method and be consistent

## Localization vs. Translation

Translation means converting words from one language to another. Localization means making content feel native to the target market.

<Callout type="example">
**Worked example - Mexico vs. Spain:** A SaaS company expands from English into Spanish. They translate their homepage word-for-word. The English headline was "Streamline your workflows." The Spanish translation becomes "Optimice sus flujos de trabajo." This ranks for exactly zero searches in Mexico, because Mexican marketers search for "automatizar procesos de trabajo" - a completely different phrase. The same product, targeting the same language, requires different keyword research for Mexico vs. Spain vs. Argentina. Running your English content through Google Translate and hitting publish is not international SEO. It is wasted effort.
</Callout>

Proper localization includes:
- **Keyword research done natively** in each target market, not translated from English
- **Local currency and pricing** displayed in market-appropriate formats
- **Date and number formats** that match local conventions (13/06/2025 vs. June 13, 2025)
- **Cultural references** that resonate with the target audience
- **Local trust signals** like region-specific testimonials, certifications, or payment methods

## The International SEO Workflow

<Mermaid chart={`
flowchart LR
  A[Validate demand<br/>in target country] --> B[Choose URL<br/>structure]
  B --> C[Set up hreflang<br/>on all pages]
  C --> D[Native keyword<br/>research per locale]
  D --> E[Localize content<br/>not just translate]
  E --> F[Build local<br/>backlinks]
  F --> G[Monitor GSC<br/>for hreflang errors]
  G --> A
`} />

**Step 1 - Validate demand first:** Before building anything, check Google Search Console for organic impressions from your target countries on your existing English pages. If you are already getting 5,000+ monthly impressions from Germany with zero German content, that is proof of latent demand worth investing in. Start with markets that show organic signals before creating content for markets that may not convert.

**Step 2 - Set up the URL structure:** Pick subfolder, ccTLD, or subdomain and configure server-side geo-targeting in Google Search Console under "International Targeting."

**Step 3 - Implement hreflang across all pages:** Use a site crawler (Screaming Frog, Sitebulb, or Semrush) to audit your implementation and catch errors. Common errors include missing self-referencing tags and broken alternate URLs.

**Step 4 - Do native keyword research:** Hire a native speaker or use tools like Semrush or Ahrefs with country-specific filters. Interview local users about their terminology. Study what your local competitors rank for.

**Step 5 - Localize, not translate:** Rewrite content to fit local search intent, cultural context, and terminology.

**Step 6 - Build local backlinks:** Links from local domains (a .de site linking to your /de/ pages) send strong geographic signals to Google. Use local PR, guest posting, and directory listings.

**Step 7 - Monitor for errors:** Set up Google Search Console properties for each locale. Check the "International Targeting" report and the "Coverage" report regularly.

<Callout type="tip">
**Pro tip:** Use Ahrefs' "Top Pages" feature filtered by country to find which of your existing pages already rank in target markets. These pages are your highest-ROI localization candidates because Google already sees them as relevant - they just need a translated, localized version to rank even higher.
</Callout>

## When to Use International SEO

Start this work when any of these are true:
- Your analytics show meaningful traffic or signups from countries you have not explicitly targeted
- You are launching into a new market and want organic visibility from day one
- Competitors in your target markets dominate local search results and you need a structural advantage
- You have a product that solves a problem people actively search for in multiple languages

---

## The One-Line Takeaway

**International SEO is not about translating your content - it is about building a precise system of signals so Google serves the right page, in the right language, to the right person in the right country.**
