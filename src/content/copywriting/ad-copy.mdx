# Ad Copy (Search, Social, Display)

## What It Is

Ad copy is the written text inside a paid advertisement. It includes the headline, the description, and any call-to-action (CTA) button text. The goal is simple: get the right person to stop, read, and click.

Every ad channel - search, social, and display - has its own rules. Search ads live inside Google or Bing results. Social ads appear in feeds on platforms like Meta, LinkedIn, or TikTok. Display ads are the banner images you see on websites across the internet. Each format rewards a different writing approach because the audience is in a different mindset when they see the ad.

The core principles never change: speak to one person, address one problem, make one offer, ask for one action. What changes is how much space you have and how much awareness your reader already has.

## Real-World Example

In 2023, Booking.com ran a search ad test where they changed a single word in their headline - from "Best Hotels" to "Hotels Near You" - and saw a 14% increase in click-through rate for mobile users. The lesson: search users are often in a specific, immediate moment. Copy that matches that moment (location, urgency, intent) beats generic superlatives every time.

<Callout type="example" title="Booking.com: One Word, 14% More Clicks">
Booking.com A/B tested headlines on Google Search in 2023. Replacing "Best Hotels" with "Hotels Near You" lifted mobile CTR by 14%. The winning version matched the user's immediate intent rather than making a brand claim. This is called "query mirroring" and it is one of the fastest wins in search ad copy.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

In Q2 2024, worldwide CTR for search ads was 1.63% - more than double the 0.66% CTR for social ads, and four times the 0.39% seen on retail media (eMarketer, 2024). Those gaps exist largely because of copy quality and intent matching. A well-written search ad speaks directly to what someone just typed. A poorly written one loses the click to a competitor who did the same thing better.

Poor ad copy wastes budget fast. If 1,000 people see your ad and only 3 click because the message was vague, you have burned money on 997 impressions that did nothing. Good copy pulls the right people in and filters the wrong ones out - reducing wasted spend and improving conversion rates downstream.

This matters for any business running paid campaigns. It is not just a creative exercise. It is a revenue lever.

## How It Works

The core framework is: match the channel to the mindset, then match the copy to the moment.

**Search copy** - The user already has a problem. They typed it. Your job is to mirror that intent, show you have the answer, and remove friction. Use keywords in headlines. Include a price, a deadline, or a differentiator. Keep descriptions specific.

**Social copy** - The user is not looking for you. They are scrolling. Your job is to stop the scroll in the first line, then earn the click. Lead with the problem or the outcome. Save the brand name for later. Shorter is usually better on mobile.

**Display copy** - The user has even less attention. You often get one headline and one image. The headline must work standalone. Focus on one benefit only. Use action verbs. Match the visual and the text so they reinforce each other.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake is writing one version of copy and running it across all three channels unchanged. A headline that works in search ("Fix Slow Wi-Fi Today - Free Setup Guide") sounds like spam on a Facebook feed and disappears inside a display banner. Tailor the length, the opening hook, and the level of urgency to the channel. What you say matters less than where and when you say it.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
Always write the CTA before the body copy. Decide what action you want first - "Start Free Trial", "Get a Quote", "Download Now" - then work backwards. Every sentence in the ad should make that action feel easier or more obvious. If a sentence does not support the CTA, cut it.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Match the copy to the mindset: search users are hunting, social users are browsing, and display users are barely paying attention - write differently for each.**
