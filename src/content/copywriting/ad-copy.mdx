# Ad Copy (Search, Social, Display)

In 2025, Google search ad clicks hit their highest level in five years and average CPCs rose 12.88% year over year. Every word in your ad now costs more than it did last year. Writing tighter, sharper copy is no longer optional.

## Quick Summary

- Ad copy is the written text of a paid ad: headline, description, and CTA button text.
- Search, social, and display users are in completely different mindsets. The same copy fails across all three.
- The cross-industry average Google Search CTR in 2025 is 6.66%, up 3.74% year over year ([WordStream, 2025](https://www.wordstream.com/blog/2025-google-ads-benchmarks)).
- Social ads average 0.5% to 1.6% CTR. The copy job is scroll-stopping, not intent-matching.
- Display CTR averages 0.05% to 0.1%. One benefit, one visual, one action.

## What It Actually Is

Ad copy is any written text that runs inside a paid placement to get a specific person to take a specific action. It covers the headline (the first thing they see), the description (the supporting argument), and the call-to-action (the instruction).

Think of it like a job applicant covering letter sent to three very different hiring managers. The search user is a manager who already posted a job listing and is actively reviewing applications. The social user is a manager who is catching up on industry news and was not expecting to hire today. The display user is a manager who saw your flyer on a coffee shop wall while waiting for their order. The same cover letter fails all three. The right letter for each of them is short, specific, and tuned to how much they already care.

## Why It Matters (with data)

The gap between channels is enormous. According to [WordStream's 2025 Google Ads Benchmarks](https://www.wordstream.com/blog/2025-google-ads-benchmarks), the average search CTR across all industries is **6.66%**. Arts and Entertainment tops the list at 13.10%. Even the lowest-performing category, Dentists and Dental Services, averages 5.44%.

Social ads tell a different story. [2025 social CTR benchmarks](https://helentmedia.com/2025-social-media-ctr-benchmarks/) place most platforms between 0.5% and 1.6% CTR. Facebook video ads using traffic objectives average 1.57% across 23 industries. Display ads sit even lower, between 0.05% and 0.1% globally ([AI Digital, 2025](https://www.aidigital.com/blog/ctr-for-display-ads)).

Those gaps are not purely about platform audiences. They reflect how well copy is matched to intent. A search user typed a specific phrase. Your ad is either the answer to that phrase or it is not. On social and display, your ad interrupted something they actually wanted to do, so you start with negative attention. The copy has to earn its way in from a deficit.

Cost data makes this even more urgent. Average Google Ads CPC in 2025 is $5.26 overall, with legal at $8.58 and home improvement at $7.85 ([WordStream, 2025](https://www.wordstream.com/blog/2025-google-ads-benchmarks)). A 1% improvement in CTR on a $10,000/month budget can mean hundreds of additional clicks at zero additional spend. Good copy is the cheapest lever you have.

## How It Works: The Playbook

### Step 1: Identify the Channel and the Mindset

Before writing a single word, answer: where will this person see the ad, and what were they doing one second before?

- **Search:** They typed a query. They have a problem, a need, or a comparison in mind. They are in buying mode or research mode.
- **Social:** They are scrolling a feed, watching friends, consuming content. Your ad arrived uninvited.
- **Display:** They are reading an article, using an app, or watching a video. Your banner is peripheral noise unless something in it is relevant to them right now.

### Step 2: Write to the Mindset

**Search copy rules:**

1. Put the most important keyword in Headline 1. Google bolds query-matching text.
2. Use Headline 2 for a differentiator: price, speed, guarantee, or unique benefit.
3. Use Headline 3 for a CTA or brand name.
4. In descriptions, be specific. "Save 30% on annual plans" beats "Great value."
5. Add numbers, deadlines, or social proof when possible.

**Social copy rules:**

1. The first line is everything. It must stop the thumb before the "See more" cutoff.
2. Lead with the problem the audience has, not the product you sell.
3. Keep body copy under 150 characters on mobile unless you have a strong hook.
4. UGC-style and testimonial copy consistently outperforms polished brand copy by up to 5x ([Billo, 2025](https://billo.app/blog/what-is-a-good-ctr/)).
5. Write the CTA button text to complete the sentence in the body: "Ready to fix this?" + "Start Free Trial."

**Display copy rules:**

1. Assume the headline will be read in isolation. It must make sense with no description.
2. One benefit only. "Cut your energy bill" is better than "Save energy and money and the environment."
3. Use an active verb at the start: Get, Cut, Stop, Save, Try.
4. Match headline tone to the visual. A funny image with a serious headline creates confusion and kills clicks.
5. Test contrast in copy length: very short (3 words) vs. short (8 words). Both outperform medium lengths in display.

### Step 3: Write 3 to 5 Variants

Never run one version. Write at least three variants that test:
- A different hook angle (problem vs. outcome vs. curiosity)
- A different length (short vs. medium)
- A different CTA verb (Get vs. Start vs. Try vs. Claim)

### Step 4: Test and Kill Losers Fast

Run variants simultaneously with equal budgets. Give each at least 100 impressions before drawing conclusions. Kill the bottom performers and allocate budget to the winner. Then write new challengers to beat the current winner.

## Real Company Examples

### Booking.com: One Word Lifted Mobile CTR by 14%

In a 2023 Google Search test, Booking.com changed a single headline word. "Best Hotels" became "Hotels Near You." Mobile CTR increased by 14%. The reason is straightforward: "Best Hotels" is a brand claim with no context. "Hotels Near You" mirrors the actual intent of a mobile user who is often searching while traveling. This technique is called query mirroring, matching the language and implied location of the search query in the headline.

<Callout type="example" title="Booking.com: Query Mirroring in Action">
Swapping "Best Hotels" for "Hotels Near You" in a Google Search headline produced a 14% mobile CTR lift for Booking.com. The winning version answered the implied question behind the query ("where can I stay near here?") instead of making a generic quality claim. For search ads, proximity to the user's actual thought beats all other headline strategies.
</Callout>

### Seltzer Goods: Facebook Ad Overhaul Lifted Revenue 785% in 30 Days

eCommerce brand Seltzer Goods worked with inflow in 2024 to restructure their Facebook ad copy from generic product descriptions to outcome-focused storytelling copy. The revised ads led the first line with the emotional outcome ("Give someone a gift they actually keep") rather than the product category. Revenue from paid social rose 785% within 30 days of the new copy launching ([GoInflow Case Study, 2024](https://www.goinflow.com/blog/facebook-ads-case-study-seltzer-goods/)). The core change was moving from what the product is to what the product does for the buyer.

<Callout type="example" title="Seltzer Goods: Outcome-First Copy on Facebook">
Shifting from product description copy ("Unique art prints and gifts") to outcome-led copy ("Give someone a gift they actually keep") drove a 785% revenue increase in paid social within 30 days. Social users do not care about your product category. They care about what happens to them if they click. Start there.
</Callout>

### Heights: Copy Testing Combined with Landing Page Validation

UK health brand Heights ran systematic copy A/B tests across Meta ads in 2024, then validated winning angles on their website. Three experiments that used copy-matched landing pages produced lifts of 29.7%, 27.6%, and 25.0% at 95% statistical significance. Earlier tests that used validated copy but mismatched landing pages showed 9.1% and 9.4% non-significant drops. The lesson: copy consistency from ad to landing page is as important as the copy itself.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Running identical copy across all three channels.**
Search copy tends to be functional and keyword-dense. Pasting that text into a Facebook ad sounds robotic. Pasting it into a display banner is unreadable. Each channel needs its own copy written from scratch.

**Mistake 2: Writing the CTA last.**
Most copywriters write the headline, then the description, then the CTA as an afterthought. Reverse this. Write the CTA first. Every word before it exists to make the CTA feel like the obvious next step.

**Mistake 3: Using superlatives with no proof.**
"World's Best," "Industry-Leading," and "Top-Rated" are invisible. Readers skip them without processing them. Replace superlatives with specifics: "4.9 stars from 12,000 reviews" beats "Highly Rated" every time.

**Mistake 4: Testing too many variables at once.**
Changing the headline, the image, and the CTA simultaneously tells you nothing. If CTR doubles, you do not know which change caused it. Test one variable at a time. Run for at least 7 days with equal budgets per variant.

**Mistake 5: Ignoring the quality score or relevance score impact.**
Google assigns a Quality Score partly based on expected CTR. Better copy directly lowers your CPC. A Quality Score of 8 vs. 4 on the same bid can reduce cost per click by 30 to 50%. Writing for the human reader also writes for the algorithm.

## Key Takeaways

- The 2025 average Google Search CTR is 6.66%. If yours is below 4%, the copy is the most likely reason.
- Search users are hunting: mirror their query. Social users are browsing: stop their scroll. Display users are distracted: one benefit, one action.
- Always write the CTA first, then work backwards through the body copy and headline.
- Specifics beat superlatives: "Saves 3 hours per week" outperforms "Saves time."
- UGC-style and testimonial copy outperforms polished brand copy by up to 5x on social platforms.
- Copy consistency from ad to landing page is as important as the ad copy itself. Disconnect between the two kills conversions even when the ad copy is strong.
