# Copywriting 101

## What It Is

Copywriting is the craft of writing words that make people take action. Not pretty words. Not clever words. Words that sell, convert, sign up, click, or buy. Every headline on a product page, every subject line in an email, every button label on a website - that is copy.

The word "copy" comes from old newspaper printing, where "copy" meant the text handed to the typesetter. Today it means any text written with a persuasive goal. A copywriter is not a journalist or a novelist. A copywriter is a salesperson who uses words instead of a handshake.

Good copy does one job: it moves a reader from where they are now to the action you want them to take. That might be buying a product, subscribing to a list, downloading a guide, or simply reading the next sentence. Every word either earns its place by moving the reader forward, or it should be cut.

## Real-World Example

In 2012, Unbounce ran an A/B test on a call-to-action (CTA) button. A CTA is the button or link that tells the reader what to do next - "Buy Now," "Sign Up," etc. The original button read: "Start your free 30 day trial." The new version changed just one word: "Start **my** free 30 day trial."

That single word swap - "your" to "my" - increased click-through rate by 90%.

</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Every business that sells something - online or offline - has a copy problem. If your website homepage does not clearly tell a visitor what you do and why they should care within 5 seconds, they leave. If your email subject line is boring, it does not get opened. If your ad copy does not speak to a real pain point, nobody clicks.

Bad copy wastes your ad budget. Good copy multiplies it. A landing page with a strong headline converts more visitors than one with a weak headline, even if everything else is identical. Personalized CTAs convert 202% better than generic ones (HubSpot, 2024). That is not a small edge - it is a 3x difference from words alone.

Copywriting matters most at three moments: when a stranger first finds you (ad or search result), when they land on your page (headline and body copy), and when they are about to decide (CTA and offer copy). Fixing copy at any of these three points can double your results without spending more on traffic.

## How It Works

The most widely used copywriting framework is AIDA: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. Every effective piece of copy - from a 6-word ad to a 10-page sales letter - follows this basic flow.

<Mermaid chart={`flowchart TD
    A["Attention - Hook the reader\n(headline, subject line, opening)"]
    B["Interest - Keep them reading\n(relevant problem or story)"]
    C["Desire - Make them want it\n(benefits, proof, specifics)"]
    D["Action - Tell them what to do\n(clear CTA, low friction)"]
    A --> B
    B --> C
    C --> D`} />

**Attention:** Your first line must stop the scroll. Use a surprising stat, a bold claim, or a direct question that names the reader's problem. If the headline fails, nothing else matters.

**Interest:** Introduce a problem the reader already feels. Do not invent pain - reflect it back. People read copy that sounds like their own internal monologue.

**Desire:** Shift from features to benefits. A feature is what the product does. A benefit is what the reader gets. "256GB storage" is a feature. "Never delete a photo again" is a benefit. Stack specific proof: real numbers, named customers, before-and-after results.

**Action:** Tell the reader exactly what to do next. One action per piece of copy. Vague CTAs like "Learn more" underperform specific ones like "Get the free checklist." The reader should never have to guess the next step.

<Callout type="tip">
Before writing any copy, write one sentence that answers: "Who is my reader, what do they want, and what is the one thing I want them to do?" If you cannot answer that in one sentence, you are not ready to write the copy yet.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common copywriting mistake is writing about the company instead of the reader. Phrases like "We are a leading provider of..." or "Founded in 2010, our mission is..." make the reader's eyes glaze over. Replace every "we" with "you" wherever possible. Test this: search your homepage copy for the word "we" and count how many times it appears vs. "you." If "we" wins, rewrite it. Research shows that including the word "you" in marketing material can increase conversions by 47%.
</Callout>

Three other mistakes to avoid:

1. **Burying the benefit.** Do not make the reader dig for why they should care. State the benefit in the headline or the first sentence.
2. **Weak or absent CTA.** Ending copy without a clear next step is like a sales pitch that ends with "anyway, let me know." Tell them exactly what to click, call, or do.
3. **Ignoring specifics.** "Thousands of customers" is weak. "4,317 marketers use this every week" is strong. Specific numbers build trust.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Every word in your copy should either move the reader toward the action you want, or be deleted.**
