# Copywriting 101

In 2025, the average person sees between 4,000 and 10,000 marketing messages per day. The only thing standing between your message and the delete button is your copy.

## Quick Summary

- Copywriting is writing words designed to make people take a specific action: click, buy, sign up, or read on.
- Good copy is not clever writing. It is clear, specific, and reader-focused writing.
- Personalized CTAs convert 202% better than generic ones, according to SmartBlogger research.
- The AIDA framework (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action) is the backbone of almost every high-converting piece of copy.
- One weak headline can tank a landing page even if the product, price, and design are all excellent.

## What It Actually Is

Copywriting is the craft of writing words that move people to take action. Not decorative words. Not clever words. Words that sell, convert, click, or sign up. Think of it like this: a copywriter is a salesperson who cannot shake your hand or read your face. All they have are words on a screen.

The distinction matters because most people confuse copywriting with content writing. Content writing informs and entertains. Copywriting persuades and converts. A blog post that teaches you something is content. The button at the bottom of that post that says "Download the full checklist" is copy. Both matter. They work differently.

<Callout type="info">
The word "copy" traces back to old newspaper print shops, where "copy" meant the handwritten text passed to the typesetter. Today it means any text written with a persuasive goal: ads, landing pages, email subject lines, button labels, product descriptions, even push notifications.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters (with data)

The numbers on copy performance are not marginal improvements. They are order-of-magnitude differences.

- **Personalized CTAs convert 202% better** than generic ones, based on a study of 330,000 calls to action ([SmartBlogger, 2024](https://smartblogger.com/copywriting-statistics/)).
- **47% of email recipients** decide whether to open an email based on the subject line alone, before reading a single word of the body ([SmartBlogger, 2024](https://smartblogger.com/copywriting-statistics/)).
- **Landing pages with a single, clear headline** have a 21% higher conversion rate than pages without one.
- **Adding "because" to a request** increases compliance by 34%, even when the reason given is trivial. People respond to reasons.
- **Urgency words like "now"** can increase sales by 332% in direct-response copy ([Codeless, 2025](https://codeless.co/copywriting-statistics/)).
- **73% of people skim** rather than reading web content fully, which means your structure, subheads, and opening lines carry most of the weight.

Copy does not just affect individual conversions. It multiplies the return on every dollar you spend on traffic. If you double your conversion rate with better copy, you effectively halve your cost per acquisition without touching your ad budget.

## How It Works: The AIDA Playbook

The most widely used copywriting framework is AIDA: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. Every piece of copy that works follows this flow, whether it is a 6-word ad headline or a 10-page sales letter.

### Step 1: Attention

Your headline or subject line is a gatekeeper. If it fails, nothing else gets read. Use one of these proven openers:

- A surprising, specific statistic: "73% of visitors leave your page in under 8 seconds."
- A named problem the reader already feels: "Why your landing page gets traffic but no conversions."
- A bold, clear benefit promise: "Write a homepage that converts in 60 minutes."

Avoid vague openers like "Welcome to our website" or "We are excited to announce." Those waste the only moment you have.

### Step 2: Interest

Once you have attention, you must hold it. The fastest way: describe the reader's problem better than they can describe it themselves. When people feel understood, they keep reading.

Do not invent problems. Reflect real ones. Pull language from customer reviews, support tickets, and sales calls. The closer your copy sounds to the reader's internal monologue, the more it resonates.

### Step 3: Desire

Desire is built on benefits, not features. A feature is what the product does. A benefit is what the reader gets from it.

- Feature: "256GB of storage"
- Benefit: "Never delete a photo or app again"

Stack proof alongside benefits: specific numbers, named customer results, before-and-after outcomes. "Thousands of customers" is weak. "4,317 marketers use this every week" is credible.

### Step 4: Action

Every piece of copy needs one clear next step. Not two. Not three. One. Vague CTAs like "Learn more" or "Click here" consistently underperform specific ones like "Get the free checklist" or "Start my free trial."

Research shows that formatting a CTA as a button (rather than a text link) alone increases clicks by 45%. The copy on that button matters too.

<Callout type="tip">
Before writing any copy, write one sentence that answers: "Who is my reader, what do they want right now, and what is the single action I want them to take?" If you cannot write that sentence in under 30 seconds, you are not ready to write the copy yet. Clarity of purpose precedes clarity of words.
</Callout>

## Real Company Examples

### Unbounce: One Word, 90% More Clicks

In a widely cited A/B test, Unbounce changed a single word on a CTA button. The original read: "Start your free 30 day trial." The new version: "Start **my** free 30 day trial."

That swap, "your" to "my," increased click-through rate by 90%. No design change. No price change. No new offer. One pronoun. The reason: "my" creates a sense of ownership before the reader has even clicked. They mentally committed to the trial before taking the action.

<Callout type="example">
Unbounce's "your" vs. "my" CTA test is a foundational case study in copywriting because it isolates the variable perfectly. Everything else stayed the same. One word, one change, 90% lift. It proves that copy is not decoration on top of a product. Copy IS the product experience at the moment of decision.
</Callout>

### Veeam: Rewording a CTA, 167% More Clicks

Veeam Software, a data backup company, tested two versions of a CTA button on their product page. The original said "Buy Now." The new version said "Try Free for 30 Days." Result: a 166.66% increase in click-through rate.

The lesson here is not just about the word "free." It is about reducing the perceived risk of the action. "Buy Now" signals commitment and cost. "Try Free for 30 Days" signals low risk and reversibility. Copy controls how the reader feels about the action, not just what the action is.

<Callout type="example">
Canva ran a similar test on their homepage CTA. Changing button copy from a generic label to benefit-focused text ("Design for free") boosted CTR by 28%. Across three different companies, benefit-focused and low-risk CTA copy consistently outperformed transactional language.
</Callout>

### Email Subject Lines: The $1 Difference

Barack Obama's 2012 campaign team ran over 500 A/B tests on fundraising emails. The highest-performing subject line was not a polished, professional one. It was: "Hey." A single word. Casual. Human. That email generated millions in donations because it felt personal, not mass-marketed.

The takeaway for 2025: the best subject line sounds like it came from a person, not a marketing department.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Writing About Yourself Instead of the Reader

The most common homepage mistake is copy that starts with "We are..." or "Our company was founded in..." The reader does not care yet. They arrived asking "Can this help me?" Answer that question first.

Count the "we" vs. "you" ratio on your homepage. If "we" wins, rewrite it. Every "we provide" can become "you get." Every "our platform does" can become "you can."

### Mistake 2: Burying the Benefit

Many writers save the best for last, the way a presenter builds to a punchline. Readers are not waiting. State your biggest benefit in the headline or the opening sentence. Everything after that is proof and justification.

### Mistake 3: Vague, Weak CTAs

Ending copy without a specific next step is like a sales conversation that ends with "anyway, let me know." The reader should never have to guess what to do. "Learn more" is not a CTA. "Download the 2025 Pricing Guide" is a CTA.

Emails with a single, focused CTA increase clicks by 371% compared to emails with multiple competing links, according to SmartBlogger research.

### Mistake 4: No Specificity

Vague claims destroy trust. "Trusted by thousands" is ignored. "Used by 4,317 marketing teams as of Q1 2025" is believed. Specifics make claims feel real. Use actual numbers, dates, names, and percentages wherever possible.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring Skimmers

73% of readers skim. They read headlines, subheads, bullets, bolded words, and CTAs. They skip paragraphs. Write every piece of copy so that a reader who only reads the bold text and the bullets still gets the full message. If your copy only works when read word-for-word, most readers will miss it.

## Key Takeaways

- **Copy is salesmanship in print.** Every word either moves the reader toward the action or should be cut.
- **AIDA works.** Attention, Interest, Desire, Action is the underlying structure of almost every high-converting piece of copy.
- **Personalization multiplies results.** Personalized CTAs outperform generic ones by 202%. One pronoun change can lift clicks by 90%.
- **Benefits beat features.** What you get beats what it does, every time.
- **Specifics build trust.** Exact numbers outperform vague claims. Always.
- **Structure for skimmers.** 73% of readers skim. Your headlines, bullets, and bolded phrases carry the message for the majority of your audience.
