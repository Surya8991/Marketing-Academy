# B2B Copy: Pain, Proof, Process

## What It Is

B2B copy (business-to-business copywriting) is writing aimed at convincing a company - not a single consumer - to buy a product or service. The audience is not one person. It is usually a buying committee: an average of five decision-makers who each have different concerns, different objections, and different definitions of success.

That committee dynamic changes everything about how you write. B2C (business-to-consumer) copy sells to emotion and speed. B2B copy must sell to logic, reduce perceived risk, and build enough trust that five people can agree to sign a contract worth thousands or millions of dollars.

The three pillars that make B2B copy work are Pain, Proof, and Process. Pain names the problem the buyer already feels. Proof shows that your solution actually solves it. Process explains how the solution works, step by step, so risk-averse buyers feel safe moving forward.

## Real-World Example

Slack launched its enterprise offering in 2019 targeting IT and operations buyers, not end users. Instead of leading with "messaging app," its copy led with pain: "Email overload is costing your team hours a week." It backed that claim with social proof from companies like IBM and Target, then explained the onboarding and security process in plain language. By 2024, Slack's enterprise segment (companies paying over $100,000 per year) had grown to represent more than 40% of its total revenue.

## Why It Matters

According to HubSpot's State of B2B Landing Pages research, pages that acknowledge the customer's pain point before presenting a solution increase form completion rates by 33%. Pages whose headlines directly address pain points convert 45% better than pages that lead with product benefits (HubSpot, 2024).

That gap exists because B2B buyers do not wake up wanting software. They wake up with a problem: a process that is too slow, a team that is burning out, a compliance risk no one has solved. If your copy does not name that problem first, the buyer has no reason to keep reading.

The Pain-Proof-Process framework works because it mirrors how B2B buyers actually make decisions: they recognize a problem, look for evidence that a solution exists, then evaluate whether the vendor can actually deliver it.

## How It Works

**Pain** - Open with the exact problem your buyer is living with. Use their language, not yours. "Manual reporting wastes three hours every Friday" beats "automated reporting solution."

**Proof** - Follow the pain statement with hard evidence: case studies, customer quotes, third-party data, logos of recognizable clients. Proof must match the pain. If you named a specific problem, the case study must show that specific problem being solved.

**Process** - Close with a clear explanation of how your product delivers the result. Buyers fear the unknown. A simple "how it works" section lowers perceived risk and gives champions inside the buying committee something concrete to share with skeptical colleagues.

  B --> C[Stack Proof\nCase study + stat + logo]

Apply this framework at the page level (home page, solution page, landing page) and at the section level. Each section of a long-form B2B page can run its own mini Pain-Proof-Process loop.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common B2B copy mistake is leading with the product instead of the pain. Copy that opens with "Introducing our AI-powered platform" forces the buyer to figure out why they should care. Copy that opens with "Your sales team is losing deals because follow-up emails arrive too late" pulls the buyer in immediately. Always name the pain before you name the product. Generic pain is also a trap: "We help companies grow faster" is not a pain statement, it is a wish. Get specific: team size, industry, exact bottleneck.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**B2B copy does not sell products - it sells relief from a named, specific pain, backed by proof that the relief is real, and a clear process that makes buying feel safe.**
