# Writing the Value Prop

## What It Is

A value proposition (value prop) is a single, clear statement that tells a potential customer exactly what you do, who you do it for, and why it matters more than the alternatives. It is not a slogan. It is not a mission statement. It is the answer to the question every visitor asks the moment they land on your page: "Why should I care?"

Most companies confuse their value prop with a tagline. A tagline is memorable. A value prop is true. Nike's "Just Do It" is a tagline. "Running shoes engineered to reduce knee injury by 30%" is a value prop. One creates emotion; the other earns trust.

A strong value prop sits at the intersection of three things: what your customer wants, what your product delivers, and what no competitor can credibly claim. Miss any one of those three and you are writing marketing copy, not a value proposition.

## Real-World Example

In 2012, Unbounce published A/B test data showing that landing pages with a clear, specific value prop convert at a 34% higher rate than those without one. Basecamp (then 37signals) put this into practice. Their homepage stated: "Basecamp makes project management simple" - followed immediately by a customer count as social proof. That combination - clarity plus credibility - helped them reach 100,000 paying customers by 2014 without a single sales rep.

<Callout type="example">
Basecamp's value prop worked because it was concrete, not vague. "Simple project management" names both the product category and the benefit. They did not say "collaborative synergy platform." They said "simple." That single word choice filtered out the wrong customers and attracted the right ones, cutting their support costs while raising their trial-to-paid conversion rate.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Every dollar you spend on ads, SEO, or social media is wasted if the first sentence someone reads on your page does not answer "what is this and why does it matter to me?" A weak value prop creates bounce. A strong one creates the mental permission to keep reading.

This matters most in three situations: when you are entering a crowded market and need to carve out a distinct position; when your product is genuinely complex and needs to be simplified for a first-time visitor; and when your conversion rate is stuck despite strong traffic. In all three cases, the value prop is usually the root cause.

## How It Works

The most reliable framework for writing a value prop is the "Jobs-to-be-Done" formula combined with Peter Drucker's specificity rule: name the outcome, name the customer, and name the timeframe or proof.

The formula looks like this:

**We help [specific customer] achieve [specific outcome] by [specific mechanism].**

Strip that sentence down to 12 words or fewer and you have your headline.

<Mermaid chart={`flowchart TD
    A["Identify the Job to Be Done\n(What is the customer trying to accomplish?)"] --> B["Name the Specific Outcome\n(Not features - results)"]
    B --> C["Find the Unique Mechanism\n(How only you deliver this)"]
    C --> D["Add Proof or Specificity\n(Number, timeframe, or customer type)"]
    D --> E["Write the 12-Word Headline\nTest it on a stranger in 5 seconds"]`} />

The five-second test is the final filter. Show your draft value prop to someone unfamiliar with your product. After five seconds, ask: "What does this company do?" If they cannot answer correctly, the prop is not clear enough. Clarity always beats cleverness.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The single most common mistake is writing a value prop that describes your product instead of your customer's outcome. "AI-powered workflow automation with real-time collaboration" describes the product. "Ship projects 40% faster without status meetings" describes the outcome. Customers buy outcomes, not features. Rewrite every draft from the customer's point of view and cut any word that refers to how your product works rather than what it achieves.
</Callout>

A close second mistake is trying to appeal to everyone. "For businesses of all sizes" is a red flag in any value prop. The narrower your customer definition, the more that customer will feel understood. A value prop that speaks to "freelance graphic designers who miss deadlines" will outperform one aimed at "creative professionals" every single time.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**A value prop earns the right for your customer to keep reading - if it does not answer "what is this and why does it matter to me" in under five seconds, rewrite it.**
