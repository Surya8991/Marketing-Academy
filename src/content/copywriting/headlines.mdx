# Headline Writing

## What It Is

A headline is the first line of text a reader sees - the title of an ad, email, blog post, or landing page. It does one job: make the reader want to read the next line.

David Ogilvy, one of advertising history's most cited practitioners, said: "On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy." That means if your headline fails, 80% of your ad spend is wasted before anyone reads a single word of your offer.

A good headline is not clever. It is clear. It tells the reader exactly what they will get, who it is for, or what problem it solves - in plain language, in under 10 words when possible.

## Real-World Example

In 2012, Upworthy launched a content site built entirely on headline testing. Their editorial team wrote 25 different headlines for every single article, then A/B tested the top two. Within 12 months, their monthly unique visitors climbed from near zero to 87 million. The content was not unique - the headlines were.

<Callout type="example">
**Upworthy: 87 million monthly visitors in 12 months through headline testing**

Upworthy's rule was simple: write 25 headlines per article, always. Most headlines they wrote were bad. That was the point. By forcing writers to generate 25 options, they pushed past the obvious phrasing and found angles that genuinely created curiosity. The site's traffic proved that headline quality - not content quality alone - drives clicks.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Every piece of marketing has a headline problem. An email subject line is a headline. A Google ad's first line is a headline. A YouTube thumbnail title is a headline. A landing page H1 is a headline.

According to a 2024 survey cited by SEO analysts, 36% of SEO experts say the title tag (the headline in search results) is the single most important on-page SEO element. It affects both whether Google ranks you and whether users click when they do see you.

The business problem headlines solve is attention. You are competing with dozens of other messages every time a reader opens a browser, email inbox, or social feed. A weak headline means you lose that competition before the race starts.

## How It Works

The most reliable headline framework is built on four properties, often called the 4 U's: Useful, Urgent, Unique, and Ultra-specific. A strong headline hits at least two of these. A great one hits all four.

**Useful** - the reader learns something or gets something valuable.
**Urgent** - there is a reason to act or read now, not later.
**Unique** - the angle is different from what competitors say.
**Ultra-specific** - numbers, names, and details replace vague claims.

Weak: "Improve Your Marketing"
Strong: "How 3 Subject Line Changes Lifted Our Email Opens by 42% in 30 Days"

The strong version is useful (it teaches a tactic), urgent (implies a quick result), unique (specific story), and ultra-specific (3 changes, 42%, 30 days).

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**The most common mistake: writing the headline last.**

Most writers draft the body first, then slap a title on top. This produces weak, generic headlines because the writer is tired and just wants to finish. Write your headline first - or write it last but treat it as a separate creative session. Give it the same effort as the entire body copy. If you can only do one thing, spend more time on the headline than on anything else in your copy.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Use numbers whenever you can.** Headlines with numbers outperform those without in almost every test. "7 Ways to Write Better Headlines" beats "How to Write Better Headlines" because numbers signal a concrete, scannable payoff. Odd numbers (3, 7, 9, 11) tend to outperform even numbers in headline tests, according to content marketers at HubSpot and Orbit Media.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Your headline is not decoration - it is the gate every reader must pass through, and most copy fails because the gate is locked.**
