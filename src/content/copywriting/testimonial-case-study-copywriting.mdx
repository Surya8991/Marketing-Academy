# Testimonial & Case Study Copywriting Craft

"Great product, highly recommend" has never sold anything. A number a buyer can picture themselves hitting sells everything.

## Quick Summary

- Generic praise fails because it is not falsifiable, a reader cannot tell if "great service" means anything at all.
- Specificity beats superlatives: one real number outperforms ten adjectives, and [testimonials on sales pages lift conversions by roughly 34% on average](https://wisernotify.com/blog/testimonial-statistics/).
- The strongest case-study copy follows a before, struggle, after arc, not a chronological retelling of the project.
- Getting quotable language requires a live conversation and a specific follow-up question, not a written survey.
- [92.4% of B2B buyers read reviews and case studies before purchasing](https://wisernotify.com/blog/testimonial-statistics/), so the craft of writing them well is a direct revenue lever, not a nice-to-have.

## Why "Great Product!" Doesn't Convert

A generic testimonial makes a claim with no way to check it. "Amazing team, five stars" could describe any vendor in any category, so a skeptical reader's brain simply discounts it.

Specific claims work the opposite way. Involve Digital's own before-and-after copy makes the point plainly: swap "Involve Digital did a great job on our website, highly recommend" for "our enquiry rate went from 0.6% to 2.8%, that's an extra 22 qualified leads per month from the same traffic, and the site paid for itself in 4 months."

The second version is checkable. A reader can picture their own conversion rate moving from 0.6 to 2.8, and that mental rehearsal is what actually persuades.

Superlatives ("best," "incredible," "game-changing") trigger a reader's ad-skepticism reflex. Numbers, timeframes, and named specifics bypass it because they read as reporting, not selling.

<Callout type="warning">
If a testimonial would still be true with the company name blacked out and swapped for a competitor, it is too generic to publish. Specificity is what makes a quote unfakeable.
</Callout>

Three well-structured, specific testimonials do more work than twenty vague ones stacked on a page. Curate for precision, not volume.

## A Narrative Structure for Case-Study Copy

Chronological retellings, "first we did X, then Y, then Z", read like meeting minutes. Buyers do not read minutes; they read stories with tension and resolution.

Use a four-beat arc instead:

1. **Before.** Name the specific, painful starting state: the metric, the workaround, the manual process eating someone's Tuesday afternoons.
2. **Struggle.** What did they try first that did not work? This beat builds credibility, it shows the problem was real, not a strawman built to flatter your product.
3. **Turn.** The moment your product entered and what specifically changed in the workflow, not a features list.
4. **After.** The outcome in the customer's own measurable terms, plus how fast it arrived.

That last beat matters more than most writers realize. Time-to-Value, how quickly the result showed up, has become as persuasive to buyers as the size of the result itself, because a fast win feels lower-risk to commit to.

Write the headline from the "after" beat, never the company name. "From 0.6% to 2.8% enquiry rate in 4 months" earns a click; "How We Helped Acme Redesign Their Site" does not.

## Interviewing Customers to Extract Quotable Language

You cannot write a specific quote from a vague answer. The quote has to come from the customer's own mouth, and getting it there is an interview skill, not a writing skill.

Run the interview as a live call, not an emailed questionnaire. People are more candid and more specific in conversation, and a follow-up question only works in real time.

The single highest-leverage move: when someone says something vague, ask them to make it concrete on the spot.

- They say "it saved us a ton of time." You ask: "Can you put a number on that, hours per week, maybe?"
- They say "the team loves it." You ask: "What's the one thing that changed about a Monday morning now versus before?"
- They say "way easier now." You ask: "Easier compared to what, specifically, what did the old process require?"

<Callout type="tip">
Offer a specific phrase or analogy in your follow-up question, people tend to mirror the language you hand them. Ask "so it went from a fire drill to a non-event?" and you will often get that exact phrase back, quotable as-is.
</Callout>

Record the call with permission. Natural speech produces rhythm and specificity that a written response almost never does, and you can lightly clean up filler words without changing what was actually said.

Never paraphrase a quote into something "punchier." A cleaned-up quote that changes the customer's actual words stops being evidence and starts being your copy wearing their name.

## Key Takeaways

- Generic praise is unfalsifiable and gets discounted by skeptical readers; specific numbers and timeframes are checkable and persuasive.
- Structure case-study copy as before, struggle, turn, after, not a chronological project recap.
- Time-to-Value, how fast the result arrived, is now nearly as persuasive as the size of the result.
- Interview live, ask "can you put a number on that?" whenever you hear vague praise, and mirror language back to prompt quotable phrasing.
- Never paraphrase a quote into something punchier; lightly clean filler, never change meaning.
