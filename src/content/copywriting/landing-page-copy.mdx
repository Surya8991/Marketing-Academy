# Landing Page Copy

## What It Is

A landing page is a standalone web page built for one purpose: to get visitors to take a single action. That action might be signing up for a newsletter, starting a free trial, or buying a product. Landing page copy is the text that persuades them to do it.

What makes landing page copy different from other writing is structure. Every section of the page has a job. The headline hooks attention. The subheadline explains the offer. The body copy handles objections. The call-to-action (CTA) - the button or form - tells the visitor exactly what to do next.

The order matters just as much as the words. Readers scan pages from top to bottom, so the most important persuasion work happens at the top. If the headline does not connect within a few seconds, most visitors leave before reading anything else.

## Real-World Example

Unbounce, a landing page platform, analyzed conversion data across thousands of pages in 2024 and found that pages written at a fifth-to-seventh grade reading level had a median conversion rate of 11.1%. Pages written at a college reading level converted at just 5.3% - a gap of more than two times. Simpler language, shorter sentences, and plain words outperform clever writing almost every time.

## Why It Matters

Most paid advertising sends visitors to a landing page. If the page does not convert, every dollar spent on ads is wasted. Even a modest improvement in conversion rate - say from 4% to 6% - cuts your cost per acquisition by a third without spending more on traffic.

Landing page copy also matters for organic traffic. A blog post or social post that links to a weak landing page bleeds the value of that traffic. Good copy captures the momentum a visitor arrives with and turns it into action.

The business problem landing page copy solves is friction. Visitors arrive curious but skeptical. Copy has to answer three unspoken questions fast: What is this? Why should I care? Why should I trust you?

## How It Works

Think of a landing page as a vertical funnel. Each section qualifies the visitor and moves them one step closer to clicking the CTA. Here is the hierarchy from top to bottom:

<Mermaid chart={`flowchart TD
  A["Headline: Name the benefit or outcome"]
  B["Subheadline: Clarify who it is for and how it works"]
  C["Social Proof: Reviews, logos, or a key stat"]
  D["Features + Benefits: What you get and why it matters"]
  E["Objection Handling: Address the top 2-3 doubts"]
  F["CTA: One clear action with low-friction copy"]
  A --> B
  B --> C
  C --> D
  D --> E
  E --> F`} />

**Headline** - Lead with the outcome the visitor wants, not the name of your product. 'Get your first 100 email subscribers in 30 days' beats 'Welcome to GrowMail.'

**Subheadline** - Add one sentence that names who the product is for and how it delivers the headline promise.

**Social proof** - Credibility borrowed from others. Use real names, real numbers, and real results. A single specific testimonial ('I went from 0 to 1,200 subscribers in three weeks - Priya M., freelance designer') beats five vague ones.

**Features and benefits** - Features describe what the product does. Benefits explain what the visitor gains. Always pair them. 'Automated email sequences (feature) so you can sleep while your list grows (benefit).'

**Objection handling** - Before the visitor clicks, their brain raises doubts: Is this legit? Is it too expensive? Will it work for me? Address the top two or three objections directly. An FAQ section works well here.

**CTA** - One button. One action. Use first-person language: 'Start my free trial' converts better than 'Start your free trial' because the visitor imagines themselves doing it.

<Callout type="tip">
Personalized CTAs - ones that change based on who the visitor is or where they came from - convert 202% better than generic CTAs, according to HubSpot research. Even a small change like swapping 'Get started' for 'Start my free marketing course' can lift clicks meaningfully.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The single most common mistake is writing for yourself instead of the visitor. Founders and marketers love talking about their product's features, their company story, and their technology. Visitors only care about one thing: what is in it for them. Read every sentence and ask: does this tell the visitor what they gain? If the answer is no, rewrite it or cut it. A page that opens with 'We built this because we believed...' will lose to one that opens with 'Double your sign-up rate in two weeks.'
</Callout>

A related mistake is having more than one CTA. Every extra button or link is a decision the visitor has to make. More decisions mean more drop-off. Pick one action and design the entire page around it.

Finally, do not bury the headline promise. Many pages start with a navigation bar, a banner image, and a tagline before the real value proposition appears. Move the strongest benefit to the very first line a visitor reads.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Landing page copy works top to bottom: a headline that names the outcome, social proof that builds trust, and a single CTA that removes all friction.**
