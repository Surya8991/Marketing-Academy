# Landing Page Copy

In 2025, the median landing page converts at just 6.6% across industries. The gap between a page that converts at 4% and one that converts at 12% is almost never the product or the traffic source. It is the copy.

## Quick Summary

- Every section of a landing page has one job. Headline hooks, subheadline clarifies, social proof builds trust, body copy handles objections, CTA converts.
- Copy written at a 5th-to-7th grade reading level converts at 11.1%. College-level copy converts at 5.3% (Unbounce, 2024).
- Mobile accounts for 82.9% of landing page traffic (Unbounce), so short sentences and scannable structure are not optional.
- One CTA per page. Every extra link or button is a decision that causes drop-off.
- Personalized CTAs convert 202% better than generic ones (HubSpot research).

## What It Actually Is

Landing page copy is the text on a standalone web page built for a single conversion goal. That goal might be a free trial signup, a lead form submission, a webinar registration, or a direct purchase. Unlike a homepage or blog post, a landing page does not try to do multiple things. It drives one action.

Think of it like a sales conversation compressed into a page. A good salesperson does not open with a company history. They lead with the problem the prospect has, show they understand it, prove they can solve it, and ask for the next step. Landing page copy follows the exact same arc, just in written form and without the back-and-forth.

## Why It Matters (with data)

The business case for good landing page copy is straightforward: it multiplies the value of every dollar spent on traffic.

- The median landing page conversion rate is 6.6%, but the top 10% of pages convert at 11.45% or higher, according to [Unbounce's Q4 2024 benchmark data](https://unbounce.com/conversion-benchmark-report/). The difference between median and top-decile performance is almost entirely copy and layout, not ad spend.
- [Backlinko's 2025 landing page statistics roundup](https://backlinko.com/landing-page-stats) reports that email traffic converts at 19.3%, which is 77% higher than paid search traffic. The copy has to match the promise in whatever channel sent the visitor.
- A/B testing headline copy alone can lift conversion rates by up to 20%, according to [GetResponse's 2025 copywriting guide](https://www.getresponse.com/blog/copywriting-landing-page-conversions).
- 48% of visitors exit without engaging with any marketing materials on the page (Gartner). Most of those exits happen in the first five seconds, which is entirely a headline problem.
- Webinar landing pages average 22.3% conversion (GetResponse), in part because the copy sets up a specific, time-bound benefit that is easy to say yes to.

<Callout type="example">
Unbounce analyzed thousands of real landing pages for its 2024 Conversion Benchmark Report and found that pages written at a 5th-to-7th grade reading level converted at a median of 11.1%. Pages written at a college reading level converted at 5.3%. The product was identical. Only the copy changed. Simpler words, shorter sentences, and bullet points over paragraphs more than doubled conversion rates.
</Callout>

## How It Works: The Playbook

Think of a landing page as a vertical funnel. Each section does a specific job, and they must appear in order. Skip one or reorder them and the persuasion chain breaks.

### Step 1: Headline

The headline is the single most important line on the page. If it does not hook the visitor within three to five seconds, they leave. Lead with the outcome the visitor wants, not the name of your product or company.

- Weak: "Welcome to GrowMail"
- Strong: "Get your first 1,000 email subscribers in 30 days"

The strongest headlines name a specific result, a specific audience, or a specific timeframe. All three together is ideal. Keep it to one line if possible.

### Step 2: Subheadline

The subheadline has one job: answer the question the headline raised. It names who the product is for and gives a brief mechanism for how it delivers the headline promise. One or two sentences maximum.

Example: "GrowMail gives freelance designers a done-for-you email sequence that runs automatically, so you grow your list without spending hours writing."

### Step 3: Social Proof

Visitors arrive skeptical. Social proof borrows credibility from others who have already taken the risk. Effective social proof is specific, not vague.

- Weak: "Thousands of happy customers!"
- Strong: "Priya M., freelance designer: 'I went from 0 to 1,200 subscribers in three weeks using GrowMail's starter sequence.'"

Use real names, real results, and real numbers. Company logos, review counts, press mentions, and case study stats all work. Place at least one social proof element above the fold, before the visitor has to scroll.

### Step 4: Features and Benefits

Features describe what the product does. Benefits explain what the visitor gains. Always pair them. Write the feature, then add "so you can" or "which means" and finish with the benefit.

- "Automated email sequences (feature) so you can grow your list while you sleep (benefit)."
- "Mobile-optimized templates (feature), which means your emails look great on the 82.9% of phones that open them (benefit)."

Use bullets, not paragraphs, for this section. Visitors scan before they read.

### Step 5: Objection Handling

Before a visitor clicks the CTA, their brain raises objections. Is this legit? Is it worth the money? Will it work for someone like me? An FAQ section or a brief trust block directly addresses the top two or three doubts.

Common objections to address:
- Price: "Is there a free plan?" or "What does it cost?"
- Risk: "Can I cancel anytime?" or "Is my data safe?"
- Fit: "Does this work for [my industry / my size / my situation]?"
- Effort: "How long does setup take?"

Naming the objection and answering it directly is far more persuasive than ignoring it.

### Step 6: CTA

One button. One action. Everything else on the page exists to earn this click.

Use first-person language. "Start my free trial" consistently outperforms "Start your free trial" because the visitor mentally rehearses doing it. "Download my free guide" beats "Download the guide."

Make the CTA specific to what the visitor gets, not what they have to do:
- Generic: "Submit"
- Better: "Start my free 14-day trial"
- Best: "Get instant access to 30 email templates"

<Callout type="tip">
HubSpot research found that personalized CTAs, ones that change based on where the visitor came from or who they are, convert 202% better than generic CTAs. Even a simple swap from "Get started" to "Start my free design course" can lift clicks meaningfully. If your platform supports dynamic text replacement, use it.
</Callout>

## Real Company Examples

### Shopify's Signup Page

Shopify's primary signup landing page has used variations of "Start your free trial" as the headline CTA for years. In 2024, they shifted the hero copy to lead with the outcome ("Sell anywhere. Build your business.") rather than the product name. Their above-the-fold section includes a single email field and one CTA button, no navigation links, and a line of social proof naming the number of businesses using the platform. The page removes every distraction and routes all attention to one action.

### Duolingo's Language Learning Page

Duolingo's landing pages for paid conversion use a consistent structure: a benefit-first headline ("Learn a language in just 5 minutes a day"), a short subheadline naming the mechanism ("Our bite-sized lessons make it easy"), star ratings and download counts as social proof, three bullet-point benefits, and a single CTA ("Get started for free"). In 2024 A/B tests reported in industry case studies, Duolingo found that removing the secondary navigation from landing pages increased trial signups by 18% because visitors had no exit ramp before the CTA.

<Callout type="example">
GetResponse tracked conversion data across webinar landing pages in 2024 and found they averaged 22.3% conversion, versus a 6.6% median across all page types. The common factor was copy structure: a specific outcome headline ("Learn to run Facebook ads profitably in 60 minutes"), a named deadline (date and time), a short bullet list of what attendees would walk away with, and a single registration CTA. The specificity of the outcome and the time constraint did the persuasion work.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

**1. Writing about the product instead of the visitor**

The most common error is copy that leads with the company story, the founding team, or the product's features. Visitors only care about one thing: what is in it for them. Audit every sentence. If it does not tell the visitor what they gain, cut it or rewrite it. A page that opens with "We built this because we believed..." will lose to one that opens with "Double your email list in 30 days."

**2. Multiple CTAs**

Every extra button or link forces a decision. More decisions cause drop-off. Pages with two CTAs do not double conversions; they split attention and dilute the primary action. Pick one goal and build every word around it. If you have secondary goals (like getting visitors to read a blog post), those belong on a different page.

**3. Burying the value proposition**

Many pages start with a navigation bar, a hero image, and a tagline before the real benefit appears. By the time the value proposition shows up, many visitors have already left. Move the headline benefit to the very first element the visitor reads. Above the fold should answer: "What is this, and why should I care?"

**4. Vague social proof**

"Trusted by thousands" is not social proof. It is a claim. Real social proof includes a specific person, a specific result, and a specific timeframe. "Trusted by 14,000 freelancers" is better. "Trusted by 14,000 freelancers, including Priya M. who grew from 0 to 1,200 subscribers in three weeks" is far better.

**5. Ignoring reading level**

Clever, complex writing feels like a signal of quality to the writer. To the visitor scanning a page on their phone, it is friction. According to Unbounce's 2024 data, moving from college-level to 5th-to-7th grade reading level more than doubles median conversion rate. Use short sentences. Use common words. Break paragraphs into bullets.

## Key Takeaways

- Headline first: name the outcome the visitor wants, not your product name.
- Simple language converts. 5th-to-7th grade reading level outperforms college-level copy by more than 2x (Unbounce, 2024).
- One CTA per page. Every extra link is a leak.
- Social proof must be specific: a real name, a real number, a real timeframe.
- Address objections directly in the copy. Naming a doubt and answering it is more persuasive than pretending the doubt does not exist.
- Mobile is the majority: 82.9% of landing page traffic is on phones. Write for scanning, not reading.
