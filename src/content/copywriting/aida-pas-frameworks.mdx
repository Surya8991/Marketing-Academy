# AIDA, PAS, and the Core Frameworks

## What It Is

Copywriting frameworks are fill-in-the-blank structures that guide a reader from "I just noticed this" to "I want to buy this." Instead of starting with a blank page, you follow a proven sequence of steps that match how people actually make decisions.

The two most widely used frameworks are AIDA and PAS. AIDA stands for Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. PAS stands for Problem, Agitate, Solution. Both have been in use for over a century, and both still appear in ads, landing pages, emails, and product pages you see every day.

These frameworks are not magic formulas. They are thinking tools. Learning them trains your eye to spot what a piece of copy is missing and gives you a reliable starting point when you face a deadline.

## Real-World Example

Dollar Shave Club launched its now-famous video ad in 2012 using a near-perfect AIDA structure. The opening line grabbed Attention ("Our blades are f***ing great"), the video held Interest with humor and a factory tour, Desire was built by showing the low price and convenience, and the final screen drove Action with a clear URL. The video cost roughly $4,500 to produce and drove 12,000 new subscribers within 48 hours of launch.

## Why It Matters

Most copy fails not because the writer lacks talent but because it skips a stage. A headline grabs Attention, then jumps straight to a price (no Interest, no Desire). Or a blog post spends three paragraphs on a problem but never offers a clear Solution. Frameworks prevent these gaps.

A 2024 SparkToro and Semrush study found that 58.5% of Google searches ended without a single external click. That means readers are making snap judgments faster than ever. A framework ensures your most important hook lands in the first line, not the third paragraph.

Use AIDA when you are introducing a product or brand to someone who does not know you yet. Use PAS when your reader already feels a pain point and you want to move them to a solution quickly. Both frameworks work for emails, ads, landing pages, and social posts.

## How It Works

**AIDA - four stages:**

1. **Attention** - Stop the scroll. Use a bold headline, a surprising stat, or a direct question.
2. **Interest** - Keep them reading. Show you understand their situation with specifics.
3. **Desire** - Make them want the outcome. Use benefits, testimonials, and vivid results.
4. **Action** - Tell them exactly what to do next. One clear call to action.

**PAS - three stages:**

1. **Problem** - Name the exact pain the reader feels right now.
2. **Agitate** - Twist the knife. Show what happens if the problem goes unsolved.
3. **Solution** - Present your offer as the clear, logical answer.

Two other frameworks worth knowing:

- **BAB (Before, After, Bridge):** Describe life before your product, paint the better life after, then explain how your product bridges the gap.
- **4Ps (Promise, Picture, Proof, Push):** Make a promise, paint a picture of the result, provide proof, push with a call to action.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake is skipping the Agitate stage in PAS. Writers name the problem and jump straight to the solution, leaving the reader feeling no urgency. Agitation is not about being negative - it is about helping the reader feel how much the problem is costing them in time, money, or stress. Without it, your solution feels like a nice-to-have instead of a must-have.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Pick a framework before you write a single word, because structure is what turns good ideas into copy that converts.**
