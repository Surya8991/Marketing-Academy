# Comparison Page Copywriting (Us vs Them)

Someone searching "[Your Category] vs [Competitor]" has already decided to buy something. Your job is not to convince them, it is to not blow the trust they walked in with.

## Quick Summary

- Comparison pages target buyers actively evaluating options, the highest-intent traffic your site will ever see, so the copy has to earn trust fast or lose the sale instantly.
- The single biggest failure mode is obvious bias: readers can smell a rigged comparison in seconds and leave for a review site instead.
- [Honest comparisons that acknowledge real tradeoffs convert better than pages that claim superiority on every dimension](https://grizzle.io/blog/comparison-pages).
- Specificity beats adjectives, "processes 10,000 requests per second" is a fact, "blazing fast" is marketing noise.
- Structure the table so a skimmer gets the verdict in five seconds, then let the prose underneath do the persuading.

## Why Comparison Pages Convert, and Why They Backfire

A visitor typing "Slack alternatives" already dislikes their current tool. They are not being educated about a category, they are choosing between two or three named options this week.

That is why comparison pages punch so far above their traffic volume. [Single-goal landing pages with one focused CTA convert at roughly 13.5%, well above multi-CTA pages](https://www.shno.co/marketing-statistics/funnel-optimization-statistics), and comparison content is inherently single-goal: pick us, or at least shortlist us.

But high intent cuts both ways. A reader this close to a decision is also actively hunting for reasons to distrust you.

The moment your copy calls the competitor's pricing "confusing" while calling your own identical pricing "flexible," the reader notices. Not consciously, maybe, but they notice, and the page stops being useful to them.

<Callout type="warning">
The tightrope is real: you must mention the competitor fairly enough to be believed, while still being persuasive enough to be worth publishing. Split the difference and you get a page nobody trusts and nobody acts on.
</Callout>

That tension is the entire craft of this format. Everything below is about walking that line without falling off either side.

## Structuring the Table and the Copy Around It

Readers scan the comparison table first and read prose second, so the table has to survive on its own before a single paragraph backs it up.

**Pick criteria your buyer actually cares about**, not ones engineered to make you win. If price-per-seat matters to your buyer and you lose on it, put it in the table anyway. Hiding a criterion you lose on is more damaging than losing it visibly, because someone will find it on G2 in the next tab.

**Use plain checkmarks and short phrases, not paragraphs, inside cells.** A cell that says "Real-time sync across unlimited workspaces" beats one that says "Yes."

**Give the competitor real credit somewhere above the fold.** [Naming the specific buyer the competitor genuinely serves better, e.g. "if you just need basic scheduling, they're probably enough", builds more credibility than pretending you win everything](https://www.getpassionfruit.com/blog/b2b-comparison-pages-and-alternatives-seo-framework-examples).

Below the table, use short "yes, but" sections instead of one long verdict paragraph:

1. **Where they win.** State it plainly, in one sentence, no hedging.
2. **Where you win, and for whom.** Tie it to a use case, not an adjective. "Teams above 50 seats" beats "enterprises."
3. **The switching cost.** Address migration, data export, or contract lock-in before the reader has to ask.

End the table section with the one line that matters most: who should NOT choose you. That line, oddly, is what makes the rest of the page believable.

## Common Mistakes That Make Pages Feel Like Biased Ads

Most comparison pages fail the same way: they read like an internal sales deck that got exported to HTML.

**Loaded adjectives on one side only.** If your onboarding is "intuitive" and theirs is "basic" for the same feature set, you have written an ad, not a comparison. [Applying the same descriptive standard to every product being compared is what separates a comparison page from a competitor takedown](https://www.getpassionfruit.com/blog/b2b-comparison-pages-and-alternatives-seo-framework-examples).

**Vague superlatives instead of numbers.** "The #1 alternative" convinces nobody. A number the reader can verify, a benchmark, a customer count, a specific feature gap, is the only kind of claim that survives scrutiny.

**Omitting a criterion you lose on.** This is the fastest way to lose all remaining trust, because the reader assumes everything else on the page is equally curated.

**Claiming superiority on every single row.** No real product wins every category against every competitor. [Content that explicitly acknowledges tradeoffs reads as self-aware and accurate; content claiming wins everywhere reads as spin, to human readers and increasingly to AI answer engines evaluating comparison content too](https://www.unusual.ai/blog/how-to-write-comparison-content-that-ai-models-trust).

<Callout type="tip">
Write the page as if the competitor's own marketing team will read it and grudgingly admit it is fair. If they would call it a hit piece, so will your prospects.
</Callout>

Fix these four and the page stops sounding like an ad. It starts sounding like the most useful thing the buyer read all week, which is exactly the reputation that compounds into organic links and repeat traffic over time.

<Callout type="example">
Chili Piper's comparison page against Calendly openly states that basic scheduling needs are served fine by Calendly, then pivots to why complex, multi-person B2B routing is where Chili Piper's product exists. That single admission does more trust-building work than a dozen feature checkmarks.
</Callout>

## Key Takeaways

- Comparison-page visitors are bottom-of-funnel and already comparing named options, so the copy's job is credibility, not persuasion from zero.
- Acknowledge real competitor strengths and name who should pick the competitor instead. That honesty is what makes your wins believable.
- Use identical descriptive language for both products in the table. Loaded adjectives on one side only are the fastest way to lose trust.
- Replace vague superlatives with verifiable numbers everywhere you can.
- Never hide a criterion you lose on. Readers check other sources within the same browsing session.
