# Email Copywriting

## What It Is

Email copywriting is the craft of writing the words inside a marketing email: the subject line, preheader text, body copy, and call-to-action (CTA) button. Each section has a single job. The subject line earns the open. The preheader supports it. The body builds the case. The CTA converts the reader.

Most people treat email copy as one block of text. It is not. It is a chain of small decisions, and a weak link at any point breaks the whole sequence. A brilliant body copy paragraph cannot save a boring subject line because the email never gets opened. A compelling subject line cannot save a vague CTA because the reader does not know what to do next.

The goal of email copywriting is not to write well in the literary sense. The goal is to move the reader one step forward: from inbox to open, from open to read, from read to click.

## Real-World Example

In 2023, Grammarly ran a reactivation email campaign targeting lapsed free-tier users. The subject line read: "You have unused writing suggestions." It referenced something specific the user had already started - their own drafts. The preheader added: "Pick up where you left off." The body was three sentences. The CTA button said "See my suggestions." The campaign achieved a 42% open rate, well above the 2024 industry average of 37.72% reported by GetResponse.

<Callout type="example">
**Grammarly Reactivation Email (2023):** The subject line referenced the user's own data ("You have unused writing suggestions"), which created personal relevance without feeling invasive. The preheader directly continued the thought. The body was under 50 words. The CTA button used first-person language ("See my suggestions") instead of generic text like "Click here." Specificity did all the work.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Email is still the highest-return channel in digital marketing. According to GetResponse's 2024 benchmarks, the average email click-through rate sits at 2.00%, but campaigns with strong copy and clear CTAs consistently outperform that floor. With over 4 billion email users globally, even small improvements in open rate or CTR produce large revenue swings.

This matters most when:
- You are running a product launch and need a sequence that builds anticipation over several days
- You have a list of leads who signed up but have not purchased
- You are running a sale and every hour of the campaign window has a cost to it
- You need to re-engage subscribers who have gone quiet

Bad email copy wastes the list you spent months or years building. Good copy treats that list as an asset and gives every subscriber a reason to keep reading.

## How It Works

The four-part structure of a marketing email works in sequence. Each part has one job and hands off to the next.

**Subject line:** Keep it under 50 characters so it does not get cut off on mobile. Make a specific promise or ask a specific question. Avoid all-caps and excessive punctuation marks - spam filters flag them and readers ignore them.

**Preheader text:** This is the preview line that appears next to the subject line in most inboxes. Most senders leave it blank or let the email client pull the first line of body copy. Write it intentionally. Use it to add a second reason to open the email, not to repeat the subject line.

**Body copy:** Open with the most important sentence. Readers scan before they read. Use short paragraphs - two to three sentences maximum. Write like you are talking to one person, not a segment of thousands. Use "you" more than "we." Make one core argument, not five.

**CTA button:** Use action verbs: "Download," "Start," "Get," "Book." Add specificity where you can: "Get my free guide" beats "Download now." Place the CTA above the scroll point so readers do not have to hunt for it.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**The most common mistake is writing a subject line that matches the body copy too closely.** If the subject line says "50% off everything this weekend" and the email body says the same thing, the reader learns nothing new by opening it. Treat the subject line as a teaser - reveal just enough to earn the click, then let the body deliver the payoff. Also avoid "clever" subject lines that sacrifice clarity. A subject line that makes the reader guess what the email is about will be skipped, even if the offer inside is strong.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Test one variable at a time.** If you change the subject line, the preheader, the CTA color, and the body length all in the same send, you will not know what drove any change in results. Run A/B tests on your subject line first - it affects whether anyone reads anything else. Once you have a winning subject line formula, move to the CTA.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Your subject line decides whether the rest of your copy gets read, so write it last - after you know exactly what the email delivers.**
