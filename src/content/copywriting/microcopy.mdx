# Microcopy: Buttons, Forms, Empty States

In 2025, the difference between a 60% cart abandonment rate and a 45% one often comes down to six words beneath a checkout button. Microcopy is the smallest lever in your product, and it moves the biggest weight.

## Quick Summary

- Microcopy is any short, functional text in a UI: button labels, form hints, error messages, placeholder text, empty state copy, and confirmation messages.
- Expedia removed one optional form field and gained $12 million in annual revenue. MailChimp changed "Submit" to "Send Campaign" and saw an 18% jump in click-through rate.
- The three highest-leverage surfaces are buttons (outcome specificity), forms (inline reassurance), and empty states (the explain-and-invite pattern).
- Baymard Institute research shows 31% of websites still lack live inline validation on forms, a fixable gap that directly raises error rates and abandonment.
- Good microcopy is invisible when it works. Users only notice it when it is wrong, confusing, or missing.

## What It Actually Is

Microcopy is every piece of short, functional text that lives inside a product interface rather than on a marketing page. It includes button labels, input field hints, error messages, placeholder text, tooltip copy, empty state messages, confirmation dialogs, and loading state text.

Think of it as the signage inside a building. You do not go to a mall to read the signs, but without them you get lost, confused, and eventually leave. Microcopy is the signage that keeps users oriented at every decision point.

The term was coined by Joshua Porter in 2009 after he found that adding a single sentence near a credit card field, explaining why the site was asking for a billing address, cut payment errors dramatically. That observation created a discipline: every label, tooltip, and confirmation message is copy worth testing and refining, not just a default placeholder.

## Why It Matters (with data)

The numbers make the case:

- **Cart abandonment sits at roughly 70% globally** according to Baymard Institute's aggregate research across hundreds of studies. A meaningful share of that is form friction, not price objection.
- **Expedia removed one optional "Company Name" field** from their booking form and revenue increased by $12 million per year, as documented by UX researcher Jared Spool. One unnecessary field was costing them that much.
- **MailChimp's button test** found that changing "Submit" to "Send Campaign" raised click-through rate by 18%. The new label told users exactly what would happen next.
- **Michael Aagaard's research** across multiple e-commerce A/B tests found that outcome-specific button labels can increase conversions by up to 17.18% compared to generic labels.
- **Baymard's 2024 checkout usability study** found that 31% of top e-commerce sites still lack live inline validation on form fields, and 4% implement it in a way that actively confuses users. These are known fixes with measurable impact that most sites have not shipped.
- A reassurance line ("We will never share your email") placed directly below an email field, rather than in the footer, consistently lifts form completion rates in B2B SaaS sign-up tests by 15-20% according to [Brand Vision's UX writing research](https://www.brandvm.com/post/ux-writing-conversion-microcopy).

The pattern is consistent: small text changes at friction points produce outsized results because they address doubt at the exact moment it forms.

### Buttons: Write the Outcome, Not the Action

Generic labels like "Submit," "Click here," or "Continue" describe what the user does, not what they get. Outcome-specific labels describe what the user receives after clicking.

The test: read only the button label. Can you tell what will happen next? If not, rewrite.

| Generic label | Outcome-specific label |
|---|---|
| Submit | Get my free audit |
| Sign up | Start my 14-day trial |
| Download | Download the 12-page PDF |
| Continue | Save and go to shipping |
| Learn more | See how it works (2 min) |

The "first-person" framing ("Get my free guide" rather than "Get your free guide") has shown small but consistent lifts in multiple tests, because it makes the user feel like they are choosing rather than being instructed.

### Forms: The Three-Layer Stack

Every sensitive or non-obvious field needs three pieces of copy stacked in order:

1. **Label** (above the field, always visible, describes what goes in the field)
2. **Helper text** (below the field, format guidance or why you are asking)
3. **Reassurance copy** (below helper text, addresses the fear the field triggers)

Do not use placeholder text as the only hint. Placeholder text disappears when the user clicks into the field. If they forget the format requirement and make an error, the hint is gone when they need it most. Placeholder text is decoration; helper text is functional.

For email fields specifically: the reassurance copy that works is specific, not vague. "We will never share your email with third parties" outperforms "No spam" because it names the actual fear.

### Empty States: Acknowledge, Explain, Invite

An empty state is what a user sees when they first sign up, when a search returns zero results, or when a list has no items. A blank screen with no copy reads as a broken product.

Good empty state copy follows three steps:

1. **Acknowledge** the empty state directly and honestly
2. **Explain** why it is empty (first visit, no results, filtered too narrowly)
3. **Invite** one specific action to fill it

Example - bad: *[blank screen]*

Example - better: "Nothing here yet. Import your first contacts to get started." with a single "Import contacts" button.

The invite must be a single action. Offering three options in an empty state creates decision paralysis at the worst possible moment.

### Error Messages: Name, Explain, Fix

The Baymard Institute found that error message content directly affects how quickly users recover and whether they give up. The three-part structure for every error:

1. **Name** the error specifically ("Card number is too short" not "Invalid card")
2. **Explain** what caused it if it is not obvious ("We need 16 digits, and yours has 13")
3. **Fix it** by telling them exactly what to do ("Check the number on the front of your card and re-enter it")

Never use all-caps error labels, exclamation points, or language that implies the user is at fault ("You entered the wrong password" vs. "That password does not match our records").

<Callout type="tip">
**The outcome test for every button:** after writing a button label, finish the sentence "I want to ___." If the button label is the natural answer, it passes. "I want to submit" fails. "I want to get my free audit" passes. Run this test before shipping any CTA.
</Callout>

## Real Company Examples

### Expedia: $12 Million from Removing a Field

Expedia's booking flow included an optional "Company Name" field. Users who saw it were confused about whether it was required for their booking. Many filled it in incorrectly or stalled. UX researcher Jared Spool documented that when Expedia removed the field entirely, annual revenue increased by $12 million. No redesign, no price change: one fewer field, one fewer hesitation point.

The lesson: microcopy is not just about what you add. Sometimes it is about what you remove to eliminate confusion entirely.

<Callout type="example">
**Expedia field removal: +$12M annual revenue**

One optional form field, "Company Name," was confusing users who were booking personal travel. They could not tell if it was required or what it meant for their reservation. Removing it eliminated the confusion entirely. The conversion gain translated to $12 million per year. This is the highest-documented ROI for a single UX copy decision on record.
</Callout>

### MailChimp: 18% CTR Lift from a Button Label

MailChimp's email send button previously read "Submit." Their team changed it to "Send Campaign" - two words that tell users exactly what will happen when they click. Click-through rate on that button rose 18%. The stakes of the action (sending an email to thousands of subscribers) made users hesitate at "Submit." "Send Campaign" confirmed they were doing the right thing, which removed the hesitation.

### Yoast: 11.3% Checkout Conversion Lift

Yoast, the WordPress SEO plugin company, tested two microcopy additions on their checkout page in 2023. They added "there will be no additional costs" near the order summary, and a "continue shopping" text link below the primary CTA button. No layout change, no price change. Completed purchases rose 11.3%. The copy addressed the two most common checkout fears directly: hidden fees and commitment anxiety.

<Callout type="example">
**Yoast checkout test (2023): +11.3% completed purchases**

Two phrases targeted two fears. "There will be no additional costs" addressed the hidden fee worry that causes users to abandon carts moments before payment. "Continue shopping" gave users a visible exit that reduced the perceived commitment of clicking the primary button. Both together produced an 11.3% lift. The insight: anxiety at checkout is usually about what users cannot see, not what they can.
</Callout>

### Bohme: Friction Reduction at Cart

Apparel retailer Bohme added one line to their cart page: "Undecided? Don not worry. You have 21 days to get your money back." The line appeared directly below the checkout button, at the exact moment purchase commitment anxiety peaks. This addresses the return-policy fear without requiring users to search for a return policy page. Specificity matters: "21 days" is more reassuring than "easy returns."

### Magic Spoon: Trust at the Decision Point

Cereal brand Magic Spoon places "Try risk-free, 100% happiness guaranteed" directly beneath their add-to-cart button. The placement is deliberate: the reassurance copy appears at the exact moment users are deciding whether to convert. Moving it to the footer would lose most of its impact. Proximity to the CTA is as important as the words themselves.

## Common Mistakes

**1. Writing for the company, not the user.**
"Submit your information" describes what the company receives. "Get your free audit" describes what the user gets. Every button label and confirmation message should be written from the user's perspective. Ask: what does the user walk away with? Lead with that.

**2. Using placeholder text as the only field hint.**
Placeholder text disappears on click. Users forget format requirements, make errors, and have no hint available when they need it most. Use a persistent label above the field and a helper text line below it. Placeholder text can reinforce format (e.g., "MM/DD/YYYY") but should never carry essential information.

**3. Generic error messages.**
"Something went wrong" tells users nothing. It does not name the error, explain the cause, or tell them how to fix it. Generic error messages increase support tickets and abandonment rates in equal measure. Every error message should answer three questions: what went wrong, why, and what to do next.

**4. Burying reassurance copy in footers.**
Privacy notes, return policy summaries, and "cancel anytime" language have zero impact in the footer. They work when placed directly below the field or button they relate to, at the exact moment the user is experiencing the doubt that copy addresses.

**5. Empty states with no action.**
A blank screen with one sentence of explanation but no button is a dead end. Users land there, read the explanation, and have nowhere to go. Every empty state needs one specific action that moves the user forward. Not two or three options - one clear next step.

**6. Testing only headlines, never microcopy.**
Most A/B testing programs focus on hero copy and page headlines. The conversion gains from microcopy tests (button labels, form hints, error messages) are often larger per-effort because they target users at the moment of decision, not the moment of arrival. Add one microcopy test per sprint.

## Key Takeaways

- Microcopy is signage, not decoration. Users rely on it to navigate friction points without realizing it is there.
- Button labels should name what the user gets, not what they do. "Get my free report" beats "Submit" every time.
- Helper text belongs below the field in permanent view. Placeholder text belongs only as a format hint, never as the only instruction.
- Reassurance copy cuts abandonment when it sits next to the button or field it relates to, not in the footer.
- Every empty state needs three things: acknowledge, explain, invite - and the invite must be a single action.
- Error messages that name, explain, and instruct cut retry rates and abandonment. Error messages that say "invalid input" do not.
- Expedia proved that removing copy (a confusing optional field) can be worth more than adding it. Audit what you can cut.
