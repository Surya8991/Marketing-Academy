# AARRR: Pirate Metrics

AARRR (pronounced like a pirate's growl, which is why it's called "Pirate Metrics") is a five-stage framework for understanding your users' entire journey. Venture capitalist Dave McClure introduced it in 2007, and it has since become the single most popular lens for thinking about growth in startups and digital businesses.

The five stages are: **Acquisition** (how people find you), **Activation** (their first "wow" moment), **Retention** (whether they return), **Referral** (whether they bring others), and **Revenue** (whether they pay). Each stage is a gate - users either pass through or drop off.

## Quick Summary

- AARRR gives you a clear map of where users are dropping off, so you stop guessing at what to fix.
- Most teams over-invest in Acquisition while ignoring Activation and Retention - the real growth killers.
- Fixing a mid-funnel leak (like Activation) compounds through every stage below it, for free.
- Facebook's activation insight (7 friends in 10 days) and Dropbox's referral loop are textbook examples of optimizing a single stage to explosive effect.
- The right order to fix stages is: Retention first, then Activation, then Referral, then Revenue, then Acquisition last.

<Callout type="info">
**Why the order matters:** Dave McClure's original advice was to work the funnel in reverse. If you fix Retention first, every user you acquire and activate has a reason to stay. If you fix Acquisition first without fixing Retention, you're pouring water into a leaky bucket.
</Callout>

## The Five Stages in Plain English

Think of AARRR as a series of doors. Each door a user walks through means they've committed a little more to your product. Your job is to make each door as easy as possible to open.

### Stage 1 - Acquisition: How do people find you?

Acquisition is any channel that brings a new user to your product for the first time. This includes Google search (SEO), paid ads, social media, press coverage, word of mouth, or a friend's recommendation.

The key metric is not just "how many visitors" but **cost per acquisition (CPA) by channel** - meaning, how much did it cost to bring one person in through each specific channel? A blog post that brings in 500 visitors for $200 of writing time has a CPA of $0.40. A paid ad that brings in 500 visitors for $2,000 has a CPA of $4.00. AARRR forces you to compare these honestly.

**What to track:** visitors by channel, CPA by channel, conversion rate from visitor to signup.

### Stage 2 - Activation: Did they have a great first experience?

Activation is the moment a new user first feels the value of your product - their "aha moment." This is not just signing up. It's actually doing the thing that makes the product worth using.

For Facebook, research in the early days showed that users who added 7 friends within their first 10 days were dramatically more likely to stay. That was Facebook's activation event. For Slack, the team discovered that once a team sent 2,000 messages, they considered themselves genuinely activated. For a fitness app like Runna, completing the first workout is the activation milestone.

Activation is often the **highest-leverage stage in the entire funnel.** Here's why: if 20% of your signups activate today, and you improve that to 40%, every stage below it - Retention, Referral, Revenue - doubles in throughput without you spending a single extra dollar on ads.

**What to track:** percentage of new signups who complete the activation event within 24 hours, 3 days, and 7 days.

### Stage 3 - Retention: Do they come back?

Retention measures whether users return after their first session. It is typically tracked as Day 1, Day 7, and Day 30 retention - the percentage of users who open your product one day, one week, and one month after signup.

Low Day 30 retention is the clearest sign of a product problem. If users try your product once and never return, no amount of marketing spend will save you. This is the "leaky bucket" situation: you pour users in at the top, they drain out the bottom.

Calm, the meditation app, measures retention by tracking daily meditation sessions. The goal is habit formation - if users meditate daily for two weeks, they are very likely to keep using Calm. Apple Fitness+ runs programs like "Time to Walk" specifically to create recurring engagement touchpoints rather than one-off sessions.

**What to track:** Day 1, Day 7, Day 30 retention rates; churn rate (for subscription products); session frequency per week.

### Stage 4 - Referral: Do they bring others?

Referral is when your existing users bring in new users. At a referral rate above 1.0 (each user brings in more than one new user), your product grows on its own without any ad spend. This is what people mean by "viral growth."

Referral is the most underused stage. Most teams assume word-of-mouth just happens if the product is good enough. In reality, the best referral loops are engineered deliberately. Dropbox offered 500MB of free storage to both the person who referred and the person who joined - a two-sided incentive that cost Dropbox almost nothing (extra storage) but drove 35% of daily signups at its peak. Uber gave both riders free ride credits for inviting friends during its global expansion phase.

**What to track:** referral rate (new users from referrals / total new users), Net Promoter Score (NPS) as a leading indicator, viral coefficient (K-factor).

### Stage 5 - Revenue: Do they pay?

Revenue measures how you convert user activity into business income. For a SaaS product this includes Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR), conversion rate from free to paid, and Average Revenue Per User (ARPU). For an e-commerce store it is average order value and purchase frequency.

Strava, the fitness tracking app, is a good example of deliberate revenue design: basic activity tracking is free, but advanced analytics and route planning are locked behind a paid subscription. The free tier creates activation and retention. The premium tier captures revenue from power users who are already deeply engaged.

Revenue should always be evaluated alongside acquisition cost. If you spend $100 to acquire a customer who generates $80 of lifetime revenue, you are shrinking with every new customer.

**What to track:** MRR/ARR, conversion rate from free to paid, ARPU, Lifetime Value (LTV), LTV-to-CAC ratio (ideally 3:1 or higher).

## Two Real-World Case Studies

<Callout type="example" title="Dropbox: Engineering Every AARRR Stage Deliberately">
Dropbox grew from 100,000 users in 2008 to nearly 4 million by 2009 - a 3,900% increase in 15 months. They did it by optimizing each AARRR stage in sequence:

- **Acquisition:** A waitlist referral program created scarcity and buzz before launch.
- **Activation:** A 2-minute demo video explaining the product increased signups by 10% overnight.
- **Retention:** Files synced seamlessly across devices, making Dropbox genuinely indispensable.
- **Referral:** Both the referrer and the new user received 500MB of free storage - a two-sided incentive that drove 35% of all daily signups at its peak.
- **Revenue:** A freemium model converted heavy users to paid plans once they hit storage limits.

The referral program alone is now taught in business schools as a model of engineered virality.
</Callout>

<Callout type="example" title="Facebook: Finding the Activation Number That Changed Everything">
Facebook's early growth team discovered a single data point that transformed their onboarding: users who added **7 friends within their first 10 days** were dramatically more likely to become long-term active users. Below that threshold, most users drifted away.

This insight shifted Facebook's entire product focus. Every onboarding screen, every email, every prompt was redesigned around getting new users to that 7-friend milestone as fast as possible. The result was one of the fastest-growing social networks in history.

The lesson is not the number 7 - it's that there is always a specific activation threshold for your product. Your job is to find it through data, then design everything around hitting it.
</Callout>

## Where to Start: Diagnosing Your Funnel

Most teams waste months optimizing the wrong stage. Here is a simple diagnostic process:

<Mermaid chart={`

  S[Start] --> Q1{Day 30 retention\nbelow 20%?}
  Q1 -- Yes --> FIX1[Fix Retention first]
  Q1 -- No --> Q2{Less than 40%\nof signups activate?}
  Q2 -- Yes --> FIX2[Fix Activation next]
  Q2 -- No --> Q3{Referral rate\nbelow 0.5?}
  Q3 -- Yes --> FIX3[Build Referral loop]
  Q3 -- No --> Q4{LTV less than\n3x CAC?}
  Q4 -- Yes --> FIX4[Fix Revenue model]
  Q4 -- No --> FIX5[Scale Acquisition]
`} />

Run this diagnostic every month. Your bottleneck stage will shift as the product matures.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Obsessing over Acquisition while the rest of the funnel leaks.** More users into a broken funnel just means more people who churn. Before scaling paid ads, check your Day 7 retention. If it is below 15%, stop buying traffic and fix the product. Research consistently shows that 90% of startups fail - and most of those failures share a common thread: scaling acquisition before proving retention.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">
**Skipping the Referral stage entirely.** Most teams treat referral as something that will happen automatically if the product is good enough. It rarely does. Referral is an engineered lever: you need a trigger (when to ask), a mechanic (what users share), and an incentive (why they share). Without deliberately building this, you leave your strongest growth channel untouched.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Instrument first, optimize second.** Before running any growth experiments, make sure you can actually measure each AARRR stage. Many teams discover they cannot accurately measure Activation or Retention because the tracking was never set up. Your first sprint should be analytics infrastructure: event tracking, cohort analysis, and funnel visualization. Every experiment after that will produce real data instead of guesses.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Fix the leakiest stage in your funnel before spending another dollar bringing more people in.**
