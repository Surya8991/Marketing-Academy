# The North Star Metric

Most marketing and product teams drown in dashboards: signups, sessions, CTR, MQLs, NPS, ARPU. The North Star Metric (NSM) is the antidote. It is one single number that captures the core value your product delivers to customers, and that the whole company can rally behind. If your team cannot name its NSM in one sentence, this lesson is for you.

## Quick Summary

- A North Star Metric is ONE number that measures the value customers get from your product - not revenue, not signups.
- It must be a leading indicator: it predicts future revenue, rather than just reporting past results.
- Every team in the company should be able to move it with their daily work.
- Break it into 3-5 input metrics so individual teams have something concrete to act on each week.
- Revisit it at least once a year - the right NSM for your current stage often changes as you scale.

## What It Actually Is

The North Star Metric is the one metric that best predicts long-term, sustainable growth. It measures the value customers actually get from your product. It is not revenue (a result) and not a vanity number (signups). It sits between the two.

Think of it this way:

- **Too downstream (lagging):** Quarterly revenue, annual ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue - total yearly subscription income)
- **Too upstream (vanity):** App downloads, registered users, pageviews
- **Just right (the NSM):** Value delivered to an active customer

Concrete example: Spotify's NSM is "time spent listening." It is not paid subscribers - that is a downstream result. People who listen more, churn less (meaning they cancel less often) and upgrade more. Move listening time up, and revenue follows automatically.

<Callout type="info">
The term "North Star Metric" was coined by Sean Ellis around 2010 and turned into a full operating framework by Amplitude's North Star Playbook in 2017. Amplitude's research across hundreds of companies shows that an effective NSM must do three things at once: express customer value, act as a leading indicator of revenue, and be actionable by the team. Lagging metrics like quarterly revenue fail the third test - by the time the number moves, the work that caused it is months old.
</Callout>

## The Three "Games" Your Product Can Play

Every product fits into one of three engagement models. Knowing which game you play points you directly toward the right type of NSM.

| Game | What it measures | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Attention Game | Time spent in product | Facebook, Netflix, Spotify, TikTok |
| Transaction Game | Frequency of completed transactions | Amazon, Airbnb, Uber, Stripe |
| Productivity Game | Work efficiency or output created | Slack, Salesforce, Notion, Figma |

Pick the game that matches how your product creates value. A productivity tool measuring "time spent" would be measuring the wrong thing entirely - you want users to get things done fast, not linger.

## Real Company Examples

### Slack - "Teams That Have Sent 2,000+ Messages"

Slack's famous activation threshold is not just "users who signed up." Their internal growth team identified that teams reaching 2,000 messages sent had an extremely high probability of converting to paid plans and staying long-term. This specific number - 2,000 messages - became their proxy for "a team that has genuinely adopted the product." It is a brilliant NSM because it measures actual value delivered (teams communicating effectively), not just account creation.

### Facebook - "Users Who Add 7 Friends in 10 Days"

Facebook's early growth team, led by Chamath Palihapitiya, discovered through data analysis that users who added at least 7 friends within their first 10 days had dramatically higher long-term retention. Monthly Active Users (MAU) was their public NSM, but internally the activation event - 7 friends in 10 days - was what the growth team optimized for. This single finding reshaped Facebook's entire onboarding experience and is widely credited with driving its explosive growth from 2008 to 2012.

### Airbnb - "Nights Booked"

Airbnb moved from tracking "signups" to "nights booked" as its core operating metric. In its S-1 (IPO filing) for its 2020 stock market listing, "Nights and Experiences Booked" was the headline operating metric, totaling 251 million in 2019. The shift mattered because signups told Airbnb almost nothing about whether hosts and guests were getting real value. Nights booked directly measured value on both sides of the marketplace.

### Netflix - "Median View Hours Per Month Per Subscriber"

Netflix uses subscriber count as its public metric, but internally the team tracks engagement depth. Their research (published in their 2024 shareholder letters) shows that members who watch at least a certain threshold of hours per month have dramatically lower churn rates. The metric ensures Netflix optimizes for genuine viewing satisfaction, not just subscriber acquisition.

<Callout type="example">
**Worked Example: A SaaS Project Management Tool**

Step 1 - Write the value statement: "Our product is valuable when a team completes a project on time using our tool."

Step 2 - Turn it into a metric: "Number of projects marked complete per team per month."

Step 3 - Test it against the three criteria:
- Does it reflect customer value? Yes - completing projects is the whole point.
- Does it lead revenue? Yes - teams completing projects stay subscribed and expand seats.
- Can your teams move it? Yes - the product, onboarding, and success teams all have levers.

Step 4 - Decompose into inputs: new team activation rate, templates used per new team, tasks assigned per project, projects started per month per team.

That is your NSM stack. Individual teams own the inputs; leadership watches the NSM.
</Callout>

## The Four-Step Playbook to Find Your NSM

### Step 1 - Write the Value Statement

Finish this sentence: "Our product is valuable when a customer ______."

- Airbnb: "...books and stays in a place."
- Slack: "...sends messages inside a team."
- Dropbox: "...stores and retrieves files from any device."
- Duolingo: "...completes a language lesson daily."

The verb in your answer usually points directly to your NSM.

### Step 2 - Turn the Verb into a Metric

Take the action from your value statement and make it measurable:

- "books and stays" becomes "nights booked"
- "sends messages" becomes "messages sent per team"
- "completes a lesson daily" becomes "daily active learners"

### Step 3 - Test Against Three Criteria

Ask these three questions. If any answer is "no," keep iterating:

1. Does it reflect real customer value (not just activity)?
2. Does it predict revenue (leading, not lagging)?
3. Can multiple teams in the company move it with their work?

### Step 4 - Decompose into Input Metrics

An NSM is useless if only the CEO can influence it. Break it into 3-5 input metrics that individual teams own. Amplitude's framework names four types of inputs:

- **Breadth:** How many people reach the core value? (new user activation rate)
- **Depth:** How deeply are they using it? (features adopted per user)
- **Frequency:** How often do they return? (sessions per week)
- **Efficiency:** How fast do they get value? (time-to-first-value in minutes)

## NSM Patterns by Business Model

| Business Type | Common NSM | Companies |
|---|---|---|
| Marketplace | Completed transactions | Airbnb (nights booked), Uber (rides completed) |
| Social / Content | Time spent or sessions | Facebook (DAU), Netflix (view hours), Spotify (listening time) |
| SaaS / Collaboration | Active teams doing core action | Slack (teams at 2k messages), Asana (tasks completed) |
| Storage / Utility | Files or actions completed | Dropbox (files saved), Google Drive (docs created) |
| E-commerce | Gross Merchandise Volume (GMV) | Amazon (orders), Shopify (merchant revenue) |
| Freemium B2B | Weekly paid seats | Airtable (weekly paid users), Notion (paid workspaces) |

About 50% of companies use a revenue-related NSM, 35% use customer growth metrics, and 30% use consumption or engagement growth, according to research published by Andreessen Horowitz in 2024 based on surveys of growth-stage startups.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**The four mistakes that kill NSM programs:**

1. **Picking revenue as your NSM.** Revenue is a lagging result, not a leading indicator. You can boost it short-term with discounts and harm long-term retention at the same time.

2. **Picking a vanity metric.** Signups, pageviews, app downloads, and raw MAU (Monthly Active Users) often go up while the actual product experience gets worse. If your NSM rises but churn (cancellations) also rises, your NSM is wrong.

3. **Never revisiting it.** Your NSM is not permanent. LinkedIn's early NSM around endorsements made sense in the connection-building era but would be a poor metric today. About 25% of growth-stage companies changed their NSM in a recent 12-month period (Andreessen Horowitz, 2024). Reassess at least once a year.

4. **Letting every team have its own.** The whole point is alignment. If marketing tracks one number and product tracks another, you do not have a North Star - you have two compasses pointing in different directions.
</Callout>

## Before Product-Market Fit: A Special Case

If your product is early-stage and you are not sure whether people actually want it yet, do not obsess over an NSM. Instead, track cohort retention (what percentage of users who signed up in month X are still active in month X+3). Retention is the clearest signal of product-market fit. Once retention curves flatten at a healthy level, then find your NSM.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Your North Star Metric is the proof that customers are getting real value - and every number in your company should trace back to moving it.**
