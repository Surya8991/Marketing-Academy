# Sample Size Math for A/B Tests

Most A/B tests fail not because the idea was bad, but because the test was under-powered. You peeked too early, called it significant, and shipped a change that was just random noise.

Microsoft runs more than 10,000 A/B tests per year on its ExP (Experimentation Platform). Their research shows that only about one-third of those tests produce a measurable positive effect. The other two-thirds either show no difference or actually hurt metrics. That ratio holds even at a company with some of the most sophisticated experimentation infrastructure on the planet. Getting your sample size right is the foundation that makes the other two-thirds safe to dismiss.

## Why Sample Size Matters

A test with too few visitors is like flipping a coin three times and concluding it is biased. You might see two heads in a row, but that tells you almost nothing about the true probability.

Running with too small a sample causes two distinct problems:

1. **False positives (Type I error):** You declare a winner when the difference was just noise.
2. **False negatives (Type II error):** You miss a real improvement because your test lacked the sensitivity to detect it.

Both errors cost money - false positives ship bad changes, false negatives bury good ones.

## The Core Formula

The practical formula for a two-sample proportions test (the most common CRO scenario) is:

```
n = (Z_alpha/2 + Z_beta)^2 * (p1*(1-p1) + p2*(1-p2)) / (p1 - p2)^2
```

Where:
- `n` = required visitors per variant
- `Z_alpha/2` = 1.96 for 95% confidence (two-tailed)
- `Z_beta` = 0.84 for 80% power
- `p1` = baseline conversion rate
- `p2` = expected conversion rate after change (baseline + MDE)
- `MDE` = minimum detectable effect (the smallest lift you care about)

**A practical shortcut:** For a 95% confidence / 80% power test detecting a 20% relative lift on a 5% baseline:

```
n per variant = 16 * 0.05 * 0.95 / (0.05 * 0.20)^2
              = 16 * 0.0475 / 0.0001
              ≈ 7,600 visitors per variant
```

So you need roughly 15,200 total visitors split evenly between control and treatment before you can call results.

## The Three Inputs You Must Lock Before Launch

### 1. Baseline Conversion Rate

Pull this from your analytics for the exact page and audience segment you are testing. Do not use sitewide averages - a checkout page converting at 3% and a homepage converting at 12% need very different sample sizes.

### 2. Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE)

This is the smallest lift that would actually change a business decision. If you would only ship a change that moves conversion rate by at least 5% relative, set MDE to 5%. Chasing smaller effects requires exponentially more traffic.

Common MDEs by traffic tier:

| Monthly Visitors | Realistic MDE |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Under 10,000    | 20-30%       |
| 10,000-100,000  | 10-20%       |
| 100,000+        | 5-10%        |
| 1M+             | 1-5%         |

### 3. Run Duration

Calculate expected run time:

```
Days needed = (n per variant * 2) / (daily visitors * traffic allocation)
```

Cap tests at four weeks maximum. After four weeks, external factors (seasonality, news events, changing user mix) start to confound results even if you hit your sample size.

<Callout type="warning">
**Peeking kills your test.** Checking results before you hit your planned sample size and stopping early when you see significance is the single most common and damaging mistake in A/B testing. It inflates your false positive rate from 5% to as high as 26% (Johari et al., 2017). Commit to your sample size in writing before the test starts, and do not look at conversion rates until you hit it.
</Callout>

## Segmentation and Multiple Metrics

Every additional segment you analyze (mobile vs. desktop, new vs. returning, country) multiplies your false positive risk. Use Bonferroni correction: divide your alpha by the number of tests.

If you run your primary metric plus three segments at p < 0.05, you need p < 0.0125 in each sub-test to maintain an experiment-wide false positive rate of 5%.

Pick one primary metric before the test starts. Secondary metrics are exploratory, not confirmatory.

## Sequential Testing: An Alternative Approach

If you genuinely cannot wait for a fixed sample, sequential testing (also called always-valid p-values or anytime-valid inference) lets you peek without inflating error rates. Tools like Statsig and Optimizely use this approach. The tradeoff: you need about 30% more total sample to achieve the same power as a fixed-horizon test.

<Callout type="info">
**Free tools to do the math:** Evan Miller's Sample Size Calculator (evolver.io/tools/sample-size) handles the proportion test. For more complex designs (revenue metrics with high variance, ratio metrics), use the R package `pwr` or Python's `statsmodels.stats.power`.
</Callout>

## A Worked Example

**Scenario:** You want to test a new CTA button on a product page.

- Baseline conversion rate: 4.2%
- MDE: 15% relative lift (you want to detect a change from 4.2% to 4.83%)
- Confidence: 95%, Power: 80%
- Daily visitors to the page: 800

**Step 1 - Calculate n:**

Using an online calculator with p1 = 0.042, p2 = 0.0483:
- n per variant = 9,100 visitors

**Step 2 - Calculate run time:**

- Total visitors needed: 18,200
- Daily visitors: 800
- Days: 18,200 / 800 = 22.75 days

**Conclusion:** Run the test for 23 days. Do not check results until day 23. Ship or kill based on that single read.

## What to Do When You Have Low Traffic

Low-traffic sites have two honest options:

1. **Focus on macro conversions with large MDEs.** Test things bold enough that even a 25% relative lift is plausible - major layout changes, entirely new flows, radical headline rewrites.
2. **Use qualitative methods first.** UserTesting, session recordings, and heatmaps tell you what to fix. Run the A/B test only to confirm the fix worked, with a large expected effect size.

Avoid the trap of running 20% traffic to a variant on a site with 500 monthly visitors. You will never reach significance, and the test will just confuse you.
