# CRO vs Growth Experimentation

## What It Is

Conversion Rate Optimization (CRO) and Growth Experimentation both rely on the same core toolkit: hypotheses, A/B tests, and data. But they serve different mandates, and mixing them up leads to wasted effort and misaligned teams.

CRO is narrow by design. Its job is to squeeze more value out of existing traffic on an existing funnel. You pick a page, identify friction, run a test, and lift the conversion rate. The scope stays inside the funnel. The metric is almost always conversion rate or revenue per visitor.

Growth Experimentation is broader. It treats the entire business model as a hypothesis. Teams ask questions like: Should we charge monthly or annually? Should we add a freemium tier? Should we enter a new channel? A/B tests are just one tool here. The outputs might be new product features, new pricing models, or entirely new acquisition loops. The metric changes with each experiment.

<Callout type="info">
CRO optimizes what exists. Growth Experimentation questions whether what exists is the right thing at all.
</Callout>

## Real-World Example

Booking.com is the most cited case in the experimentation literature for good reason. By 2018, the company was running more than 1,000 concurrent A/B tests across its product at any given time. Their experimentation culture covered both CRO-style work (button copy, hero images, urgency signals on hotel pages) and growth-style bets (new market entry, loyalty program design, mobile app monetization). The result: their conversion rate across core markets consistently outpaced competitors by 2 to 3 percentage points, a gap that translated into hundreds of millions in annual revenue. The key distinction internally was that CRO tests were owned by product teams and had a 2-week cycle, while growth experiments were owned by strategy teams and ran for quarters.

<Callout type="example">
Booking.com ran over 1,000 simultaneous experiments by 2018. CRO tests had a 2-week cycle and focused on page-level friction. Growth experiments ran for quarters and evaluated whole business models. Two mandates, one infrastructure.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Knowing which mode you are in changes every decision you make.

If you are in CRO mode, you need statistical significance fast, you need a clear baseline conversion rate, and your success metric is fixed before the test starts. The business problem is efficiency: more revenue from the same traffic.

If you are in Growth Experimentation mode, you need longer runways, executive buy-in for inconclusive results, and a portfolio mindset. Some experiments will fail and that is the point. The business problem is direction: figuring out where to invest resources before you scale them.

Misidentifying the mode causes real damage. Teams that run growth bets on 2-week CRO timelines declare tests inconclusive and kill ideas that needed more time. Teams that run CRO tests with quarterly growth-experiment budgets waste money iterating on a funnel that should be replaced entirely.

According to a 2024 industry benchmark from Shno, companies that invest in structured experimentation systems achieve conversion rate lifts of 15% to 30% and time-to-decision reductions of 40% to 60%. The lift is real, but only when the mandate is clear.

## How It Works

The framework below separates the two modes at the decision point where most teams go wrong: choosing the scope before choosing the method.

<Mermaid chart={`flowchart TD
    A[Business Question] --> B{Scope Check}
    B -->|Improve existing funnel| C[CRO Mode]
    B -->|Question the model or channel| D[Growth Experiment Mode]
    C --> E[Pick one page or step]
    E --> F[Form hypothesis on friction]
    F --> G[Run A/B test - 2 to 4 weeks]
    G --> H[Ship winner, document learner]
    D --> I[Define the bet and the metric]
    I --> J[Set a runway - 4 to 12 weeks]
    J --> K[Run MVP or test variant]
    K --> L{Result}
    L -->|Signal found| M[Scale or build]
    L -->|No signal| N[Kill and rotate budget]`} />

The decision point at the top (scope check) is the most important step. Most teams skip it and default to A/B testing everything, which is CRO mode even when the question is a growth mode question.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake is running a growth experiment on a CRO timeline. A team bets on a new pricing model, gives it two weeks, sees no significant result, and kills it. But pricing model changes take time to propagate through word-of-mouth, sales cycles, and usage patterns. Applying a 2-week CRO cadence to a 12-week growth question guarantees false negatives. Before you start any test, write down which mode you are in and set the timeline accordingly.
</Callout>

A second common mistake is using the same success metric for both modes. CRO almost always optimizes for conversion rate. Growth experiments might optimize for 90-day retention, annual contract value, or payback period. Using conversion rate as the metric for a growth experiment often produces a local maximum that looks good short-term and destroys long-term unit economics.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**CRO and Growth Experimentation use the same tools but answer different questions - CRO asks 'how do we convert more people?' while Growth Experimentation asks 'are we converting the right people in the right way?'**
