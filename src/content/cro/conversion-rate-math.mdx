# Conversion Rate Math

## What It Is

A conversion rate is the percentage of visitors who complete a desired action. That action could be a purchase, a sign-up, a download, or a phone call. The formula is simple: divide the number of conversions by the number of visitors, then multiply by 100.

**Conversion Rate = (Conversions / Visitors) x 100**

So if 200 people visit your landing page and 4 of them buy something, your conversion rate is 2%. That single number connects your traffic to your revenue, which makes it one of the most important metrics in all of digital marketing.

The global average website conversion rate sits around 3.68% across industries (Invespcro, 2024). E-commerce specifically averages closer to 2%. The top 10% of landing pages convert at 11.45% or above - which shows how much room there is to grow once you understand the math.

## Real-World Example

In 2023, Booking.com ran over 25,000 A/B tests across its platform. One recurring finding was that small copy and layout changes - things that took hours to implement - lifted conversion rates by 0.5% to 2%. At Booking.com's scale of roughly 1.5 million room nights booked per day, a 1% lift on a single page flow translated to tens of millions of dollars in additional annual revenue. The math is the same at any scale: a small percentage gain on a large number produces a very large absolute gain.

## Why It Matters

Most marketing teams focus on getting more traffic. Buy more ads, publish more content, rank for more keywords. But traffic costs money. Conversion rate optimization (CRO) - the practice of improving your conversion rate - generates more revenue from traffic you already have.

Here is the business problem it solves: your cost per acquisition (CPA) drops every time your conversion rate rises. If you spend $10,000 on ads and convert 100 visitors, each customer cost you $100. Double your conversion rate to 4% and suddenly each customer costs $50 - with no increase in ad spend. That is why even a 1% lift can be worth millions at scale.

CRO also surfaces product and UX problems. A low conversion rate is a signal: something is stopping people from acting. The math forces you to ask why.

## How It Works

There are four numbers you need to track and one ratio to watch:

1. **Visitors** - how many people reached the page
2. **Conversions** - how many completed the goal
3. **Conversion Rate** - conversions divided by visitors, times 100
4. **Revenue per Visitor (RPV)** - average order value multiplied by conversion rate

RPV is especially useful because it tells you the dollar value of every single visitor who lands on your page. If your average order is $80 and your conversion rate is 2.5%, each visitor is worth $2.00 on average. Lift the rate to 3.5% and each visitor is worth $2.80 - a 40% revenue increase with zero extra traffic.

### How to Calculate a Revenue Lift

Use this three-step process whenever you want to size the value of a conversion rate improvement:

1. **Start with current state** - monthly visitors x current conversion rate x average order value
2. **Model the new state** - same visitors x new (higher) conversion rate x same order value
3. **Find the difference** - new monthly revenue minus current monthly revenue, times 12

Example: 50,000 monthly visitors, 2% conversion rate, $60 average order value.
- Current monthly revenue: 50,000 x 0.02 x $60 = $60,000
- At 3% conversion rate: 50,000 x 0.03 x $60 = $90,000
- Annual lift: ($90,000 - $60,000) x 12 = **$360,000 per year**

That is the value of one percentage point.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Do not optimize conversion rate in isolation.** A high conversion rate on low-quality traffic is not a win. If you target bargain hunters to boost sign-ups, your churn rate will spike and lifetime value will drop. Always look at conversion rate alongside revenue per visitor, customer lifetime value (LTV), and refund rates. Optimizing one number while ignoring the others leads to decisions that hurt the business overall.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Statistical significance matters.** If you run an A/B test for two days with 300 visitors and declare a winner, you are probably reading noise as signal. Use a sample size calculator before you start any test. A common rule of thumb: aim for at least 1,000 conversions per variant before calling a result. Most underpowered tests lead to false positives that do not hold up when rolled out fully.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**A 1% lift in conversion rate is never just 1% more revenue - at scale, it compounds into the single highest-ROI lever in your entire marketing stack.**
