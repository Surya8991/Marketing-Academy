# The Hero Section Formula

## What It Is

The hero section is the first thing a visitor sees when they land on your website - before they scroll. It sits "above the fold," a term borrowed from print newspapers, where the most important stories appeared on the top half of the folded paper. Online, it means everything visible on screen without scrolling.

Your hero section has one job: convince the visitor to stay and take one specific action. Nothing more. Visitors decide whether to stay or leave within three seconds of landing on your page, and that decision happens entirely in this zone. According to Nielsen Norman Group research, users do 80% of their scanning above the fold.

The formula is simple: a headline that names the outcome, a subheadline that explains how, and one clear button. Get those three right and everything else is optional.

## Real-World Example

Carrot, a real estate lead generation platform, ran an A/B test in 2023 on their hero section. The original version had a generic headline and a cluttered layout. The new version used a focused outcome-based headline, removed competing links, and placed a single CTA button directly below the subheadline. The result: a 50% increase in conversions from the same traffic, with no changes to the rest of the page.

## Why It Matters

Most businesses spend money driving traffic - through ads, SEO, or social media - and then lose visitors in the first three seconds because the hero section fails to answer the basic question: "What is this and why should I care?"

A weak hero section wastes your entire acquisition budget. A strong one does the opposite: it pulls visitors down the page, pre-qualifies them, and sets up the sale before they even read the rest of your content.

This matters especially if you run paid ads. Every visitor costs money. A hero section that converts at 4% instead of 2% doubles the return on every ad dollar you spend, without changing your targeting or budget.

## How It Works

The hero section formula has four components, stacked in order of importance:

1. **Headline** - one sentence naming the outcome the visitor will get. No cleverness. No company name. Just the result.
2. **Subheadline** - one or two sentences explaining who this is for and how the outcome is delivered.
3. **CTA button** - one action only. Label it with the outcome, not a verb like "Submit." Example: "Get My Free Audit" beats "Submit."
4. **Supporting visual** - an image, screenshot, or short video that shows the product or outcome in context. Optional but powerful.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake is writing a headline about your company instead of your customer's outcome. "Welcome to Acme Co - Innovating Since 1998" tells the visitor nothing useful. "Cut Your Accounting Time in Half" tells them exactly what they get. Write every headline in terms of the visitor's result, not your identity. A second common error is adding multiple CTA buttons. Having more than one competing CTA can decrease conversions by up to 266%, according to conversion rate research. Pick one action and commit to it.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Your hero section earns the scroll: nail the headline, strip everything that competes with the CTA button, and your whole page performs better.**
