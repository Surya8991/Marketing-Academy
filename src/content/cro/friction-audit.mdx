# Friction Audit

## What It Is

A friction audit is a systematic review of every step a user takes on their way to completing a goal - signing up, buying, booking, or downloading. The goal is to find the moments where people slow down, get confused, or give up entirely. "Friction" is any unnecessary effort: too many form fields, a confusing page layout, slow load times, hidden costs revealed at checkout, or a call-to-action button that is hard to find.

The audit treats your funnel like a leaky pipe. Every drop that falls out before the end is a lost conversion. Before you can fix the leak, you need to find exactly where the water is escaping. That is what a friction audit does: it maps the holes.

The output is a prioritized list of friction points ranked by severity and ease of fixing. You then run tests - A/B tests or simple edits - to remove each point and measure whether conversions improve.

## Real-World Example

Expedia discovered in 2010 that a single optional form field - "Company Name" - was causing users to enter their billing address under a company name instead of their personal name, which caused payment failures. Removing that one field saved the company an estimated $12 million per year. The friction was invisible until someone ran a systematic audit of the checkout form.

## Why It Matters

Cart abandonment averages 70% globally, and mobile abandonment exceeds 80% (Baymard Institute, 2024). That means most people who intend to buy never finish. Some of that is natural browsing behavior. But a large share is caused by friction you put in their way without realizing it.

A friction audit is most useful when:
- Your traffic is growing but conversions are flat
- A funnel step has an unusually high drop-off rate
- You are about to run paid ads and want to make sure the landing page converts before spending budget
- Users are completing sign-up but not activating - the friction may be post-signup

It solves a specific business problem: you are spending money to bring people in, but losing them before they convert. Finding and removing friction is almost always cheaper than buying more traffic.

## How It Works

A friction audit follows five steps. Start with data to find where people drop off, then use qualitative tools to understand why, then fix and test.

<Mermaid chart={`flowchart TD
    A[Step 1: Map the Funnel] --> B[Step 2: Pull Drop-off Data]
    B --> C[Step 3: Watch Session Recordings]
    C --> D[Step 4: Run User Surveys]
    D --> E[Step 5: Prioritize and Fix]
    E --> F{Did conversions improve?}
    F -- Yes --> G[Document and move to next friction point]
    F -- No --> C

**Step 1 - Map the funnel.** Write out every step a user takes from first touch to goal completion. Include the pages, the forms, the emails, and the confirmation screen.

**Step 2 - Pull drop-off data.** Use Google Analytics or any analytics tool to find which steps lose the most users. A step with 60% drop-off is your first target.

**Step 3 - Watch session recordings.** Tools like Hotjar or Microsoft Clarity record real user sessions. Watch people who dropped off. You will see where they paused, scrolled back, or rage-clicked.

**Step 4 - Run user surveys.** A simple one-question exit survey - "What stopped you from completing your purchase today?" - gives you the language users use to describe the friction. That language is gold.

**Step 5 - Prioritize and fix.** Rank friction points by two factors: how many users are affected, and how hard the fix is. High impact, easy fix goes first. Then test each change individually so you know what actually moved the number.

<Callout type="tip">
Start with the step that has the highest drop-off rate, not the step that looks the most broken to you. Your instincts about what is confusing are often wrong. The data tells you where the real problem is.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
The most common mistake is running a friction audit on the landing page while ignoring the steps after it. Many companies obsess over above-the-fold design but never look at the checkout flow, the sign-up confirmation email, or the onboarding sequence. Friction anywhere in the funnel costs you conversions. Audit the whole path, not just the front door.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Every form field, every extra click, and every confusing label is a reason for your user to leave - a friction audit finds them before they cost you another sale.**
