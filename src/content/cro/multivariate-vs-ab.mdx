# Multivariate Testing vs A/B Testing: When to Use Each

Picking the wrong test type is one of the most common CRO mistakes. Run a multivariate test on low-traffic pages and you will wait months for significance. Run an A/B test when you have multiple interacting elements and you will miss the combination that actually wins.

## What Is A/B Testing?

An A/B test (also called a split test) compares two versions of a single page or element. Visitors are split randomly: half see the control (A), half see the challenger (B). You change one thing - a headline, a button colour, a form layout - and measure which version drives more of your target action.

**Best for:**
- Single-element changes (headline, CTA, hero image)
- Low-to-medium traffic pages (under ~50,000 monthly visitors)
- Quick iteration cycles where speed matters more than depth

## What Is Multivariate Testing?

Multivariate testing (MVT) changes multiple elements simultaneously and measures every combination. If you test 2 headlines and 3 button colours, you get 6 combinations (2 x 3). The traffic is split across all 6 variants.

**Best for:**
- Redesigns where several elements change together
- High-traffic pages (100,000+ monthly visitors recommended)
- Understanding which element contributes most to the lift

## The Traffic Requirement: The Mistake Everyone Makes

<Callout type="warning">
Multivariate tests multiply your traffic needs. A test with 3 elements and 2 variants each creates 8 combinations. If your page needs 1,000 conversions per variant to reach significance, you now need 8,000 total conversions - not 2,000. Running MVT on a low-traffic page is how teams spend 6 months collecting inconclusive data.
</Callout>

To estimate required traffic, use this rough formula:

```
Required visitors = (variants count) x (baseline conversions needed for significance)
```

For most e-commerce goals, you need at least 200-300 conversions per variant. A 6-variant MVT needs 1,200-1,800 total conversions minimum.

## Real-World Scale: Booking.com

<Callout type="example">
Booking.com runs more than 1,000 concurrent A/B tests at any given time - a figure the company has documented publicly since at least 2017. The majority are simple A/B tests, not multivariate, because most pages are testing one hypothesis at a time. MVT is reserved for their highest-traffic flows (search results, checkout) where the volume supports it. The A/B testing software market exceeded $850M in 2024, reflecting how central systematic experimentation has become in digital product teams.
</Callout>

## Statistical Significance Explained Simply

<Callout type="info">
Statistical significance answers one question: how likely is it that the difference you see is real rather than random chance? The industry standard is 95% confidence - meaning there is only a 5% probability the result occurred by luck. Do not call a test early just because one variant is leading. Peeking at results and stopping as soon as you see a win inflates your false-positive rate dramatically.
</Callout>

## Choosing Your Test: A Quick Reference

| Factor | A/B Test | Multivariate Test |
|---|---|---|
| Elements changing | 1 | 2 or more |
| Monthly visitors needed | 10,000+ | 100,000+ |
| Time to significance | Days to weeks | Weeks to months |
| Insight depth | Which version wins | Which element drives the win |
| Tooling complexity | Simple | Moderate to high |

## Step-by-Step: Running an A/B Test

1. **Define one hypothesis** - "Changing the CTA from 'Submit' to 'Get My Free Guide' will increase clicks by 15%."
2. **Set your success metric** - click-through rate, form completions, revenue per visitor.
3. **Calculate sample size** - use a sample size calculator (Evan Miller's is free and accurate).
4. **Build variants** - control (A) and challenger (B) only.
5. **Set the test duration** - run for at least one full business cycle (usually two weeks minimum).
6. **Do not peek** - check results only at your pre-defined end date.
7. **Ship or learn** - if B wins at 95%+ confidence, ship it. If not, document what you learned and iterate.

## Step-by-Step: Running a Multivariate Test

1. **Identify interacting elements** - headline, subheadline, hero image, primary CTA.
2. **Limit combinations** - aim for under 16 total combinations to keep traffic requirements manageable.
3. **Confirm traffic** - verify you have enough monthly visitors to reach significance in under 8 weeks.
4. **Use a full-factorial or fractional-factorial design** - most MVT tools handle this automatically.
5. **Analyse main effects and interactions** - look for which element drives the most variance, and whether any combination outperforms its individual parts.
6. **Implement the winning combination** - not just the best headline or best button in isolation.

## Common MVT Pitfalls

- **Too many variants**: 4 elements with 3 variants each = 81 combinations. You will never reach significance.
- **Ignoring interaction effects**: sometimes headline A + button B outperforms any other combination even if neither wins individually.
- **Stopping at the wrong time**: MVT results stabilise later than A/B results. Give them time.
- **Testing unrelated pages simultaneously**: traffic from different pages should not be mixed into the same experiment pool.
