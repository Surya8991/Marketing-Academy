# Customer Research for CRO

## What It Is

CRO - conversion rate optimization - is the practice of getting more of your existing visitors to take a desired action. Most people think of it as an A/B testing discipline. It is. But testing without research is guessing with a spreadsheet attached.

Customer research for CRO means gathering direct evidence about why people do or do not convert on your site. It covers qualitative methods - heatmaps, session recordings, on-site surveys, customer interviews, and user testing - alongside the quantitative analytics you already track. The goal is to generate hypotheses grounded in real user behavior, not assumptions.

The ratio of spend tells the story: for every $92 companies spend acquiring a visitor, only $1 is spent on converting them (Invesp, 2024). Research is how you close that gap without buying more traffic.

## Real-World Example

Booking.com runs one of the best-documented CRO research programs in the industry. In 2023, their experimentation team reported running over 1,000 concurrent A/B tests at any given time - but every test begins with a qualitative research phase. Teams use session recordings and exit surveys to identify friction points before writing a single line of test code. This research-first approach helped the company maintain a conversion rate well above the industry average for travel (approximately 3.4% versus an industry norm closer to 2%).

## Why It Matters

Without customer research, your A/B tests are based on opinions. Someone on the team thinks the button color is wrong. Someone else thinks the headline is too long. You end up testing the highest-paid person's hypothesis first.

Research replaces opinion with evidence. It answers three questions that analytics alone cannot:

- **What** are users doing on the page? (heatmaps, recordings)
- **Where** in the funnel are they dropping off? (funnel analysis, scroll maps)
- **Why** are they leaving without converting? (surveys, interviews)

The "why" is what analytics can never tell you. A page with 80% bounce rate has a problem. Analytics tells you that. Only a user interview or an exit survey tells you what the problem actually is.

## How It Works

A CRO research cycle has four stages: discover, diagnose, hypothesize, and test. Research lives in the first two stages.

<Mermaid chart={`flowchart TD
  A[Discover: Analytics audit - find drop-off pages] --> B[Diagnose: Qualitative research - heatmaps, recordings, surveys]
  B --> C[Hypothesize: Write a prioritized test backlog]
  C --> D[Test: Run A/B or multivariate experiments]
  D --> E{Result}
  E -->|Win - implement| A
  E -->|Loss - learn| B`} />

**Stage 1 - Discover:** Pull your analytics. Find pages with high traffic and low conversion, or pages with high exit rates. These are your research targets. Do not research everything - focus on pages where a conversion lift will actually move revenue.

**Stage 2 - Diagnose:** Apply your qualitative tools in sequence.

- **Heatmaps** show click patterns and scroll depth. Install Hotjar, Microsoft Clarity (free), or Crazy Egg. Look for clicks on non-clickable elements (frustration clicks) and check whether your main CTA is above the average scroll fold.
- **Session recordings** let you watch real users navigate. Watch 20-30 recordings on your worst-performing page. Look for hesitation, back-and-forth scrolling, and rage-clicks.
- **On-site surveys** ask visitors a single question at the right moment. An exit-intent survey asking "What stopped you from completing your purchase today?" yields direct, actionable answers. Keep surveys to one question.
- **Post-purchase surveys** ask new customers what nearly stopped them from buying. This is the most underused research method in CRO. The people who converted can tell you what almost made them leave.
- **User testing** puts 5-8 people from your target audience on your site with a specific task. Watching someone try to complete checkout for the first time is humbling and invaluable.

**Stage 3 - Hypothesize:** Synthesize your research into a prioritized list of test ideas. Each hypothesis should follow this format: "Because [research finding], we believe [change] will [expected outcome] for [user segment]."

**Stage 4 - Test:** Run your highest-priority hypothesis as a controlled experiment. The research cycle restarts after each test.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**The most common mistake is treating research as optional.** Teams skip directly to testing because research feels slow. They run tests based on best practices copied from case studies at other companies. The result is a low win rate and a backlog full of ideas no one believes in. Research is not a phase you do once at the start of a program - it is a continuous input into every test cycle. Run at least one qualitative research session per quarter per key page, even when your testing program is mature.
</Callout>

A second common mistake is over-indexing on heatmaps and ignoring surveys. Heatmaps show behavior. Surveys show motivation. You need both. A heatmap showing users clicking your FAQ section repeatedly tells you they have questions. Only a survey or recording tells you what the question is and whether your answer satisfies it.

A third mistake is surveying the wrong people. Exit surveys catch visitors who did not convert - useful, but skewed toward people who were never going to buy. Balance them with post-purchase surveys targeting people who did convert. The contrast between the two groups reveals what tips people from hesitation to action.

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Customer research is what turns A/B testing from expensive guessing into a systematic process for finding and fixing the real reasons people do not convert.**
