## Why Being 'Better' Is a Losing Strategy

Most marketing tries to win by being better than the competition. Category creation is the opposite move: it makes competition irrelevant by defining a new game with rules you wrote.

Research from the book *Play Bigger* (2016), corroborated by multiple 2024 analyses, found that category kings capture approximately **76% of the total market value** in their category. The remaining competitors split the other 24%. Winning the category is far more valuable than winning a feature war.

<Callout type="warning">
Category creation is not rebranding. It is not finding a new way to describe what you already do. It requires genuine insight into a problem that existing categories fail to solve, and a willingness to educate the market rather than just sell to it.
</Callout>

---

## The Classic Example: HubSpot and Inbound Marketing

In 2006, Brian Halligan and Dharmesh Shah noticed that buyers were tuning out cold calls and banner ads. Rather than building a better email-blasting tool, they named the shift: "inbound marketing." They then spent years publishing content, running conferences (INBOUND), and certifying practitioners, all teaching the category they had invented.

The payoff: HubSpot crossed **$100M ARR by 2014** and went public on the NYSE the same year. By 2024 the company had surpassed $2.6B in annual revenue. Competitors who tried to copy the product faced a company that owned the vocabulary, the community, and the search rankings for every relevant term.

### Step 1 - Identify the Problem the World Has No Word For

A viable category starts with a genuine pain point that existing categories leave unnamed. The test: when you describe the problem to a prospect, do they lean in and say "yes, exactly, we have no way to talk about this internally"?

Research methods that work:
- Win/loss interviews focused on the language buyers use, not the features they mention
- Support ticket analysis looking for recurring complaints with no product label attached
- Job-to-be-done interviews asking "what were you doing before this product existed?"

### Step 2 - Name the Category

The name must pass three tests:
1. It describes the problem, not the solution (buyers search for problems, not solutions)
2. It is intuitive enough to spread by word of mouth
3. It is not already owned by a competitor

Good category names tend to be two or three words: inbound marketing, revenue operations, product-led growth, zero-trust security. They feel obvious in hindsight, which is the point.

### Step 3 - Craft Your Point of View

A Point of View (POV) document is a 1-3 page narrative that explains:
- Why the old category is broken
- What forces made the new problem emerge now
- What the world looks like when the new category wins
- Why your company is uniquely positioned to lead it

The POV is not a product brochure. It is a manifesto. It should make competitors uncomfortable and make prospects feel understood.

<Callout type="tip">
Publish your POV publicly. Category creation requires educating the market, and you cannot educate people who cannot find your thinking. Gating the POV behind a form defeats the purpose.
</Callout>

### Step 4 - Mobilize an Ecosystem

No company creates a category alone. You need analysts writing about it, consultants certifying in it, media covering it, and adjacent vendors acknowledging it. Tactics:

- Brief Gartner, Forrester, and IDC analysts on the category before your next product launch
- Launch a free certification program (HubSpot Academy, Salesforce Trailhead, Drift's Conversational Marketing Certification)
- Host a community or conference named after the category, not your company
- Co-author research with a respected third party that establishes the category's size and urgency

### Step 5 - Own the Lightning-Strike Moments

Category kings do not drip-feed their message. They create high-impact moments that force the market to take a position: a landmark report, a live event with thousands of attendees, a book, a viral data study. These events compress attention and signal that the category has arrived.

Drift's "Conversational Marketing" book (2019), Gainsight's Pulse conference (now the world's largest Customer Success event), and Snowflake's "Data Cloud Summit" are all examples of manufactured lightning-strike moments.

<Callout type="example">
Gainsight did not just build customer-success software. It invented and then owned the "Customer Success" job title itself, running the Pulse conference from 2013 onward. By the time competitors arrived, Gainsight had trained the people who would evaluate all of them.
</Callout>

## Common Failure Modes

| Mistake | Why It Kills the Category |
|---|---|
| Naming the category after your product | Forces competitors into a "vs. you" frame; buyers feel locked in before they buy |
| Trying to own two categories at once | Dilutes the POV; analysts and media cannot place you |
| Educating the market then failing to close | Warms up buyers for competitors; pair content investment with a tight sales motion |
| Letting the category name drift | Consistency is compounding; every synonym you tolerate fragments the market signal |
| Moving on after one quarter | Category creation takes 3-7 years; most companies quit at year one |

## Measuring Category Leadership

Traditional marketing metrics measure share of wallet. Category creation requires measuring share of mind:

- **Branded search volume** for the category term (not your company name)
- **Analyst placement** in emerging category reports
- **Speaker mix** at category-adjacent conferences - are you the keynoter or a sponsor?
- **Job posting language** - are buyers writing your category name into job descriptions?
- **Backlink anchor text** - are journalists and bloggers using your category vocabulary?

When prospects use your vocabulary before they have ever spoken to your sales team, the category is working.

## Getting Started This Quarter

1. Run three customer interviews asking: "What problem were you solving before you found us, and what did you call it internally?"
2. Identify the gap between their language and your current marketing language
3. Draft a one-page POV naming the gap and the forces that made it emerge now
4. Share the draft with five prospects and five analysts for reactions
5. If the reactions are strong, schedule a lightning-strike moment (report, event, or book) within 12 months
