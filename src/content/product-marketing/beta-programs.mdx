# Beta Programs

## What It Is

A beta program is a controlled early-access release of your product to a selected group of real users before the public launch. Unlike a private internal test, beta users are actual customers or prospects - people outside your company who interact with the product in their real environment.

The term 'beta' comes from software development, where 'alpha' is internal testing and 'beta' is the first external release. But today, product marketers use beta programs for a much bigger goal than just finding bugs. A well-run beta is a marketing engine that builds word-of-mouth, generates testimonials, and turns early users into vocal advocates before your product is even officially available.

The key distinction: a beta program is not a focus group. Users are doing real work with your product, not just answering survey questions. That real usage creates genuine feedback, genuine attachment, and genuine advocacy.

## Real-World Example

Notion's pre-launch beta strategy is one of the most studied in SaaS (software-as-a-service) history. Before their 2.0 launch, Notion built a waitlist that collected over 20,000 signups. They gave early access in waves, asked for structured feedback, and spotlighted power users in their community. The result: by 2019, Notion had reached 1 million active users - almost entirely through word-of-mouth seeded by those early beta advocates. They spent close to zero on paid advertising during that phase.

<Callout type="example">
**Notion 2.0: Building a Million Users Without Paid Ads**

Notion ran a gated beta for their 2.0 product and collected 20,000+ waitlist signups before any public launch. They used tiered access - some users got in early, others waited - which created social scarcity. Beta users felt like insiders. They posted about Notion on Twitter and Reddit, wrote setup tutorials, and built public template galleries. That organic content drove the next wave of signups. By 2019, Notion crossed 1 million active users, almost entirely from community momentum started in the beta phase.
</Callout>

## Why It Matters

Most product launches fail quietly. The product goes live, a handful of people sign up, engagement drops, and the team scrambles to figure out what went wrong. The problem is usually that there was no warm audience and no proof that real people find the product valuable.

A beta program solves both problems before launch day. It gives you a group of real users whose feedback helps you fix the product while you still can. More importantly, it gives you testimonials, case studies, and advocates who are already invested in your success.

For B2B products (those sold to businesses), beta programs also generate references - customers a sales team can point to when a prospect asks "who else uses this?" A launch with five reference customers is dramatically easier to sell than a launch with zero.

A beta program is the right tool when: you are launching a genuinely new product or major feature, you want to build community before launch, or you need real-world feedback that lab testing cannot provide.

## How It Works

A strong beta program has five phases: recruitment, onboarding, engagement, collection, and conversion.

**Recruitment** is where you define who gets in. More applications than spots creates scarcity and perceived value. Use a landing page with a clear value proposition - "be first to try X" - and a short application form to qualify candidates.

**Onboarding** sets the relationship tone. Beta users need to understand what is expected of them: give feedback, report bugs, complete certain tasks. Set this expectation clearly upfront or you will get silent users who never engage.

**Engagement** keeps users active. Regular check-ins, a private community channel, and behind-the-scenes updates make users feel like insiders, not just test subjects.

**Collection** is structured feedback gathering - surveys after key milestones, NPS (Net Promoter Score) checks, and direct interviews with the most engaged users.

**Conversion** is the marketing payoff: turning beta users into case studies, testimonials, public reviews, referrals, or paying customers.

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**Mistake: Treating Beta as Just a Bug Hunt**

The most common mistake is running a beta purely for QA (quality assurance) and forgetting the marketing opportunity entirely. Teams invite hundreds of users, fix the bugs they report, then close the program and launch with nothing to show for it - no testimonials, no case studies, no advocates. Fix this by assigning a marketing owner (not just a product owner) to the beta from day one. That person's job is to identify engaged users, capture their stories, and begin the conversion process before the beta closes.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**Keep Beta Groups Small at First**

Start with 50-100 users, not 5,000. A smaller group is easier to manage, gives you tighter feedback loops, and lets you build genuine relationships with users. You can always expand in waves. Notion and Figma both used wave-based access, which also creates ongoing press moments each time a new cohort is invited.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**A beta program is not a testing phase - it is your first marketing campaign, and the best ones turn waiting into wanting.**
