# Sales Enablement

## What It Is

Sales enablement is the process of giving your sales team everything they need to close deals. That means the right content, the right training, and the right tools - delivered at the right moment in the sales cycle. It sits at the intersection of product marketing, sales, and customer success.

In a product marketing context, sales enablement is about translating product knowledge into sales-ready assets. A product marketer knows why customers buy. The job is turning that knowledge into decks, one-pagers, battlecards, and talk tracks that a rep can actually use on a call. If sales reps are winging it or quoting outdated benefits, enablement has failed.

The term covers a wide surface area: onboarding new hires, refreshing content when a product changes, coaching on objection handling, and creating competitive materials that help reps win against rival products. It is not a one-time project - it is an ongoing function.

## Real-World Example

Salesforce has invested heavily in sales enablement through its internal platform called "Trailhead for Sales." In 2024, Salesforce reported that reps who completed structured enablement programs reached full productivity 40% faster than those who went through traditional onboarding alone. Their battlecard library covering 12 key competitors is updated quarterly by the product marketing team and is one of the most-used assets in the sales org.

## Why It Matters

The core business problem is simple: you can build a great product, but if your sales team cannot explain it clearly or defend it against competitors, you lose deals you should win. A 2024 report found that organizations with a formal sales enablement function achieved a 49% win rate on forecasted deals, compared to 42.5% for those without one - a 6.5-point gap that compounds across hundreds of deals per year (source: Spekit, 2024).

Sales enablement also fixes a specific pain felt in fast-growing companies. As products evolve and markets shift, reps often keep using stale messaging. Enablement is the system that keeps sales knowledge current.

You should prioritize this work when: you have a new product launch, you are entering a new market, a competitor just made a move, or you notice win rates dropping without a clear cause.

## How It Works

The enablement process has four repeating phases: discover, create, deliver, and measure.

**Discover** means finding out what the sales team actually needs. Talk to reps. Listen to call recordings. Look at where deals get stuck. Do not build content based on what you think sales needs - build it based on evidence.

**Create** is the production phase: decks, battlecards, one-pagers, demo scripts, objection-handling guides, and training modules.

**Deliver** means getting the content into reps' hands through the right channels - whether that is a sales meeting, a learning management system (LMS), or a content repository like Highspot or Seismic.

**Measure** closes the loop. Track whether reps use the content, whether it correlates with deal outcomes, and whether win rates improve.

## Key Deliverables

### Battlecards
A battlecard is a one-page cheat sheet on a specific competitor. It tells a rep: what the competitor claims, why those claims are weak or misleading, and how to position your product instead. Good battlecards are short, blunt, and updated regularly.

### Sales Decks
A sales deck is the main narrative a rep takes into a customer meeting. The PMM team owns the master version. The deck should follow the customer's problem, your solution, proof, and a clear next step. Reps will customize it - your job is to make the core version so good they want to start from it.

### Objection Handling Guides
A list of the 10 to 15 most common objections reps hear, with recommended responses. These are built from call recordings and rep interviews, not guesswork.

### Onboarding Training
A structured curriculum for new sales hires covering: what the product does, who buys it, why they buy it, how it compares to alternatives, and how to run a demo.

<Callout type="tip">
**Build once, reuse many times.** The research you do for a battlecard feeds the objection-handling guide. The competitive section of the sales deck is a condensed version of the battlecard. Structure your content so individual pieces plug into multiple formats - this saves time and keeps messaging consistent.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

<Callout type="warning">
**The trap: building content that no one uses.** Many PMM teams pour weeks into a beautiful 60-slide deck or a detailed battlecard - only to find that reps ignore it. The usual causes: the content is too long, it lives somewhere hard to find, or it was never explained in a live session. Fix this by keeping every asset to one page or less where possible, delivering it in a live training (not just a Slack message), and checking usage data in your content platform 30 days after launch. If usage is below 50% of the target audience, the delivery method failed - not the content.
</Callout>

## Measuring Success

Sales enablement is easier to justify when you tie it to numbers. Track these metrics:

- **Content usage rate**: what percentage of reps opened and used the asset
- **Win rate change**: did win rates improve in the deals where reps used the content
- **Ramp time**: how long does it take a new hire to close their first deal
- **Deal velocity**: are deals closing faster after enablement programs launch

<Callout type="info">
**Enablement is not a one-time event.** A training session in January does not mean reps remember the key messages in July. Build in quarterly refreshes tied to product updates and competitive changes. The companies that win at enablement treat it as a recurring program, not a project.
</Callout>

## The One-Line Takeaway

**Sales enablement is how product marketing turns product knowledge into revenue - by getting the right message to the right rep at the right moment.**
