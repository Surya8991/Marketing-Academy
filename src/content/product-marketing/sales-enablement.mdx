# Sales Enablement

In 2025, sales reps spend only 30% of their time actually selling. The other 70% goes to searching for content, prepping materials, and filling gaps that a functioning enablement program should have covered already.

## Quick Summary

- Sales enablement is the system that gets the right knowledge to the right rep at the right moment in a deal
- Organizations with formal enablement programs achieve 49% win rates versus 42.5% for those without, according to [Spekit's 2025 benchmarks](https://www.spekit.com/blog/sales-enablement-statistics-trends)
- The core deliverables are battlecards, sales decks, objection-handling guides, and onboarding curricula
- 90% of organizations now use or plan to use AI in their go-to-market efforts, making AI-ready content a new requirement for enablement teams
- Enablement is not a project, it is a recurring function tied to every product launch, competitive move, and market shift
- Revenue teams waste an average of 440 hours per year (11 full work weeks) searching for or creating content that should already exist

## What It Actually Is

Sales enablement is the process of giving your sales team the content, training, and tools they need to move deals forward, built and maintained by product marketing.

Think of it like a cockpit briefing before a flight. The pilot (your rep) knows how to fly, but before every mission they need an updated weather report, the current flight plan, and a checklist for known risks. Without that briefing, they are flying on memory from last month's training. Enablement is the briefing system.

In practice, sales enablement covers: onboarding new hires so they ramp fast, keeping content current when products change, arming reps to handle competitive objections, and creating the decks and talk tracks reps actually want to use. If your reps are improvising key messages or quoting benefits from last year's positioning, enablement has a gap.

## Why It Matters (with data)

The business case for enablement is straightforward: your product can be excellent, but if the person selling it cannot explain it clearly or defend it against a competitor, you lose deals you should win.

Key numbers from 2025 research:

- **49% vs 42.5% win rate**: Organizations with formal sales enablement achieve a 6.5-point higher win rate on forecasted deals compared to those without one ([Spekit](https://www.spekit.com/blog/sales-enablement-statistics-trends))
- **353% average training ROI**: The average return on sales training investment, making it one of the highest-return activities a GTM team can fund ([SiftHub](https://www.sifthub.io/blog/sales-enablement-statistics))
- **76% of companies** see 6% to 20% sales increases as a direct result of enablement programs ([SalesGenie](https://www.salesgenie.com/blog/sales-enablement/))
- **40-50% faster ramp**: Well-structured enablement cuts new hire ramp time nearly in half
- **67% faster deal cycles**: B2B companies using effective enablement strategies close deals significantly faster ([Competitors App](https://competitors.app/sales-enablement-content/))
- **164% year-over-year increase** in companies using AI specifically for sales training (Highspot State of Sales Enablement 2025, survey of 350 GTM professionals across 21 countries)

The problem compounds over time. As your product evolves and competitors make moves, reps default to outdated messaging unless someone maintains the system. Enablement is that maintenance function.

You should make enablement a priority when:

- You are launching a new product or major feature
- You are entering a new market or segment
- A competitor just launched, rebranded, or made an aggressive pricing move
- Win rates are dropping with no clear cause from the pipeline data
- New hire ramp time is exceeding your target

## How It Works: The Playbook

The enablement cycle runs in four repeating phases. Skip any phase and the system breaks down.

### Phase 1: Discover

Find out what reps actually need before building anything.

- Listen to recorded sales calls (tools like Gong or Chorus surface the patterns)
- Interview reps who are winning and reps who are losing
- Review lost-deal analysis: what objections killed the deal?
- Check what content exists in your repository and what is actually being used vs. ignored

### Phase 2: Create

Build assets in order of impact. Start with what unblocks the most deals:

- **Battlecards** for your top 3-5 competitors (one page each, blunt and scannable)
- **Sales deck** with the master narrative (problem, solution, proof, next step)
- **Objection-handling guide** for the 10-15 objections reps hear most
- **Demo script** with the key moments and proof points to land
- **Onboarding curriculum** for new hires covering product, buyer, competitive context, and demo skills

### Phase 3: Deliver

Content that lives in a folder no one opens is not enablement. Delivery matters as much as creation:

- Present new assets in a live sales meeting, not just a Slack link
- Load materials into your content platform (Highspot, Seismic, Showpad) with logical tagging
- Record a short walkthrough video so reps can revisit it later
- Run a role-play session for objection-handling guides, reading is not practicing

### Phase 4: Measure

Close the loop or you are guessing:

- Track content usage rate in your platform 30 days after launch
- Compare win rates in deals where reps used the asset vs. deals where they did not
- Survey reps on content usefulness every quarter
- Review ramp time for each new-hire cohort after onboarding updates

## Key Deliverables Explained

### Battlecards

A battlecard is a one-page cheat sheet on a single competitor. It answers three questions a rep needs answered before a competitive deal:

1. What does the competitor claim their strengths are?
2. Why are those claims weak, exaggerated, or not relevant to our buyers?
3. What should the rep say instead?

Good battlecards are short (one page), blunt (no corporate hedging), and updated on a quarterly cadence or immediately after a competitor makes a significant move. In 2025, battlecards also need to be AI-ready, structured so they can be surfaced by internal enablement bots during a live call, not just read beforehand.

### Sales Decks

The PMM team owns the master sales deck. It follows a consistent arc: here is the buyer's problem, here is how we solve it, here is proof it works, here is the next step. Reps will customize slides for specific accounts, your job is to make the core version so clear and well-structured that they want to start from it rather than build their own from scratch.

### Objection-Handling Guides

A curated list of the 10-15 objections reps encounter most, with recommended responses written in conversational language. These are built from call recordings and rep interviews, not guesswork from a conference room. Update them whenever a new objection pattern emerges in the field.

### Onboarding Curriculum

A structured program for new sales hires that covers: what the product does, who buys it, why they buy it, how it compares to alternatives, and how to run a compelling demo. The best onboarding programs include live role-plays with feedback, not just slide decks and self-paced videos.

<Callout type="tip">
**Build once, reuse many times.** The competitive research you do for a battlecard is the same research that feeds your objection-handling guide. The competitive section of the sales deck is a condensed version of the battlecard. Structure your content modularly so individual pieces plug into multiple formats, this cuts production time and keeps messaging consistent across every touchpoint.
</Callout>

## Real Company Examples

### HubSpot: Enabling a Massive Indirect Channel

HubSpot runs one of the largest partner and reseller networks in B2B SaaS. Their sales enablement challenge is not just training full-time employees, it is keeping thousands of external partners current on positioning, competitive differentiation, and new feature messaging. HubSpot's solution is a tiered certification program through HubSpot Academy that gates access to deal registration and co-marketing support based on completion of enablement modules. The result: partners who complete the full curriculum close deals at a higher rate and generate larger average contract values than uncertified partners. The PMM team maintains a dedicated set of partner battlecards separate from the internal ones, updated with each major product release.

<Callout type="example">
**HubSpot Partner Enablement: Certification as a Forcing Function**

HubSpot ties partner deal registration (the financial benefit partners get for finding new customers) directly to completion of their enablement certification tracks. This creates a strong incentive for partners to actually use the content rather than skip it. The PMM team owns the competitive modules and updates them quarterly. The program demonstrates a key principle: enablement works best when there is a structural reason for reps or partners to engage with the content, not just a hope that they will.
</Callout>

### Gong: Using Its Own Product to Improve Enablement

Gong (the revenue intelligence platform) has publicly documented using its own call recording and analysis product to identify the specific moments in sales calls where deals break down. Their PMM team analyzed thousands of recorded calls to find that deals with certain competitors most often stalled at the "pricing and ROI" stage of the conversation. They rebuilt their competitive battlecards and objection-handling guide specifically around that moment, with concrete ROI calculation frameworks reps could use live on a call. Win rates against those competitors improved measurably within two quarters of the update.

<Callout type="example">
**Gong: Data-Driven Battlecard Rebuilds**

Rather than rebuilding battlecards on a fixed quarterly schedule, Gong's team triggered updates based on signal from call data: when a specific objection or competitor mention showed up at above-baseline frequency in losing deals, that became the next battlecard update priority. This shifts enablement from calendar-driven to signal-driven, a model that 2025's AI-powered GTM tools are making accessible to teams of any size, not just companies that build their own call intelligence platform.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

**1. Building content based on assumptions instead of evidence.** The most common failure mode is a PMM team that decides what reps need based on product knowledge and internal strategy, without ever listening to actual sales calls or talking to reps who are losing deals. The fix is mandatory discovery before any creation phase.

**2. Creating assets that are too long to use in the field.** A 12-page competitive analysis document is a research artifact, not a battlecard. A 60-slide master deck is an archive, not a selling tool. If a rep cannot scan the key point in 30 seconds during a call, the asset is not field-ready. Every deliverable should have a clear "moment of use" in mind, and be built for that moment.

**3. Delivering via Slack message and calling it done.** Dropping a PDF link in a channel is not enablement. Without a live walkthrough, a role-play session, and a clear reason for reps to use the material, adoption will be low. The [Highspot State of Sales Enablement 2025](https://www.highspot.com/state-of-sales-enablement-2025/) found that 55% of organizations still struggle to drive GTM initiatives effectively, largely because delivery and adoption are weaker than content creation.

**4. Not measuring usage or outcomes.** If you do not know whether reps used the battlecard or whether it correlated with wins, you cannot improve the program or justify the investment. Set up usage tracking in your content platform before launch, not after.

**5. Treating enablement as a one-time project.** A sales deck built for last year's product positioning is actively misleading if the product has changed. Battlecards go stale within weeks of a competitor making a move. Enablement requires a maintenance cadence, at minimum, a review at every major product launch and every significant competitive development.

## Key Takeaways

- Sales enablement is the system that converts product marketing knowledge into deal wins, it is not a content library, it is a program
- Organizations with formal enablement achieve 49% win rates vs. 42.5% for those without, a gap that compounds significantly at scale
- The four phases are discover, create, deliver, and measure, skipping discovery or measurement breaks the whole system
- Battlecards, sales decks, and objection-handling guides are the core assets; all three should be short, scannable, and tied to specific moments in the sales process
- Delivery is as important as creation: live training and role-play drive adoption in ways that Slack links never will
- In 2025, AI-ready content design is becoming a requirement, battlecards and guides need to be structured so they can be surfaced by AI tools mid-call, not just read in advance
