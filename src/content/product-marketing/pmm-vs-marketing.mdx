Product marketing is one of the most misunderstood roles in any company. Ask ten people what a Product Marketing Manager does and you will get ten different answers. Is it marketing? Is it product? Is it sales? The honest answer: it sits at the intersection of all three, and that is precisely what makes it valuable.

A 2025 survey by Segment8 found that 41% of companies now describe themselves as 'product first' in go-to-market strategy, up 31% year-over-year. Product Marketing Managers are the people making that possible ([Segment8 State of Product Marketing 2025](https://blog.segment8.com/posts/state-of-product-marketing-2025-pmm-not-going-anywhere/)).

## Quick Summary

- Product marketing translates product capabilities into customer value and market positioning.
- PMMs own positioning, messaging, launch strategy, and sales enablement, not demand generation or paid ads.
- The role sits between Product, Marketing, and Sales, acting as the connective tissue.
- In 2025, 91% of PMMs own positioning and 79% own sales enablement, up from 64% the prior year.
- Median US PMM salary is $141,000 (Glassdoor 2025), outpacing general marketing managers by roughly $67,000.

## What General Marketing Does

General marketing covers the full spectrum of creating awareness and generating demand. This includes:

- Paid advertising (search, social, display)
- SEO and content marketing
- Email campaigns and nurture sequences
- Brand and creative
- Events and sponsorships
- Marketing operations and analytics

The goal of general marketing is to fill the top of the funnel: get the right people to know your product exists and want to learn more.

## What Product Marketing Does

Product marketing starts where general marketing often stops. Once someone is interested, PMM takes over:

- **Positioning:** Why does this product exist and why does it win?
- **Messaging:** How do we talk about it for different audiences and channels?
- **Launch strategy:** How do we bring a new feature or product to market?
- **Sales enablement:** What do reps need to close deals? Battlecards, decks, objection handling guides.
- **Competitive intelligence:** Where do we win, where do we lose, and why?
- **Customer research:** What jobs are customers hiring this product to do?
- **Onboarding:** In 2025, 37.5% of PMMs own customer onboarding, up from just 19% the prior year ([Segment8](https://blog.segment8.com/posts/state-of-product-marketing-2025-pmm-not-going-anywhere/)).

<Callout type="info">
A useful shorthand: General marketing brings people to the door. Product marketing makes sure the door is labeled correctly, the right people show up, and they understand what is inside.
</Callout>

## How It Works: The PMM Playbook

Product marketing runs on a repeatable five-step framework for any product or feature:

**1. Research the market and customer**
Before writing a single word of messaging, PMMs run customer interviews, analyze win/loss data, and map the competitive landscape. The output is a clear picture of who buys, why they buy, and what alternatives they are choosing between.

**2. Define positioning**
Positioning is the internal foundation, not the external tagline. It answers: for whom, against what alternatives, does this product deliver what unique benefit, and why should they believe us? Positioning docs are internal artifacts that guide everything else.

**3. Craft messaging**
Messaging translates positioning into words for specific audiences and channels. The same product may need a technical message for developers and a business-outcome message for CFOs. PMMs create the message hierarchy and hand it off to content, demand gen, and sales.

**4. Execute the launch**
PMMs own the go-to-market plan: which segments to target first, which channels to use, what the launch timeline looks like, and how success is measured. They coordinate across Product, Design, Engineering, Sales, and Customer Success.

**5. Enable the sales team**
After launch, PMMs build battlecards, talk tracks, demo guides, and one-pagers. Sales enablement jumped from 64% to 79% PMM ownership in a single year, reflecting how critical this handoff has become ([Segment8 2025](https://blog.segment8.com/posts/state-of-product-marketing-2025-pmm-not-going-anywhere/)).

Product marketing sits at the center of three teams:

This is not a reporting structure. It is a collaboration model. PMMs do not manage product managers or sales reps. They influence them through research, clarity, and credibility.

## Real Examples

<Callout type="example">
**Slack repositioning (2019-2020)**
When Slack needed to grow beyond tech companies into enterprises, it was not a product change. It was a positioning change. The team shifted messaging from 'messaging app for teams' to 'channel-based messaging platform that replaces email.' Product Marketing led that shift, rewriting positioning docs, sales decks, and the homepage. The result: Slack went from a startup tool to a Microsoft Teams competitor taken seriously by Fortune 500 procurement teams.
</Callout>

<Callout type="example">
**Stripe Terminal launch**
Stripe's card-present product for in-person payments was a technical leap but a messaging challenge. Their PMM team had to explain why an online payments company belonged in physical retail. They built separate messaging tracks for developers (SDK depth, docs) and business owners (reliability, unified reporting). Sales got an entirely separate battlecard deck focused on legacy POS vendor weaknesses. Each audience got a different door into the same product.
</Callout>

## PMM vs Marketing: Side-by-Side

| Dimension | General Marketing | Product Marketing |
|---|---|---|
| Primary goal | Awareness and demand | Positioning and conversion |
| Owns | Campaigns, channels, brand | Messaging, launches, enablement |
| Works with | Creative, agencies, paid teams | Product, Sales, CS |
| Measures | Leads, impressions, CAC | Win rate, attach rate, deal velocity |
| Output | Ads, content, emails | Positioning docs, battlecards, launch plans |
| Audience | Broad market | Specific buyer segments |

## Common Mistakes

**1. Writing messaging before doing research**
Skipping customer interviews and jumping straight to copy is the single most common PMM mistake. Messaging written without research sounds like a feature list, not a customer benefit.

**2. Confusing positioning with taglines**
Positioning is an internal strategic document. A tagline is one output of positioning. PMMs who conflate the two end up with catchy slogans built on no foundation.

**3. Building launch plans without sales input**
A launch plan that Sales did not help shape is one Sales will not follow. PMMs who exclude reps from planning find their battlecards ignored.

**4. Owning messaging but not measuring it**
If PMM cannot connect messaging changes to win rate or deal velocity, the function looks decorative. Instrument the funnel before you rewrite the deck.

**5. Treating all competitors the same**
Competitive strategy requires tiering: primary threats get full battlecards, secondary threats get two-paragraph briefs, emerging threats get monitoring. Treating every competitor as equally important wastes PMM capacity.

## Why the Role Is Growing

The 2025 Glassdoor data places median US PMM compensation at $141,000 total cash, roughly $67,000 above general marketing manager median ([Salary.com Product Marketing Manager Salary Report](https://www.salary.com/research/salary/alternate/product-marketing-manager-salary)). That gap reflects market demand: as software companies proliferate and differentiation becomes harder, the ability to clearly articulate why a product wins matters more than ever.

The Segment8 survey found PMMs are now taking on scope that was previously owned by other teams: customer onboarding, pricing strategy, and post-sale expansion messaging. The role is expanding because the go-to-market motion has gotten more complex, not simpler.

<Callout type="tip">
If you are considering a move into product marketing, start by running a positioning exercise on a product you use. Who is it for? Who is it not for? What alternatives exist? Why does it win? Answering those four questions well is the core of the job.
</Callout>
